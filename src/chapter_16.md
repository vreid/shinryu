# Chapter 16: Long Distance

---

## I

The last day of the first term fell on a Friday in mid-July, and it arrived with
the particular, compound melancholy of an ending that was also a beginning—the
final homeroom, the final cleaning duty, the final combined lunch at the desk
formation that Kenji had built and maintained and defended with the territorial
devotion of someone who understood that furniture arrangement was a love
language.

The morning was hot. Tokyo in July operated at a temperature that felt less like
weather and more like a personal grievance—the air thick, the humidity visible
as a haze that blurred the middle distance, the concrete radiating stored heat
with the passive aggression of a material that had been absorbing sunlight since
April and had opinions about it. The school's air conditioning, which had been
installed during a renovation in 2003 and had not been meaningfully upgraded
since, performed its duties with the exhausted competence of a system that was
doing its best and wished everyone would stop asking for more.

Isabelle arrived at 8:09. She walked through the gate—the same gate, the same
stone pillars, the same path now shaded by a canopy of dense summer green
instead of cherry blossoms. The courtyard was loud with the particular energy of
a last day: students carrying bags stuffed with personal items cleared from
desks, teachers distributing report cards and summer assignment packets, the
ambient noise pitched higher and faster than a normal Friday, as if the school
itself was accelerating toward release.

She walked through the gate alone. Caspar was not waiting at the pillar—he had
morning duties, something involving the photography club's storage inventory
that Fujita had assigned with the manic thoroughness of a president who wanted
every negative sleeve and chemical bottle accounted for before the break.

She changed her shoes. She climbed the stairs. She entered 1-3.

Yuki was at her desk. She was not reading—she was sitting with her hands folded,
looking at the empty classroom with an expression that Isabelle had never seen
on her: soft. Not analytical. Not observational. Simply soft, the face of
someone who was looking at a space she had occupied for four months and was
registering, for the first time, that it had meant something.

"Last day," Isabelle said, sitting down.

"Last day of the term," Yuki corrected. "Not the last day."

"Important distinction."

"All distinctions are important. That's what makes them distinctions."

Isabelle smiled. She would miss this—the daily calibration of Yuki's precision,
the way every conversation with her was a small exercise in accuracy, the quiet
reassurance of a person who believed that words should mean exactly what they
said and nothing more.

"Yuki."

"Mm."

"Thank you. For the first term."

Yuki looked at her. The soft expression held—didn't harden, didn't retreat
behind the analytical mask. It stayed. "For what specifically?"

"For deciding I was real."

The ghost-smile. Yuki's rarest expression, deployed now with a frequency that
would have been unthinkable in April—a softening of the mouth that lasted a full
second, accompanied by a fractional crinkling of the eyes that suggested the
smile went deeper than the surface.

"You made it easy," Yuki said.

The combined lunch was the largest it had ever been.

Kenji had expanded the desk formation to accommodate not just the core eight but
three additional guests: Nakamura Aoi from 1-2 (the manga artist, who had been
orbiting the group's edge for weeks and who had, during the photography
exhibition, sold four prints of her cat-themed illustrations and donated the
proceeds to the athletics fund without being asked); Fujita-senpai (who had been
invited by Caspar as a gesture of gratitude and who arrived carrying his camera
and the slightly dazed expression of a third-year who had never been invited to
a first-year lunch and who was not entirely sure of the protocol); and,
unexpectedly, Morimoto-sensei, who had been passing the 1-2 classroom during the
setup, had seen the desk formation, had asked what was happening, and had been
told by Kenji, with the confidence of a diplomat extending a state invitation,
that he was "welcome to join the final session of the table."

Morimoto-sensei sat at the edge. He ate his own lunch—a convenience store bento,
modest, the lunch of a teacher on a public school salary—and he watched the
group with the expression of someone who had been teaching for twenty-five years
and who still, occasionally, witnessed something in a classroom that reminded
him why he had started.

Food was shared. The tradition held—pieces of galbi and dakgalbi and hamburg
steak and salmon passed across the desks on chopsticks and bento lids, the
physical ritual of exchange that had become the group's founding act. Isabelle
had brought a final pastry: a Paris-Brest, the ring-shaped choux pastry filled
with praline mousseline cream, cut into eight portions and presented on
parchment paper. The pastry was a logistical triumph—the choux had been baked
that morning at 5 AM, filled at 6, refrigerated until 7, transported to school
in an insulated bag that Auntie Choi had packed with the focused intensity of
someone handling biomedical samples.

Kenji ate his portion and said nothing for four seconds. This was, the group
understood, the highest possible rating.

"Summer plans," Kenji announced, after the silence had been given its due.
"Final check. Go around the table."

The plans were reviewed. Kenji: Tokyo, comedy workshop, basketball. Sora:
Nagano, then Tokyo. Daiki: coding camp, then home. Yuki: personal project,
classified. Hina: home, reading, possibly a part-time job at a bookstore near
her apartment. Ren: training, regional invitational, more training. Aoi: manga
workshop in Ikebukuro. Fujita: university entrance exam preparation, which he
described with the grim resignation of a soldier reporting for a campaign he
knew would be long.

"Ji-won?" Kenji said.

"Seoul. Two weeks. Then back to Tokyo."

"Ryu?"

"Munich. One week. Then—" The pause. The fractional hesitation. "—Seoul. Maybe
two weeks."

The table absorbed this. Caspar and Isabelle, in Seoul, at the same time. The
information landed with the quiet weight of a fact that everyone had already
intuited but that no one had confirmed until now.

"Seoul together," Kenji said. He said it without emphasis, without innuendo—just
the fact, plainly stated, the way you stated weather.

"Seoul together," Caspar confirmed.

"Cool." Kenji nodded. "Bring me back something. I don't care what. A snack. A
keychain. Something that says 'my friend thought of me while he was in Korea
with his girlfriend.'"

"I'll find something."

"Something good, Ryu. Not airport-gift-shop good. _Kenji-deserves-it_ good."

"Noted."

Sora leaned across the table. "Ji-won, if you're in Seoul, and VESPER is based
in Seoul, and you have—connections—" She paused. The word _connections_ was
deployed carefully, the way a person deployed a word whose full implications
they were aware of but had chosen not to explore. "—could you maybe get me a
signed album? I'll pay. I'll pay double. I'll pay whatever it costs."

Isabelle looked at Sora. The girl who had introduced herself on the first day by
declaring Seo Yuna her bias. The girl who had played VESPER fancams during lunch
and mapped formation changes frame by frame and stood in a fan queue for three
hours and cried when Yuna touched her palm. The girl who loved VESPER with a
purity and a devotion that Isabelle recognized because she had seen it in
Caspar—the same unironic, unguarded, full-hearted love of something beautiful.

"I'll see what I can do," Isabelle said.

Sora's face did a complicated thing. "Really?"

"No promises. But I'll try."

"Ji-won. If you get me a signed album, I will name my firstborn child after
you."

"Please don't."

"Ji-won Yamamoto. It has a ring to it."

"It really doesn't."

The table laughed. Morimoto-sensei, at the edge, smiled into his convenience
store bento. The sound of the laugh—eight voices plus a teacher plus a manga
artist plus a club president, layered and overlapping and genuine—filled the
classroom and spilled into the hallway and mixed with the ambient noise of a
school on its last day, and for a moment the room held everything: the food and
the laughter and the plans and the promises and the specific, unrepeatable fact
of eleven people who had found each other in a public high school in Tokyo and
who were about to scatter into the summer and who trusted, without needing to
say it, that they would come back.

## II

The afternoon was for goodbyes.

Not dramatic goodbyes—Japanese schools did not do dramatic end-of-term
farewells. The process was administrative: report cards distributed, summer
assignments explained, desks cleaned, personal items removed. The building was
emptied systematically, floor by floor, the students flowing out through the
shoe lockers and the gate in a reverse tide that left the school, by 3 PM,
nearly silent.

Isabelle stood at the gate. Caspar stood beside her. Their bags were full—school
supplies, personal items, the accumulated material of four months. Isabelle's
bag also contained the now-empty Paris-Brest container, an insulated bag, and a
gel pack.

The gate was different in summer. The stone pillars were the same, the metal
structure was the same, but the light was different—harsh, white, the July sun
pressing down with a weight that was almost physical. The green canopy overhead
provided partial shade, the leaves dense and motionless in the still air. Beyond
the gate, the street shimmered.

"이상하다," Isabelle said. _This feels strange._

"뭐가?" _What does?_

"떠나는 게. 여기서 떠나는 거. 4개월 전에 여기로 들어왔을 때, 아무것도 몰랐잖아.
너가 여기 있는지도 몰랐고. 이 사람들도 몰랐고. 아무것도." _Leaving. Leaving this
place. When I walked in four months ago, I didn't know anything. I didn't know
you were here. I didn't know these people. Nothing._

"지금은?" _And now?_

She looked at the gate. The stone pillars. The path. The courtyard where cherry
blossoms had fallen on the first day and where, now, the trees held nothing but
green.

"지금은 돌아오고 싶어." _Now I want to come back._

He took her hand. They walked through the gate together—the same gate, the same
direction, the same two people who had walked through it arm in arm on a Monday
morning in May and who were now walking through it in July, holding hands, their
bags heavy with a term's worth of work and their chests heavy with a different
kind of weight: the weight of caring about a place and the people in it, the
weight of roots that had grown without permission, silently, while they were
busy being careful.

They walked to the corner where their routes diverged. They stopped.

"내일 뮌헨이지?" _Munich tomorrow?_

"응. 아침 비행기." _Yeah. Morning flight._

"얼마 동안?" _How long?_

"일주일. 엄마 만나고, 삼촌 만나고, 호텔 동료들도 만나려고." _A week. Seeing my
mom, my uncle, and some colleagues from the hotel._

"호텔 동료들?" _Hotel colleagues?_

"매기. 바리스타. 게르하르트. 체력 코치. 그리고 린드너 부인. 하우스키핑 감독.
내가 빗자루 잡는 법 가르쳐준 사람." _Maggie. The barista. Gerhard. The fitness
coach. And Frau Lindner. The housekeeping supervisor. The one who taught me how
to hold a broom._

Isabelle smiled. The image assembled itself: Caspar, returning to the Alpenhof
not as a porter but as a visitor, walking into the hotel where he had been
invisible for a year and being seen—by the people who had known him when he was
nobody's son, when he was just the tall quiet one with the camera, when he was
learning to sweep and to carry and to see.

"그 사람들이 기뻐할 거야." _They'll be happy to see you._

"모르겠어. 린드너 부인은 빗자루 잡는 자세 다시 교정할걸." _I don't know. Frau
Lindner will probably correct my broom posture again._

She laughed. She stepped forward and kissed him—a brief, warm contact, the taste
of praline mousseline cream still faintly present on his lips from the
Paris-Brest, the sensation of his mouth against hers now familiar enough to be a
comfort rather than a revelation but still, always, accompanied by the small
internal tremor that meant this mattered, that this was real, that the body
never fully acclimated to the proximity of the person it loved most.

"뮌헨에서 사진 많이 찍어." _Take lots of photos in Munich._

"응." _Yeah._

"그리고 서울 일정 확정되면 알려줘." _And let me know when the Seoul dates are
confirmed._

"확정됐어." _They're confirmed._

She pulled back. "확정됐어?" _They're confirmed?_

"어제. 엄마가 서울 출장 일정 잡았어. 발트슈타인 보타니컬스 서울 오피스 미팅.
나도 같이 가기로 했어. 7월 말부터 8월 중순까지." _Yesterday. My mom scheduled a
Seoul business trip. Waldstein Botanicals Seoul office meeting. I'm going with
her. End of July through mid-August._

"캐스퍼." _Caspar._

"응." _Yeah._

"2주 반이야." _That's two and a half weeks._

"응." _Yeah._

"서울에서 2주 반." _Two and a half weeks in Seoul._

"응." _Yeah._

"나랑 같은 도시에서 2주 반." _Two and a half weeks in the same city as me._

"이자벨." _Isabelle._

"응." _Yeah._

"내가 서울 간다고 했을 때 이럴 줄 알았어." _I knew you'd react like this when I
told you about Seoul._

"어떻게 반응하는데?" _How am I reacting?_

"네 눈이 지금 세 배로 커져 있어." _Your eyes are three times bigger right now._

She hit his shoulder. Lightly—the dancer's controlled contact, force without
damage. He caught her hand. He held it against his chest—the same position her
hand had occupied in the darkroom, over his heart, the gesture that had become
their shorthand for _this is real, feel it, believe it._

"서울에서 만나자," he said. _Let's meet in Seoul._

"당연히 만나지," she said. _Obviously we're meeting in Seoul._

"서울에서 할 거 있어?" _Is there anything you want to do in Seoul?_

She thought about this. She thought about Seoul—the city where she had trained,
the city where she had learned to dance and to endure and to carry the weight of
other people's expectations without losing herself. She thought about the
practice room and the break room and the convenience store on the corner where
she had bought instant ramyeon at 2 AM and eaten it standing up because all the
chairs were occupied by exhausted trainees. She thought about the han river and
the Bukchon hanok village and the Gwangjang market where Haeun had taken her to
eat bindaetteok and where Isabelle had eaten four because the batter was
perfect—light, crisp, the mung bean flavor clean and bright—and Haeun had
photographed her eating the fourth one and sent the photo to the group chat with
the caption _"This girl is 50% European and 100% Korean stomach."_

"연습실에 데려가고 싶어," she said. _I want to take you to the practice room._

"연습실." _The practice room._

"신 엔터테인먼트 연습실. VESPER가 연습하는 곳. 내가 1년 동안 있었던 곳." _The
Shin Entertainment practice room. Where VESPER practices. Where I spent a year._

Caspar looked at her. The afternoon sun was behind her—the same backlighting as
the first day, the same halo effect on her hair, the light doing the thing it
always did to her, which was to make her look like she existed in a slightly
higher resolution than the rest of the world.

"그리고 멤버들 만나게 해주고 싶어," she said. _And I want you to meet the
members._

"직접?" _In person?_

"직접." _In person._

"이자벨." _Isabelle._

"응." _Yeah._

"유나한테 직접 만나면 진짜 기절할 수도 있어." _If I meet Yuna in person, I might
actually faint._

"기절하면 하은 언니가 간호해줄 거야. 예전에 간호조무사 자격증 따려고 공부한 적
있거든." _If you faint, Haeun-unnie will take care of you. She once studied for
a nursing assistant certification._

"진짜?" _Really?_

"연습생 때. 부업으로. 포기했지만 기본 응급처치는 아직 할 줄 알아." _During her
trainee days. As a side job. She gave it up, but she still knows basic first
aid._

"너네 언니들 진짜 다양하다." _Your sisters are really something._

"알아." _I know._

They stood at the corner. The sun pressed down. The city hummed. Summer
stretched ahead of them—Munich and Seoul and the practice room and the hotel and
the families they came from and the family they had chosen, all of it waiting,
all of it real, all of it about to happen.

"일주일 동안 연락 안 되면—" she started. _If we can't reach each other for a
week—_

"연락 안 될 리가 없어." _There's no way we won't be in touch._

"비행기에서는 안 되잖아." _On the plane, we can't._

"비행기 빼고." _Except on the plane._

"그리고 뮌헨 시차." _And the Munich time difference._

"7시간. 내가 아침에 일어나면 네가 오후고, 내가 밤이면 너는 새벽이야. 우리 잘 자
인사 시간이 좀 복잡해질 수 있어." _Seven hours. When I wake up in the morning,
it's your afternoon. When it's my night, it's your dawn. Our good-night timing
might get complicated._

"잘 자 인사는 무조건 해야 돼." _The good-night must happen no matter what._

"당연하지." _Obviously._

"약속해." _Promise._

"약속해." _I promise._

She looked at him. He looked at her. The corner. The diverging routes. The last
time they would stand here for six weeks—six weeks of different cities and
different time zones and the particular, specific test of a relationship moving
from proximity to distance, from the daily architecture of shared lunches and
linked arms and hallway glances to the leaner, more demanding architecture of
phone calls and text messages and the faith that what they had built could
survive the absence of the physical.

"잘 자, 캐스퍼." _Good night, Caspar._

It was 3:30 in the afternoon. The sun was blazing. The farewell was absurd in
its timing and perfect in its meaning—the ritual deployed not at its scheduled
hour but at the moment of parting, because the moment of parting was always the
moment that required it most.

"잘 자, 이자벨." _Good night, Isabelle._

She let go of his hand. She turned. She walked toward Aoyama. She did not look
back—not because she didn't want to but because looking back would mean
acknowledging the distance, and the distance didn't deserve that power, not yet,
not while the warmth of his hand was still in hers and the taste of the kiss was
still on her lips and the summer was long and Seoul was waiting and they had
promised.

He watched her go. She could feel his gaze—the photographer's gaze, the specific
quality of attention that she had learned to recognize as clearly as a touch—on
her back, on her hair, on the line of her spine as she walked away.

She raised her hand. Not a wave. A reach—the dancer's extension, the fingers
stretching toward the sky, the gesture that had migrated from her body to
Jiwoo's and from Jiwoo's to the stages of the world.

She reached. She held it. She dropped her hand and kept walking.

Behind her, on the corner, the faint mechanical whisper of a shutter.

_Click._

## III

Munich.

The flight was eleven hours. Caspar sat in business class—his mother's booking,
non-negotiable, the seat wide and the legroom sufficient for his six-one
frame—and watched the world pass beneath him through the oval window: the
Pacific, the Siberian landmass, the Urals, the green quilted farmland of Central
Europe emerging from the cloud cover like a painting being unveiled.

He did not sleep. He developed, in his mind, the roll of film that was sitting
in its canister in his camera bag in the overhead compartment—the roll that
contained the Komazawa meet, the evening walk, the photograph of them together
(Frame 34, shot blind, the composition unknown), the shot of Isabelle walking
away (Frame 35), and one remaining frame, unused, held in reserve.

He thought about Frame 34. He had no idea what it looked like. The camera had
been at arm's length, the rangefinder useless, the composition left to instinct
and proximity. The image might be perfectly framed—two faces, close together,
the evening light warm on their skin, their smiles synchronized. Or it might be
a disaster—heads cropped, focus wrong, the image a blur of light and movement
that documented intention rather than execution.

Either way, it was the only photograph of them together. In four months of
Leicas and darkrooms and exhibition prints, he had never turned the camera on
both of them simultaneously. He had photographed her—Daikanyama, Yanaka, the
contact sheets, the secret frames. He had photographed the world around them—the
school, the city, the bench, the river. But the two of them, together, in a
single frame—that was new. That was Frame 34. And he wouldn't know what it
looked like until he was back in Tokyo, in the darkroom, watching it emerge.

The plane landed at Munich International at 6:14 AM local time. The immigration
line was short—EU passport, German citizen, the border officer glancing at his
face and his passport photo and waving him through with the bored efficiency of
a system processing its own.

His mother was waiting in arrivals.

Helena Waldstein stood at the barrier in a navy wool coat—even in July, Munich
mornings were cool enough for layers—her silver-blonde hair pulled back, her
posture suggesting that she had been standing for exactly as long as she
intended and not a second longer. She was holding a coffee in one hand and her
phone in the other, and she was, even at 6:30 AM in an airport arrivals hall,
the most composed person in the room.

She saw him. Her face did something that Caspar had seen perhaps a dozen times
in his life—a fracture in the composure, a break in the seamless surface, the
brief and total vulnerability of a mother seeing her child after an absence. It
lasted less than a second. Then it was repaired, smoothed, replaced by the warm,
controlled smile that was Helena Waldstein's public expression of love.

"Schatz," she said.

He walked to her. She put her arms around him—a brief, firm embrace, the hug of
a woman who did not do casual physical contact and whose every touch was
therefore weighted with the full force of its rarity. He was taller than her
now. This was new—or rather, it was something she hadn't experienced in person
since before the hotel, since before the year that had added five centimeters
and ten kilograms and a set of experiences that she was still, Caspar suspected,
processing.

She pulled back. She held him at arm's length. She looked at him—the full Helena
Waldstein assessment, the eyes that evaluated products and people with the same
unsparing attention.

"Du siehst gut aus," she said. _You look good._

"Danke, Mama."

"Nein, ich meine—" She paused. She was searching for words, which was unusual
for a woman who deployed language with the precision of a surgical instrument.
"Du siehst aus, als ob du angekommen bist." _You look like you've arrived._

He understood. She didn't mean arrived at the airport. She meant arrived at
himself—arrived at a version of himself that was settled, that was present, that
was no longer in transit between who he had been and who he was becoming.

"Bin ich," he said. _I have._

She nodded. The nod contained multitudes—the relief of a mother, the assessment
of a CEO, the particular satisfaction of a woman who had sent her son into the
world and was receiving him back changed in exactly the ways she had hoped.

"Komm," she said. _Come._ "Das Auto wartet." _The car is waiting._

The car was a Mercedes S-Class, black, debadged, driven by a man named Georg who
had been Helena's driver in Munich for twelve years and who greeted Caspar with
the same muted warmth he always had—a nod, a "Willkommen zurück, junger Herr,"
and the smooth, silent acceleration away from the curb.

Munich passed outside the tinted windows. The Bavarian capital in July was green
and gold—the Isar River glinting in the early light, the church spires rising
above the treeline, the Englischer Garten a vast dark mass of foliage that
occupied the city's center like a forest that had refused to leave. The streets
were clean, wide, orderly—the urban planning of a city that valued structure and
beauty in equal measure and that expressed both through the particular German
commitment to doing things properly.

Caspar watched the city he had grown up in. It looked the same. The buildings,
the streets, the bakeries, the churches—all unchanged, all precisely where he
had left them. But he was different. He was seeing Munich the way he now saw
everything—through the particular filter of a year in a hotel basement and four
months in a Tokyo school and the accumulated evidence of a life that had
expanded to include people and places and a girl whose photographs hung on his
darkroom wall and whose hand he could still feel in his, across eleven hours and
seven time zones and the entire vast blue distance of the sky.
