# Chapter 2: Parallel Lines

---

## I

Class 1-2's homeroom was on the opposite side of the hallway from 1-3, which
meant that Caspar could see Isabelle's classroom door from his seat if he leaned
back two degrees and angled his line of sight past the head of the boy sitting
diagonally in front of him.

He did not lean back two degrees.

He sat straight, hands flat on the desk surface, eyes forward, and listened to
his homeroom teacher—a thin man in his fifties named Morimoto-sensei, who had
the wiry energy of a retired distance runner and a habit of clicking his pen
against his thumbnail while he spoke—explain the class schedule with the
meticulous enthusiasm of someone who genuinely loved administrative structure.

"Cleaning duties rotate weekly. Your group is assigned a zone—classroom,
hallway, or bathroom. The rotation chart is posted by the door. If you have
questions about the rotation, please ask your cleaning group leader, not me. I
have explained the rotation. It is on the chart."

Caspar studied the chart. It was hand-drawn on a laminated sheet of A3 paper,
color-coded by zone, with names written in neat marker. His group was assigned
to the hallway for the first week. His cleaning group leader was a boy named
Watanabe Kenji, whose name was written in slightly larger characters than
everyone else's, suggesting either self-importance or bad spatial planning.

The self-introductions began.

Caspar listened to each one the way he had once listened to guest requests at
the hotel—with full attention, filing names and details into a mental index that
he could retrieve later without effort. This was a skill he hadn't known he was
building during the gap year, but it had become automatic: the head concierge at
the Alpenhof had believed that remembering a guest's name after a single
introduction was not a talent but a professional obligation, and forgetting was
not an error but an insult. After twelve months, Caspar could meet thirty people
in a single evening and recall every name, every face, every small detail they'd
offered—the wife who preferred still water, the businessman who wanted his
newspaper ironed, the child who was allergic to strawberries.

Thirty-seven high school students were easy.

Front row, left side: Kobayashi Mei, from Meguro, liked swimming. Suzuki Haruto,
from Shibuya, liked soccer and "hanging out." Nakamura Aoi, from Setagaya, liked
drawing manga—she said this quietly, almost apologetically, and the tips of her
ears turned pink. Watanabe Kenji, from Shibuya—

Watanabe Kenji stood up like a spring releasing.

He was average height, solidly built, with a round face and a mouth that seemed
permanently set to its widest aperture. His hair was buzzed short on the sides
and longer on top, styled with a gel that was working very hard. He had the
posture of someone who had never, in his entire life, entered a room without the
room knowing about it.

"Watanabe Kenji!" He said his own name the way a boxer announced a title. "From
Shibuya! I like comedy, basketball, and eating! My special skill is that I can
eat four bowls of ramen in one sitting! My record is at Jirō in Mita!" He looked
around the room, grinning, waiting for impact. "Also, I did stand-up comedy at
our middle school culture festival and I made a teacher laugh so hard she
spilled her tea!"

The class responded with the precise amount of laughter that signaled _we
acknowledge your energy and are deciding how we feel about it._ Kenji sat down,
satisfied.

Behind Kenji, a boy with a lean, athletic frame and close-cropped black hair
stood up with an efficiency that suggested he viewed standing as a transitional
state to be minimized.

"Ito Ren. Setagaya. I run track—800 meters and 1500 meters. I'm aiming for the
prefectural meet this year."

He sat down. The introduction had lasted approximately four seconds. Caspar
appreciated this.

Three seats to the right of Ren, near the window: a boy with glasses and a
slightly hunched posture, his desk already organized with a pencil case, a
mechanical pencil, an eraser, and a notebook aligned with the kind of precision
that suggested a personal grid system.

"Kuroda Daiki. Setagaya. I'm interested in programming and game development. I'm
currently building a dungeon crawler in Unity." A pause. He adjusted his
glasses. "It's turn-based. If anyone wants to playtest, I can send the APK."

Silence. Then, from across the room, Kenji's voice: "Is there a character based
on me?"

"No."

"Can there be?"

"I'll think about it."

A small ripple of laughter. Daiki sat down, the faintest trace of a smile
pulling at the corner of his mouth.

And then it was Caspar's turn.

He stood. The chair pushed back silently across the floor. He rose to his full
height—six-one, taller than everyone in the room by a comfortable margin, taller
than Morimoto-sensei by at least ten centimeters—and the room did what rooms
always did when he stood up in them.

It went quiet.

Not the polite, anticipatory quiet of listening. A different quiet—the kind that
happened when a visual input didn't match the expected pattern. Thirty-seven
brains processing the tall foreign boy with the cheekbones and the pale blue
eyes in a public school uniform, trying to reconcile the image with the context,
failing, and defaulting to silence.

"Ryu Tae-sung," he said. His Japanese was clean, measured, and deliberately
unremarkable—the same language filter Isabelle used, calibrated to suggest
competence without fluency, effort without ease. "I'm from Munich, Germany. I'm
half-German, half-Korean. I'm interested in photography."

He sat down.

The silence held for one beat longer than normal. Then the room exhaled, and the
introductions continued, and Caspar went back to listening, and did not lean
back two degrees to look at the door across the hall.

## II

The first break period arrived and the classroom transformed.

Caspar observed it from his desk the way he'd once observed the lobby of the
Alpenhof from behind the luggage cart—with the detached attentiveness of someone
who was present but not participating, watching patterns form and dissolve in
real time. Students migrated. Clusters emerged. Conversations sparked along
predictable fault lines: shared middle schools, shared neighborhoods, shared
interests stated during introductions.

He had expected to be left alone. The hotel had taught him this—that a certain
kind of stillness, a certain quality of reserved silence, functioned as social
camouflage. People who did not invite approach were generally not approached.
This was efficient. This was comfortable.

This was not what happened.

"Oi."

Caspar looked up. Watanabe Kenji was standing in front of his desk, arms
crossed, head tilted, wearing the expression of a person who had identified
something interesting and had no intention of leaving it unexamined.

"Ryu. Tae-sung."

"Yes."

"You said photography."

"I did."

"Like, Instagram photography? Phone stuff?"

"Film."

Kenji blinked. "Film. Like, actual film? With a camera that uses—" He mimed the
act of winding a film advance lever, which he performed with surprising
accuracy. "—the little clicky thing?"

"Yes."

"That's kind of—" Kenji searched for the word, his whole face working through
options. "—that's kind of extremely cool, actually. Do you develop it yourself?"

"I have a darkroom."

"You have a _darkroom._ In your house?"

"In a closet."

Kenji pulled the chair from the empty desk behind him, spun it around, and sat
on it backwards with his forearms resting on the backrest. This was apparently
happening.

"Okay, so, question. How does that work? You take the picture, and then you go
home, and you go into the closet, and then what? Is it like in the movies where
there's red light and stuff hanging on clotheslines?"

"More or less."

"Can I see it?"

Caspar looked at Kenji. The boy's face was entirely open—no agenda, no subtext,
no calculation. Just raw, unfiltered curiosity, the kind that had been
socialized out of most people by age twelve but had somehow survived in Kenji
like a protected species.

"Maybe," Caspar said.

Kenji grinned. It was a wide, uncomplicated grin that used his entire face. "I'm
going to hold you to that, Ryu."

Before Caspar could respond, another body appeared at his desk—Ito Ren, the
runner, who had materialized with the silent efficiency of someone who covered
ground quickly and without unnecessary motion.

"You're tall," Ren said. Not a question. An assessment.

"Yes."

"Do you run?"

"Not competitively."

"But you run."

Caspar considered this. During the hotel year, he had run. Early mornings,
before his shift, on the mountain roads behind the property—four kilometers,
sometimes six, in the thin alpine air, his breath clouding, the Zugspitze
looming in the predawn darkness like a monument to indifference. He'd run
because his body had needed to move after twelve-hour shifts of carrying and
fetching and standing and absorbing the particular radiation of other people's
entitlement. He'd run because it was the only time he was alone—truly alone, no
guests, no concierge, no uniform—and the only time his thoughts moved in a
single direction instead of circling.

"I run," he said.

Ren nodded. Something passed between them—not friendship, not yet, but a
recognition. Two people who understood that running was not about competition or
fitness but about the specific, wordless clarity that came from forward motion.

"We should run sometime," Ren said.

"Sure."

Ren nodded again and returned to his seat. Kenji, who had watched this exchange
with the focused attention of a nature documentarian, leaned forward.

"You just got invited to run by Ito Ren. Do you understand what just happened?
Ito Ren doesn't invite people to run. Ito Ren runs alone. Ito Ren is like a
wolf."

"We went to the same middle school," said a voice from behind Kenji. Daiki had
migrated closer, his mechanical pencil tucked behind his ear. "He's not a wolf.
He just doesn't like talking while he runs."

"That's wolf behavior," Kenji said.

"That's running," Ren said, from three desks away, without turning around.

Caspar felt something shift at the edge of his awareness—a loosening, a small
release of pressure he hadn't known he was carrying. These people were _normal._
Aggressively, beautifully normal. They weren't the children of diplomats
performing friendship in five languages. They weren't hotel guests who looked
through him like he was furniture. They were just teenagers, doing the awkward,
instinctive work of trying to connect with a stranger because the stranger
happened to be sitting near them.

He had not realized how much he'd missed this.

His phone buzzed in his pocket. He reached for it under the desk, angling the
screen toward his body.

LINE. From Isabelle.

> **신지원:** 1-3.

One message. The class number. No elaboration. But he understood the subtext—she
was confirming her location, establishing coordinates, drawing the first line on
a map they would navigate together.

He typed back:

> **류태성:** 1-2. 복도 건너편.
>
> _1-2. Across the hall._

He watched the screen. The "read" indicator appeared. Then the typing
animation—three pulsing dots—started and stopped. Started again. Stopped.

Then:

> **신지원:** 알고 있어.
>
> _I know._

He stared at those two words. _I know._ Which meant she'd already looked. She'd
already scanned the class rosters, already identified his room, already mapped
the distance between them. She had walked into this building this morning and
done exactly what he had done—assessed, cataloged, and silently located the one
person who mattered.

He put the phone away.

"Ryu." Kenji was still sitting backwards on the chair, watching him with
unconcealed interest. "Was that your girlfriend?"

"No."

"Boyfriend?"

"No."

"Family?"

"No."

"Mystery contact?"

Caspar looked at him.

Kenji raised both hands in surrender. "Okay, okay. Mystery contact. I respect
the privacy. Very cool, very mysterious."

From behind his glasses, Daiki muttered: "Leave the man alone, Kenji."

"I'm _bonding_, Daiki. This is what bonding looks like."

"It looks like harassment."

"It looks like _friendship._"

Caspar almost smiled. Almost. The muscles engaged—a micro-contraction at the
corners of his mouth, a softening around his eyes that lasted maybe half a
second—and then it was gone, smoothed back into the neutral architecture of his
resting expression.

But Kenji saw it. His eyes widened.

"Did you just almost smile? Ryu. Ryu, was that a smile? Daiki, did you see
that?"

"I saw nothing," Daiki said.

"It was there! For like a millisecond! It was like watching the sun come out
during a—"

The bell rang. Kenji swore under his breath, returned the chair to its desk, and
dropped into his own seat with the boneless collapse of someone who had been
performing at maximum output for ten minutes straight.

Morimoto-sensei re-entered the room. Mathematics textbooks were distributed. The
first lesson of high school began.

Caspar opened his textbook, uncapped his pen, and wrote the date at the top of a
clean page in his notebook.

_April 8th._

And below it, in the smallest handwriting he could manage, in the Korean script
that no one in this classroom could read:

_그녀가 여기 있다._

_She's here._

## III

Lunch at Sakuragaoka High operated on a system that Isabelle had spent twenty
minutes studying in the student handbook the night before, and which turned out,
in practice, to be far simpler and far louder than the handbook had suggested.

There was no formal cafeteria service. Students could buy bread and pre-packaged
meals from a small school store on the first floor—a window in the wall staffed
by two women in hairnets who dispensed yakisoba-pan and curry bread with the
battle-hardened efficiency of field medics—or they could eat in their homeroom,
or they could go outside to the courtyard. The only rule was that you ate.
Ōtsuka-sensei had mentioned this twice during the morning, with the gentle
insistence of someone who had seen too many students skip lunch and crash by
fifth period.

Isabelle had her bento.

She opened it at her desk, and the smell that rose from the lacquered box
stopped Yuki's hand mid-page-turn.

Auntie Choi's bento was an act of structural engineering. Steamed short-grain
rice, packed firm and glossy, occupied the left compartment. The right side held
bulgogi—thin-sliced beef marinated in soy and sesame and pear, grilled to a
caramelized sheen—alongside seasoned spinach, pickled radish cut into
quarter-moons, a small mound of stir-fried anchovy, and two pieces of rolled egg
omelette. A separate container held kimchi, sealed under three layers of wrap
because Auntie Choi understood that kimchi in a school bag was a biochemical
event that required containment protocols.

"That," Yuki said slowly, her book now fully lowered, "is not a normal bento."

"My housekeeper makes it."

"Your housekeeper."

The word landed with a small, specific weight. Yuki's gaze moved from the bento
to Isabelle's face, and Isabelle could see the file being updated—a new data
point slotting into place alongside _tall, Dutch-Korean, ballet, French pastry._
Housekeeper.

In Japan, having a housekeeper was not common. It wasn't unheard of—some
families, particularly dual-income families or those with elderly relatives,
employed part-time help. But the word carried connotations. It implied a
household that was structured differently from the norm. It implied resources.

Isabelle registered her mistake, filed it, and moved on. "She's Korean. She's
been with my family for a long time. She gets—" She paused, choosing her words.
"—very serious about lunch."

"I can see that." Yuki looked at her own lunch—a convenience store onigiri and a
small carton of green tea, purchased that morning from the Lawson near the
school gate. She looked back at Isabelle's bento. Her expression did not change,
but something in the set of her mouth suggested she was experiencing a private
reassessment of her own lunch choices.

Sora appeared. She had acquired a yakisoba-pan from the school store—a long,
soft roll stuffed with fried noodles and drizzled with mayonnaise—and was eating
it one-handed while scrolling her phone with the other.

"Ji-won. Ji-won. Look at this." She held the phone out. The screen showed a
social media post—a blurry fancam of VESPER performing at a music show, the four
members in synchronized motion, stage lights strobing behind them. "They
performed 'Glass Hour' on M Countdown yesterday and the fanchants were
_insane._"

Isabelle looked at the video. She could identify each member by silhouette
alone—Yuna in the center-left position, her movements languid and controlled;
Minji on the right, sharp and precise; Haeun in the back, explosive power masked
by a sweet smile; and Jiwoo, the youngest, only eighteen, whose stage presence
burned so bright it was almost frightening given that she was, off-stage, the
quietest person Isabelle had ever met.

She watched Jiwoo hit the chorus choreography—a sequence that Isabelle herself
had learned in the practice room, standing three meters behind the group,
mirroring their movements in the floor-to-ceiling mirror while the choreographer
shouted corrections in Korean. She remembered the exact feel of that transition:
the weight shift from the right foot, the snap of the wrist, the way the hip had
to isolate at the top of the beat. She remembered Jiwoo turning around after the
take and saying, in her soft Busan dialect, _"Unnie, your arms are better than
mine. That's annoying."_

"They're very synchronized," Isabelle said.

Sora stared at her. "Very synchronized. That's—Ji-won. That's like saying the
ocean is 'damp.'"

"I don't know much about K-pop performance," Isabelle said. The lie sat in her
mouth like a smooth stone. She could taste its shape.

Hina, from two rows back, said nothing. But when Isabelle glanced in her
direction, Hina was watching her with that particular steady gaze—not
suspicious, not accusatory, just _aware._ As if the frequency of Isabelle's
composure was pinging something in her that she hadn't yet decided how to
categorize.

Isabelle ate a piece of bulgogi. It was perfect.

## IV

Across the hallway, Caspar was eating his bento alone.

This was by choice. Kenji had invited him to eat with "the guys"—a term that
apparently encompassed himself, Ren, Daiki, and two other boys whose names
Caspar had filed but not yet fully indexed—but Caspar had declined with a "maybe
next time" that was honest enough. He needed the silence. He needed to process
the morning—the introductions, the classroom, the small ordinary rituals of a
Japanese school day—without the overlay of social performance.

He also needed to think about Isabelle.

She was here. Across the hall. In a classroom he could almost see from his desk.
She was breathing the same air, hearing the same bells, walking the same
hallways. After a year of distance—a year of silence, of unread drafts and
deleted voice messages and the slow, grinding work of trying to forget what her
hair smelled like—she was _here_, twelve meters away, separated by a wall and a
hallway and the entire unresolved architecture of whatever it was that had
broken between them on that terrace in Switzerland.

He opened his bento. Auntie Park's work: rice, kimchi-jjigae reheated and packed
into a thermos container, japchae with beef and spinach, seasoned bean sprouts,
and a small compartment containing four slices of the Munich pretzel, cut and
arranged like bread service at a restaurant.

He ate the pretzel first. Then the japchae. Then the rice, mixing it with the
jjigae the way his father did—a habit his mother considered barbaric and his
father considered the only correct way to eat soup with rice.

His phone buzzed.

He didn't reach for it immediately. He finished chewing. He set down his
chopsticks. He wiped his hands on the small cloth napkin Auntie Park had
included (cotton, pressed, folded into a precise rectangle, because even
portable napkins had standards). Then he picked up the phone.

LINE. Not Isabelle.

It was the group chat—his parents' family chat. His mother had sent another
message:

> **엄마:** How is the school? Is your homeroom teacher nice? Did you eat your
> bento? Send me a photo of your classroom! 사랑해 ❤️

His father had replied:

> **아빠:** Don't pressure him.

His mother:

> **엄마:** I'm not pressuring him, I'm being interested. There's a difference.

His father:

> **아빠:** Helena.

His mother:

> **엄마:** Ji-seok.

This exchange, which from the outside might have looked like a disagreement, was
in fact the Waldstein-Ryu marriage operating at its standard frequency. His
mother—Helena Waldstein, CEO of a cosmetics empire, a woman who ran a
twelve-country operation with the organizational precision of a military
campaign—expressed love through information-gathering. His father—Ryu Ji-seok,
chairman of a pharmaceutical conglomerate, a man who communicated in sentence
fragments and strategic silences—expressed love by protecting Caspar from his
mother's information-gathering. The dynamic had been this way for fifteen years.
It worked.

Caspar took a photo of his classroom—empty desks, blackboard, the cherry trees
visible through the window—and sent it to the group chat without comment.

His mother replied instantly: **예쁘다!! 🌸**

His father replied twelve minutes later: **Good.**

Caspar closed the family chat and navigated, with the slow inevitability of
water finding a drain, to his chat with Isabelle.

Their exchange from this morning sat on the screen. Five messages total. Eight
words in Korean. The entire emotional infrastructure of a relationship
compressed into two exchanges that said _I'm here_ and _I know_ and nothing
else.

He started typing.

> **류태성:** 점심 뭐 먹어?
>
> _What are you eating for lunch?_

He stared at the message. It was banal. It was the kind of thing you'd ask a
coworker, an acquaintance, someone you were making small talk with because the
silence was uncomfortable. It was not what you said to the girl whose kiss you'd
refused on a Swiss terrace because the force of wanting it had been so
overwhelming that it had short-circuited every word you'd ever learned.

He deleted it.

He typed:

> **류태성:** 오래간만이다.
>
> _It's been a long time._

He deleted that too.

He typed:

> **류태성:** 너 키 컸다.
>
> _You got taller._

He deleted it. She hadn't gotten taller. He had gotten taller. Or maybe they'd
both gotten taller. The point was that this was a stupid thing to say and he was
going to put the phone down and eat his bean sprouts.

He ate his bean sprouts.

He picked the phone back up.

> **류태성:** 아까 복도에서, 네가 고개 끄덕였을 때.
>
> _Earlier in the hallway, when you nodded._

He didn't finish the sentence. He didn't send it. He held the phone for ten
seconds, watching the cursor blink, and then locked the screen and slid it into
his pocket.

The bell rang. Lunch was over. He closed his bento, re-wrapped the cloth napkin,
and returned everything to its bag.

Across the hallway, through the wall he couldn't see through, Isabelle was doing
the same thing.

## V

The afternoon passed in the particular rhythm of a Japanese school
day—fifty-minute periods separated by ten-minute breaks, each transition marked
by a bell that rang through the building with the flat, institutional chime of a
system designed to move humans through time in orderly increments.

English class. Isabelle sat through it with the internal discipline of a
professional actress maintaining character during a scene she could perform in
her sleep. The teacher—a young woman named Fuji-sensei who had studied at a
university in Oregon and peppered her Japanese instructions with enthusiastic
but slightly off-tempo English phrases—distributed textbooks and led the class
through a reading exercise about a boy visiting London for the first time.

"Can someone read the first paragraph? Let's see—Shin-san?"

Isabelle stood. She looked at the page. The English was simple, declarative,
written at a level that assumed its readers had been studying the language for
three years and could handle the present perfect tense but not much beyond.

She read it. In careful, slightly accented English. The accent was real—her
native English carried traces of Dutch and the particular international-school
mid-Atlantic polish that came from growing up in multilingual environments—but
she flattened it, slowed it, added small hesitations at word boundaries. The
filter. Always the filter.

"Very good, Shin-san! Your pronunciation is excellent."

"Thank you." She sat down.

Yuki glanced at her. Just a glance—quick, lateral, the kind of look that lasted
less than a second but carried the full weight of an observation being recorded.

"You've spoken English before," Yuki said, during the break. Not a question.

"A little. My father speaks it at work."

"A little," Yuki repeated, in a tone that suggested she was choosing not to
challenge this and filing the discrepancy for later review.

Isabelle changed the subject. "What club are you planning to join?"

The pivot worked. Yuki's posture shifted—a slight straightening, a
re-engagement, the topic change accepted as a social contract. "I'm considering
the student council administrative committee. They handle event logistics and
scheduling."

"That sounds perfect for you."

The faintest trace of a smile crossed Yuki's face. "It does, doesn't it."

---

Science class. Caspar sat in the second row and listened to a biology teacher
named Kudo-sensei explain the structure of the cell with a whiteboard marker
that squeaked every third stroke. The content was elementary—he'd covered this
material two years ago at Institut Le Parcours, where the science program had
been designed by a former researcher from ETH Zurich and delivered at a pace
that assumed its students would be applying to Oxbridge by sixteen.

But Caspar didn't tune out. He listened. He took notes in Japanese, his
handwriting still stiff and angular—he could speak and read fluently, but the
physical act of writing kanji by hand was still slower than he wanted, each
character requiring a level of conscious attention that his German and Korean
handwriting had long since automated.

The boy next to him—Ren, the runner, who had somehow ended up adjacent in the
seating chart—was taking notes with the efficiency of someone who processed
information in clean, compressed packets. His notebook was spare. Key terms.
Diagrams. No decoration.

During the break, Ren turned to him. "Your kanji is careful."

It wasn't a compliment or a criticism. It was what it was: an observation from
someone who noticed details.

"I learned most of my Japanese through speaking," Caspar said. "The writing is
slower."

Ren nodded. "My grandmother is from Osaka. She says Tokyo people write too much
and say too little." A pause. "I think she's right."

This was, by Ren's apparent standards, an extensive personal disclosure. Caspar
received it accordingly.

"I'd like that approach," Caspar said.

"You already use it." Ren turned back to his notebook. Conversation complete.

## VI

The final bell rang at 3:15.

Cleaning time. The students of Class 1-2 dispersed to their assigned zones with
varying degrees of enthusiasm. Kenji, who was Caspar's cleaning group leader,
approached this responsibility the way he approached everything else—at full
volume.

"Alright, hallway crew! We have approximately forty meters of linoleum between
us and freedom. I want it gleaming. I want it reflecting. I want to see my face
in this floor, and I want my face to look good."

He distributed supplies: brooms, dustpans, damp cloths for the floor. Caspar
received a broom.

"Ryu, you take the far end. By the stairs. That's where the dust collects—it's
the Bermuda Triangle of hallway dirt."

Caspar took the broom and walked to the far end of the hallway. The corridor was
long, lit by fluorescent tubes, the linoleum worn to a dull sheen by years of
indoor shoes. He began sweeping. The motion was familiar—the hotel had required
back-of-house staff to sweep the service corridors at the end of every shift,
and Caspar's technique had been critiqued, corrected, and eventually approved by
a housekeeping supervisor named Frau Lindner who believed that a broom should be
held at precisely thirty degrees from vertical.

He swept. The dust gathered in small gray drifts along the baseboard. The
hallway was quiet—most students were in their zones, and the only sounds were
the swish of brooms and the distant, echoey noise of someone mopping a
stairwell.

Then: footsteps. Light, evenly spaced, approaching from the direction of Class
1-3.

He didn't look up. He knew the footsteps. He had cataloged them years ago, in
the marble hallways of Institut Le Parcours, where she'd walk beside him between
classes with that particular dancer's gait—each step placed, not taken, the
weight rolling through the foot from heel to ball to toe with a precision that
most people didn't achieve even when they were trying.

Isabelle rounded the corner carrying a bucket and a floor cloth.

She was in her school uniform, the sleeves of her blouse rolled to her elbows,
her hair pulled back in a loose twist secured with what appeared to be a pencil.
The bucket was plastic, blue, filled with water that sloshed gently with each
step. Her expression was composed, neutral, the face of a student going to her
cleaning assignment.

Their eyes met.

The hallway was empty. Kenji and the others were around the corner, out of
sight. The fluorescent light hummed overhead, flat and shadowless, the kind of
light that made most people look washed out but which caught the honey tones in
Isabelle's hair and the pale clarity of her skin and turned them into something
that made Caspar's hands tighten on the broom handle.

Five meters between them. Then four. Then three.

Isabelle stopped. She set the bucket down. The water stilled.

"Hi," she said.

The word was in Japanese. Casual, unremarkable, the kind of greeting any student
might offer to another student in a hallway during cleaning time. But her
eyes—large, hazel-green, steady—were saying something else entirely, in a
language that had nothing to do with Japanese or Korean or Dutch or German. They
were saying: _I have been waiting for this all day and I am terrified._

"Hi," Caspar said.

A beat. Two beats. The fluorescent light hummed.

"You're sweeping," she said.

"I am."

"You're good at it."

"I had training."

Something flickered in her expression—a recognition, a connection sparking
between his words and the knowledge she carried about his gap year, about the
hotel, about the months he'd spent performing invisible labor for people who
didn't see him. The flicker lasted half a second. Then it was replaced by
something warmer, something that lived in the corners of her mouth and the
slight narrowing of her eyes.

"The hotel," she said. Quietly. In Korean, though she shouldn't have—anyone
could have walked around the corner.

"네." _Yes._

"Was it awful?"

"Parts of it."

"Parts of it," she repeated. She looked at the broom in his hands, at his
fingers wrapped around the wooden handle, and Caspar had the sudden, visceral
awareness that she was looking at his hands the way he looked at light when he
was deciding whether to take a photograph—studying the angles, the tension, the
story being told by the way something was held.

"Your shoulders are different," she said.

"I know."

"From the luggage."

"Yes."

She nodded. The information was received, processed, stored. She picked up her
bucket.

"My zone is the stairwell at the end," she said, switching back to Japanese. "I
should go."

"Isabelle."

He said her name in the European way—three syllables, the stress on the second,
the way he'd been saying it since they were eleven years old in a Swiss boarding
school where names were the first currency of friendship. Not Shin Ji-won. Not
the Japanese version. _Isabelle._

She stopped. Her back was to him. He could see the line of her spine through the
school blouse, the slight movement of her shoulder blades as she breathed.

"우리 얘기해야 돼." _We need to talk._

She didn't turn around. Three seconds passed. The water in the bucket settled
into perfect stillness.

"알아," she said. _I know._

"언제?" _When?_

She turned her head—not fully, just enough that he could see the curve of her
cheek, the edge of her eyelash, the small, precise muscle at the corner of her
jaw that tightened when she was thinking.

"아직은 아니야." _Not yet._

She walked away. Her footsteps echoed down the corridor—light, even, placed. The
bucket swung gently at her side. She rounded the corner and was gone.

Caspar stood in the empty hallway, holding a broom, and breathed.

"Ryu!" Kenji's voice from around the corner, loud enough to vibrate the
fluorescent tubes. "You alive back there? I need that section done in five
minutes! I promised Morimoto-sensei a gleaming hallway and I am a man of my
word!"

Caspar swept.

## VII

The day ended.

Students poured through the school gate in loose, noisy streams, dispersing into
the late-afternoon light like mercury breaking apart on a tilted surface. The
cherry blossoms were still falling—thinner now, the peak giving way to the slow,
imperceptible decline that would leave the trees bare within a week. The air had
warmed since morning, carrying the faint sweetness of decomposing petals and the
exhaust tang of city buses idling on the street beyond the school wall.

Isabelle walked through the gate with Yuki beside her. This had happened
naturally—they had left the classroom at the same time, fallen into step in the
hallway, and neither had altered their pace to break the alignment. It was the
kind of unconscious synchronization that happened between people whose rhythms
were compatible, and it pleased Isabelle in a way she didn't examine too
closely.

"Which direction is your station?" Yuki asked.

"I walk. My apartment is nearby."

This was a half-truth. The apartment in Minami-Aoyama was within walking
distance—about twenty minutes at Isabelle's pace—but the walk took her through
some of the most expensive real estate in Tokyo, past galleries and boutiques
and the kind of quiet, tree-lined streets where the buildings didn't have
visible price tags because the people who lived in them didn't need to ask.

"I take the Odakyū line," Yuki said. "Setagaya. About thirty minutes."

"That's not bad."

"It's tolerable." Yuki adjusted the strap of her bag. "Shin-san—"

"Ji-won."

"Ji-won." Yuki tested the name, accepted it. "There's a boy in Class 1-2. Tall.
Foreign. Pale eyes."

Isabelle's stride did not change. Her breathing did not change. The only thing
that changed was a small contraction deep in her chest, as if a string had been
pulled taut.

"Ryu Tae-sung," Yuki said. "Half-Korean, half-German. He introduced himself
during the combined assembly portion. I was watching from three rows back."

"Oh?"

"He looks at you."

The words fell into the space between them with the clean precision of a
scalpel. Isabelle kept walking. The cherry blossoms drifted.

"He looked at you during the assembly. Once, from across the gymnasium. For
approximately four seconds." Yuki paused. "Most people don't look at strangers
for four seconds."

Isabelle said nothing. They reached the corner where the school street
intersected with the main road. Traffic moved past—a delivery truck, a taxi, a
woman on a bicycle with a child seat on the back.

"I don't know him," Isabelle said.

The lie was flawless. Her tone was even, mildly curious, the voice of someone
hearing a piece of information for the first time and finding it interesting but
unimportant. The language filter worked in all directions—it could make fluency
sound like effort, and it could make the most important person in her life sound
like a name she'd heard once and forgotten.

Yuki looked at her. The look lasted two seconds—one second longer than casual,
one second shorter than confrontational. Then she turned forward.

"My mistake," Yuki said.

They walked another block in silence. At the intersection where their paths
diverged—Yuki toward the station, Isabelle toward Aoyama—they stopped.

"See you tomorrow, Ji-won."

"See you tomorrow, Yuki."

Yuki walked away, her steps precise, her bag centered on her back, her posture
suggesting that she had filed the Ryu Tae-sung data point exactly where she
wanted it and was in no hurry to retrieve it.

Isabelle watched her go. Then she turned and walked toward Minami-Aoyama, alone,
the afternoon light warm on her face.

Her phone buzzed.

She took it out. LINE.

Not Caspar. Sora.

> **そら🌙:** Ji-won!! First day survival!! 🎉🎉 **そら🌙:** Also I found out
> there's a VESPER fan event in Shibuya next month and I'm DYING **そら🌙:** Do
> you want to come?? It's free, you just have to line up early **そら🌙:** Bring
> your housekeeper's bento, we'll need sustenance for the queue 😂

Isabelle read the messages. She thought about the fan event. She thought about
standing in a line in Shibuya, surrounded by teenagers holding VESPER
lightsticks and photo cards, waiting to see the four girls she had lived with,
trained with, eaten convenience store ramyeon with at 2 AM in a practice room in
Gangnam.

She thought about how Yuna would find this hilarious.

She typed:

> **지원:** Sounds fun. Let me check my schedule.

She put the phone away. She walked three more blocks, turned onto a quiet street
lined with zelkova trees, and entered the lobby of her building. The doorman—a
man in his sixties with white gloves and a posture that suggested decades of
professional courtesy—bowed and said, "Welcome home, Shin-sama."

_Shin-sama._ Not _Ji-won-chan._ Not _Shin-san._ The honorific of someone who
knew exactly whose daughter she was and was calibrating his respect accordingly.

"Thank you," she said, and took the elevator to the ninth floor.

---

The apartment was quiet. Auntie Choi was in the kitchen, the sound of a knife
against a cutting board marking a steady, percussive rhythm. The late-afternoon
light came through the west-facing windows at a low angle, throwing long
parallelograms of gold across the pale oak floor.

Isabelle removed her shoes in the genkan, placed them on the rack, and stepped
up into the apartment. She dropped her bag on the bench by the door and stood
for a moment in the hallway, letting the silence settle around her.

The gift basket from her mother was still on the table. She looked at it now.
White box, white ribbon, the logo of a French fashion house embossed in gold on
the lid. She didn't open it.

She went to her room. Closed the door. Sat on the edge of her bed—a
Western-style queen with white linen sheets that Auntie Choi changed every three
days—and stared at the ceiling.

Her room was large by Tokyo standards, decorated in what the interior designer
had called "Scandinavian-Korean minimalism": clean lines, neutral tones, natural
textures. A low wooden bookshelf held a mix of Dutch, Korean, English, French,
and German books—novels, poetry, a few cookbooks with spines cracked from use.
Above the bookshelf, a framed photograph: four girls in black rehearsal clothes,
laughing, caught mid-motion in a practice room. The faces were blurred by
movement, but if you knew where to look, you could see the outline of a fifth
figure at the edge of the frame—taller, lighter-haired, half-turned away.

That was her. Three months into the Seoul year, during a practice session that
had run past midnight. Haeun had set a timer on her phone and said, "Group
photo, nobody pose, I want it chaotic." The result was four idols who were about
to become the biggest group in Asia, frozen in a moment of genuine, unperformed
joy, and one Dutch-Korean girl at the edge who hadn't realized she was in the
frame until afterward.

Isabelle stared at the photo. She thought about Seoul. She thought about the
practice room—the rubber-matted floor, the mirrors that showed you every
imperfection, the speakers that rattled the walls when the bass dropped. She
thought about the exhaustion, the specificity of it: not the clean tiredness of
a ballet class but the full-body, sweat-soaked, shaking-hands depletion of
running the same eight-count forty times until the choreographer said
"acceptable" instead of "again."

She thought about how none of her new friends knew any of this. How Sora, who
worshipped VESPER with the pure, burning faith of a true fan, had no idea that
the tall foreign girl sitting next to her in Class 1-3 could perform their debut
choreography from memory, that she had Yuna's personal phone number, that she
had once beaten Minji at arm wrestling in the Shin Entertainment break room and
the leader of Asia's biggest girl group had demanded a rematch and lost again.

She smiled. Then the smile faded, because thinking about Seoul made her think
about the year that had preceded it, and the year before that was Switzerland,
and Switzerland was a stone terrace and a cold evening and Caspar's face turning
away from hers at the last possible moment.

Her phone was in her hand. She didn't remember picking it up.

She opened LINE. His chat. Their twelve-word history.

She started typing. The Korean characters appeared on the screen, each one
familiar, each one loaded.

> **신지원:** 오늘 복도에서 만났을 때, 얼마나 놀랐어?
>
> _When we ran into each other in the hallway today, how surprised were you?_

She stared at the message. Her thumb hovered over the send button.

She thought about his face in the hallway during cleaning time. The way he'd
said her name—_Isabelle_—in the European way, the way no one in Seoul had ever
said it, the way no one in Tokyo would say it. The way the sound of it in his
voice had moved through her body like a note struck on an instrument she'd
forgotten she owned.

She pressed send.

The "delivered" indicator appeared. Then "read."

Then the three dots. Pulsing. Typing.

They stopped.

Started again.

Stopped.

Isabelle waited. The light through her window shifted from gold to amber. Auntie
Choi's knife continued its rhythm in the kitchen. Somewhere in the building, a
door closed softly.

Then:

> **류태성:** 놀라지 않았어.
>
> _I wasn't surprised._

She frowned.

> **신지원:** 거짓말.
>
> _Liar._

A pause. Shorter this time.

> **류태성:** 진짜. 놀라지 않았어. 네가 있을 거라는 걸 알았어. 왜인지는
> 모르겠지만. 오늘 아침에 학교에 들어서는 순간부터, 그냥 알았어.
>
> _Really. I wasn't surprised. I knew you'd be there. I don't know how. From the
> moment I walked into the school this morning, I just knew._

Isabelle read the message three times.

She set the phone face-down on the bed.

She pressed both hands over her eyes and breathed—in through the nose, out
through the mouth, the way Jisoo-unnie had taught her to breathe before going
onstage, the way you breathe when your body is doing something your mind hasn't
authorized.

When she picked the phone back up, she typed:

> **신지원:** 나도.
>
> _Me too._

Send.

Then, before he could reply:

> **신지원:** 내일 점심 같이 먹을래? 학교에서. 옥상이 있으면 거기서. 아니면
> 어디든.
>
> _Do you want to eat lunch together tomorrow? At school. The rooftop, if there
> is one. Or anywhere._

The dots appeared immediately.

> **류태성:** 옥상은 잠겨 있을 거야. 일본 학교들은 다 그래.
>
> _The rooftop will be locked. Japanese schools are all like that._

> **신지원:** 그럼 어디든.
>
> _Then anywhere._

> **류태성:** 어디든.
>
> _Anywhere._

Isabelle stared at the screen. The last message sat at the bottom of the thread,
a single word in Korean, and it contained in its two syllables more promise and
more terror than anything she had felt in a very long time.

She typed one more message:

> **신지원:** 잘 자, 캐스퍼.
>
> _Good night, Caspar._

She used his real name. Not Ryu Tae-sung. Not the Japanese version. _Caspar._
The name she'd called him since they were eleven.

The reply came in thirty seconds:

> **류태성:** 잘 자, 이자벨.
>
> _Good night, Isabelle._

She locked the phone. She placed it on the nightstand. She lay back on the bed,
her school uniform still on, her hair fanning across the white linen, and stared
at the ceiling while the room turned gold around her.

In Hiroo, in an apartment across the city, Caspar was doing the same thing.
