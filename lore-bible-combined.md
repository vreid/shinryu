# The Lore Bible — Isabelle & Caspar

---

## Core Premise

Two teenagers from wealthy European–Korean families arrive at a Japanese public
high school at the start of their second year. They are deeply in love,
battle-tested by three years of forced separation, and completely unaware that
their level of intimacy is shocking to everyone around them. European "old
married couple" energy collides with Japanese high school social norms.

**Tone:** Comedy-forward with serious emotional undertones. Cinematic, grounded,
punchy. The comedy is purely cultural friction: two European trauma-bonded
teenagers being aggressively European in a regimented Japanese school. No cheap
drama, no love triangles.

**Format:** Episodic web novel. Each chapter explores a specific concept or
situation.

---

## The Families

### van Rijn–Shin Clan

**European root:** van Rijn Private Banking & Global Real Estate — Dutch banking
dynasty, seventeenth-century old money based in Wassenaar. The kind of wealth
that doesn't appear on lists because the lists are compiled by people who work
for them.

**Korean root:** Shin Entertainment — Asia's premier K-pop agency, built
post-IMF crisis by a former dancer who understood that cultural influence
appreciates faster than real estate.

**Father:** Willem van Rijn — CEO of van Rijn Private Banking. Perpetually in
Zurich, London, or Singapore.

**Mother:** Shin Hee-yeon — CEO of Shin Entertainment. Compact, sharp bob,
minimal makeup. Enters rooms like a conductor. Loves her daughter fiercely,
strategically, and from behind a desk via structural decisions.

### Waldstein–Ryu Clan

**European root:** Waldstein Botanicals & Aesthetics — ultra-luxury skincare
brand, rooted in German minor nobility that pivoted to luxury branding.

**Korean root:** Ryu BioPharma & Chemical — industrial chaebol,
chemicals-to-pharma pipeline.

**Mother:** Helena Waldstein — CEO of Waldstein Botanicals. German old money,
cosmetics industry. Silver-blonde hair, navy blazer, gold chain with the
Waldstein logo.

**Father:** Ryu Ji-seok — Chairman of Ryu BioPharma & Chemical. Communicates in
sentence fragments and strategic silences.

### The Inter-Family Web

The clans orbit each other through corporate boards and charity galas. Close
enough to plan a generational merger-marriage between their children. Distant
enough that the children never grow up as siblings. The corporate architecture
and personal architecture converge — and eventually detonate.

## The Children

### Isabelle van Rijn / Shin Ji-won

**Born:** January 14, 2001

**Home base:** Wassenaar, Netherlands.

**Appearance:** 5'10", statuesque. Heavy wavy caramel-blonde hair. Pale glowing
glass skin. Flawless posture of a trained dancer.

**Languages:** Dutch, Korean, English, Japanese. She downplays her Japanese
fluency; it is easier to be underestimated.

### Caspar Waldstein / Ryu Tae-sung

**Born:** March 29, 2001

**Home base:** Munich, Germany.

**Appearance:** 6'1", lean but broad-shouldered. Icy pale blue eyes. After the
exile: shoulders broadened by agricultural labor, hands roughened. A farm-built
body that reads as mysterious to teenagers.

**Languages:** German, Korean, English, Japanese. He also downplays his
Japanese.

### Moon Jungsook

Assigned to Isabelle since birth. Compact, center-parted bob. Korean in her

### Bae Myunghi

Assigned to Caspar since birth. Tall, broad-shouldered, permed hair. Irons

## The Swiss Years (Ages 10–13)

Both attended Institut Le Rosey and received an exceptional education: advanced
math and sciences, multilingual instruction (German, English minimum), IB Middle
Years Programme or equivalent, critical thinking and study discipline. Their
friendship developed naturally within this environment — unengineered by the
families.

---

## The Breakpoint (Spring 2014)

At age thirteen, Isabelle and Caspar learn their meeting was a planned
merger-marriage. Every shared moment is reframed as engineered.

Instead of pulling away, they aggressively reclaim their connection. If their
emotional bond was designed by the parents, they decide to burn the contract by
making their relationship undeniably theirs. They rebel openly and defiantly.

The parents do not just get angry — they panic. They wanted a pristine,
manageable merger at age twenty-five. They got a volatile, unsupervised crossing
of lines at thirteen. The parents act out of sheer terror at their loss of
control, dropping the hammer to hit the brakes. This triggers the three-year
exile.

---

## The Exile (Spring 2014 – Early 2017)

Privilege stripped. Caretakers removed. They are sent into radically different
environments.

### Secret Contact

Despite the enforced separation, Isabelle and Caspar maintain secret digital
contact throughout — texts and calls during exhausting days. They become each
other's emotional lifeline and build a private world the parents cannot control.
The shared punishment creates a shared enemy (the families) and forges an
unbreakable unit.

### Isabelle in Seoul

Isabelle trains with Shin Entertainment's pre-debut group, **VESPER**. She is
sent into the grinder as a nobody, not a princess. Her mother's message: "You
want independence? Earn it in my world."

She learns isolation choreography, dorm hierarchy, and bakes as stress relief.
Approximately six months before debut, she is told she will debut with VESPER —
but as a background dancer, then leave for Japan. She stays through that final
stretch to protect the four girls she trains with, ensuring their debut happens.
Isabelle and the VESPER members maintain a LINE group chat after she leaves.

### Caspar in Jeju

Caspar is sent to a Jeju botanical farming cooperative under Waldstein
Botanicals. The connection: Jeju is famous for natural cosmetic ingredients,
tying directly to his mother's supply chain. He builds a rugged, labor-intensive
physique. He masters regional Jeju cooking, which becomes his language of care.
He has been entirely out of formal education for three years.

Caspar genuinely likes VESPER as a group — he follows their pre-debut content
and enjoys their music — but because he has contact with Isabelle throughout,
this is a known interest rather than a secret fandom.

### The Parents' Miscalculation

The exile was meant to cool things down and break their bond. Instead, they
survived, hardened, and learned how to build with their own hands. The
separation created a shared enemy and delivered them to the other end deeply in
love and battle-tested.

---

## Tokyo: The Silent Apology (April 2017)

When the exile fails to break the teenagers, the parents realize they
accidentally nuked their own master plan. They hardened their kids into
self-sufficient laborers.

Tokyo is not a trap or a punishment. It is a face-saving retreat. Billionaire
chaebol leaders do not say "we made a mistake." They change the geography.
Dropping Isabelle and Caspar in the exact same Setagaya public school and
returning their caretakers is the parents' way of saying: _We surrender. Here
are two years to be normal._

It is also neutral territory where neither family dominates — and possibly the
families grooming them for Asia-Pacific operations. The tension between genuine
love and fulfilling a corporate plan they originally rejected hums underneath
everything.

### The "Bulletproof" Dynamic

There is no "will they/won't they" in this story. They possess the energy of a
fifty-year-old married couple trapped in adolescent bodies. They sit physically
entangled during class breaks. They argue in fluent, hushed German about laundry
detergent. There is zero jealousy; if a classmate hits on one of them, the other
patiently helps let the suitor down.

## Academic Context

### Why They Can Function in Japanese High School

Japanese known from early age (conversational fluency). Swiss elite education
provides strong foundations through seventh/eighth grade. Smart enough to
reactivate knowledge rather than build from scratch. Learning-how-to-learn
skills from elite schooling.

## The Six Friends

The initial mystery isn't "do they like each other?" It is "what kind of war did
these two survive?" The friends are drawn to the sheer, unapologetic intensity
of their dynamic.

### Isabelle's Three

**Nakamura Aoi:** Dance captain. Detects Isabelle's hidden physical training.

**Fujita Moe:** Obsessively organized. Notices the structural coincidences of
their lives.

**Tsukada Rin:** Loud, shame-free K-pop fan. Stumbles onto the VESPER connection
eventually.

### Caspar's Three

**Ogawa Shun:** Silent observer. Catalogs Caspar's regional Jeju skills.

**Miyake Renta:** Golden retriever athlete. Fiercely loyal without fully
understanding.

**Hosokawa Kai:** Music kid. Connects the auditory clues of Caspar's taste in
K-pop.

### The Revelation Table

The secret is discovered layer by layer. The hurt experienced by the friends
comes from the asymmetry of vulnerability, not the deception of wealth. The
healing happens at the table — through the Jeju cooking and Seoul baking that
Isabelle and Caspar have always sincerely shared with them.

## Work — Choosing the Dynasty

Isabelle and Caspar do not join school clubs. Out of their own free will, they
take part-time entry-level internships at Shin Entertainment Japan and Ryu
BioPharma Japan. They fetch water and scan inventory under bosses who know
exactly who they are.

This signals absolute victory on their own terms. The parents tried to force the
merger and failed. The teens choose the companies, and each other, autonomously.

## The Comedy Engine

### The Central Joke

Every normal Japanese high school romance norm gets obliterated by their casual
deep intimacy.

**Japanese teen romance expectations:** Shy confessions behind the school
building. Nervously walking home together. First-name usage being a huge deal.
Hand-holding being peak PDA.

**What these two do instead:** Pick lint off each other's uniforms. Share food
from the same chopsticks. Casual forehead touches when talking. Fix each other's
collars naturally. Finish each other's sentences. Bicker about nothing then
immediately be fine. She steals his jacket without asking. He automatically
carries her bag. Pet names in three different languages. They genuinely don't
understand why any of this is unusual.

### Cultural Collision Comedy

Dutch directness plus German bluntness versus Japanese politeness norms. They
say exactly what they think and classmates are horrified and fascinated.
Learning tatemae versus honne is a journey they repeatedly fail. They don't
think their behavior is remarkable, which makes it funnier.

### The Classmate Perspective

Nobody can categorize their relationship. It skips all recognized relationship
steps in Japanese dating culture. Classmates who just started nervously dating
feel personally attacked. Everyone waits for drama that never comes.

---

## Recurring Comedy Beats

### The Teacher Who Gave Up

First week: constantly separating them. Month two: just sighs. Eventually seats
them together because fighting it is more disruptive.

### The Lunch Scene Running Gag

She eats off his tray. He drinks from her bottle. Classmates watch like nature
documentary observers. Someone asks and they look confused.

### The Unshakeable Couple

Girls attempt to create jealousy — it bounces off. Boys try to befriend him
expecting relationship complaints. He genuinely has none. Everyone waits for a
breakup arc that never arrives.

### Lost in Translation

Directness causing social chaos. Misreading Japanese social cues. Accidentally
breaking unspoken rules constantly.

---

## The Serious Undercurrent

### Trauma Markers

She flinches when someone mentions her mother's agency. He has rough hands that
don't match his background. They instinctively close ranks when feeling
threatened. Survival instincts from their secondments surface unexpectedly.

### The Emotional Core

They chose each other at thirteen knowing the cost. Survived punishment designed
to break them. Their love isn't teenage butterflies — it's forged through
hardship. A quiet moment: _"I'd choose Jeju again."_ The question: is this love
or survival bonding? Does it matter?

---

## The Stealth Wealth

The wealth never performs. It serves. Name the material, not the brand. Show the
absence of logos. Deploy wealth as quiet problem-solving — a suspiciously nice
car ride, an impossibly sourced ingredient. The friends notice the gap between
what Isabelle and Caspar treat as basic and what is actually basic.

---

## Tone & Style Rules

**Dialogue:** Identifiable speech patterns. Husbands-and-wives banter.

**Food as care:** He cooks (Jeju). She bakes (Seoul). It is how they tell the
friends, and each other, that they are safe.

**Comedy source:** Cultural friction only. No cheap drama, no love triangles, no
miscommunication tropes.

**Pacing:** Mostly comedy. Sometimes devastating.
