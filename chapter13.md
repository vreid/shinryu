# Chapter 13: The Cost of Things

---

## I.

The problem arrived on a Tuesday, and it arrived through Ren.

Ren did not bring problems to the group. Ren did not, as a general rule, bring
anything to the group that was not his physical presence, his measured
observations, and occasionally his grilled salmon. He was the quietest member of
the table—quieter even than Hina, who was quiet by philosophy, whereas Ren was
quiet by constitution, the way some people were left-handed or tall. His silence
was structural. It was load-bearing.

So when Ren sat down at the combined lunch on Tuesday with his bento unopened
and his hands flat on his desk and his eyes fixed on a point in the middle
distance that suggested he was looking at something none of them could see, the
table noticed.

Kenji noticed first. Kenji always noticed first—his emotional radar operated at
a frequency that was inversely proportional to his volume, which meant that the
louder he was, the more precisely he was tracking the people around him.

"Ren."

No response.

"Ren."

Ren blinked. He looked at Kenji. His face was composed—the same lean, athletic
face, the same close-cropped hair, the same expression of pragmatic neutrality
that he wore to every meal and every class and every track practice. But
something behind the expression was different. Something had shifted—a weight
added, a balance disturbed, the particular look of someone carrying information
they hadn't decided how to hold.

"I'm fine," Ren said.

"You haven't opened your bento."

"I'm not hungry."

The table went quiet. Ren was always hungry. Ren was a distance runner who
trained six days a week and whose metabolic requirements meant he ate his bento,
then ate whatever anyone offered him, then occasionally bought a rice ball from
the school store as a mid-afternoon supplement. Ren not being hungry was a
system error.

"Ren," Isabelle said. Gently. The way she said things when the situation called
for the listener to feel safe—the voice she had developed in the practice room,
the voice that created space.

Ren looked at her. Something in his expression loosened—a single degree of
release, the jaw unclenching, the eyes softening from middle-distance to
present.

"The prefectural meet," he said. "It's in three weeks."

"The 800 and 1500," Caspar said.

"Yes." Ren looked at his closed bento. "My spikes are worn out. The foam in the
heel is compressed. I've been compensating with my stride, but the compensation
is creating a lateral stress on my left ankle that I can feel during the last
200 meters." He paused. "If I race in them, I'll either underperform or injure
myself."

"So get new spikes," Kenji said.

The silence that followed was specific. Not empty—full. Full of the thing Ren
wasn't saying, the thing that everyone at the table could feel but that only
some of them could identify. Isabelle identified it immediately. Caspar
identified it a half-second later. Hina had probably identified it before Ren
sat down.

"New spikes for the 800 and 1500 are—" Ren stopped. He restarted. "The ones I
need are specialized. Mid-distance plate spikes. Carbon fiber. The model my
coach recommends retails for—" Another stop. He was struggling with the sentence
the way a person struggled with a word in a foreign language—not because he
didn't know it but because saying it required crossing a threshold he didn't
want to cross. "—38,000 yen."

The number sat on the table. 38,000 yen. Roughly 250 euros. Roughly 280 dollars.
The cost of a pair of high-performance running spikes designed for competitive
mid-distance racing—a cost that, for Caspar, was less than the price of the
Skyberries Auntie Park bought weekly, and that, for Isabelle, was less than a
single bottle of the Waldstein Botanicals moisturizer in her school bag, and
that, for Ren, was clearly and unmistakably a problem.

"My parents—" Ren started, and the sentence structure itself communicated
everything: the fact that he was starting a sentence with _my parents_ meant
that the issue was not about preference or inconvenience but about the specific,
non-negotiable mathematics of a household budget that did not include 38,000 yen
for running shoes.

"My parents are covering the meet registration and the travel," Ren said. "My
mom offered to help with the spikes, but—they already—" He stopped again. His
jaw tightened. The athlete's jaw, the competitor's jaw, the jaw of someone who
was accustomed to pushing through physical pain and who was now encountering a
different kind of pain—the pain of needing something and not being able to get
it, the pain of a gap between aspiration and resource that no amount of training
could close.

"It's fine," Ren said. "I'll race in my current ones. I'll tape the ankle."

"You can't tape a structural deficiency," Daiki said.

Everyone looked at Daiki. Daiki, who rarely spoke during emotional moments, who
operated primarily in the register of technical observation, who was now looking
at Ren with an expression of genuine concern filtered through the language of
engineering.

"The lateral stress you're describing is a biomechanical issue caused by
asymmetric cushioning," Daiki continued. "Taping addresses the symptom, not the
cause. If the foam is compressed, the heel drop is altered, and the kinetic
chain from ankle to knee is compromised. Racing 1500 meters on a compromised
kinetic chain at competitive pace—" He adjusted his glasses. "—is a non-trivial
injury risk."

The table absorbed this.

Ren looked at Daiki. "I know."

"Then you can't race in them."

"I don't have another option."

The sentence landed. _I don't have another option._ The sentence of a
fifteen-year-old who had done the math and found it unworkable and who was now,
at a lunch table surrounded by friends, stating a fact that he had no power to
change.

Caspar looked at Isabelle. The look lasted one second. One second of private
communication, encrypted, transmitted in the frequency they had developed over
weeks of shared meals and shared secrets and the shared understanding that the
world they came from and the world their friends lived in were separated by a
gap that was measured in money and that money, for them, was not a problem.

Isabelle received the look. She understood it immediately. The question it
contained was not _should we?_ The question was _how?_

Because the answer to _should we_ was obvious. Of course they should. A friend
needed something. The thing cost money. They had money. The equation was simple.
The execution was not—because the execution required navigating the most
dangerous territory in the geography of their hidden lives: the gap between what
they could do and what they could be seen doing.

They could not hand Ren 38,000 yen. They could not buy him the spikes and
present them as a gift. Either gesture would expose the full scope of their
resources, would transform the careful architecture of "housekeeper and tutor"
into the unmistakable profile of "family with access to limitless wealth," and
would, in doing so, change the nature of every relationship at the table. Money,
once visible, could not be unseen. It would sit between them like a
wall—invisible, intangible, but structurally present in every interaction, every
shared meal, every food-sharing tradition that had been built on the assumption
of approximate equality.

The money had to be invisible. It had to arrive as something other than money.
It had to solve the problem without revealing the solver.

Isabelle ate a piece of her bulgogi. She chewed slowly. She thought.

## III.

Wednesday. Caspar spoke to Fujita.

The conversation happened after the regular photography club meeting, in the art
room, while the other members packed their bags and filtered out. Caspar waited
until the room was empty—just him and Fujita, the late-afternoon light slanting
through the windows, the smell of fixative and old paper that was the art room's
permanent atmospheric signature.

"Fujita-senpai."

Fujita looked up from his camera bag, where he was arranging lenses with the
tender attention of a jeweler sorting gemstones. "Ryu-kun. What's up?"

"The exhibition."

"What about it?"

"I want to expand it."

Fujita's hands stilled on the lens. He looked at Caspar with the cautious,
hopeful expression of a man who had been told something good might be happening
and was waiting for confirmation that it was real. "Expand how?"

"Open it to the whole school. Not just a hallway display—an event. An evening.
We print a larger selection of work, we display it properly, and we sell prints.
Small format—postcard size, maybe A5. Affordable. 500 yen each. The proceeds go
to a school fund."

"A school fund for what?"

"Athletics support. Equipment, travel, registration. For students who need help
covering costs."

Fujita was quiet. He set the lens down. He leaned against the art table and
looked at Caspar with an expression that had moved past hope into something more
specific: the expression of a club president who had been running a five-member
photography club for three years with minimal budget and chronic undersupport
and who was now hearing, from his most talented member, a proposal that would
give the club visibility, purpose, and a connection to the broader school
community.

"You want to use the photography exhibition to fund track shoes," Fujita said.

"Not specifically track shoes. A general support fund. But yes."

"Why?"

Caspar considered the question. The honest answer was: _Because my friend needs
running spikes and can't afford them and I have more money than I will ever be
able to spend and the only way to use that money without destroying the
friendship is to build a system that makes the money invisible._ He did not say
this.

"Because the club should be useful," he said. "Not just to us. To the school.
Photography is about seeing—and part of seeing is seeing what people need."

Fujita looked at him for a long time. The art room was quiet. The afternoon
light moved incrementally across the floor.

"Ryu-kun," Fujita said. "How long have you been thinking about this?"

"Since yesterday."

"You came up with this plan in one day."

"I had help."

"Shin-san?"

"Yes."

Fujita nodded slowly. The nod contained the accumulated understanding of a
third-year student who had watched, over the past four weeks, as a first-year
with a Leica and a stoic face and a girlfriend from the adjacent classroom had
quietly, systematically transformed his struggling club from an afterthought
into something that mattered.

"Let me talk to Tanaka-sensei," Fujita said. Tanaka-sensei was the faculty
advisor for the photography club—a mild-mannered art teacher who had agreed to
supervise the club primarily because no one else had volunteered and who
performed his advisory duties with the gentle disengagement of a man who
believed that students should be left to figure things out on their own. "If he
approves, and if the administration approves the fund, we can do this."

"Thank you, senpai."

"Don't thank me. You're doing all the work." Fujita picked up his lens again. He
looked at it—the curved glass, the coated elements, the precise engineering of
an instrument designed to capture light. "Ryu-kun."

"Yes?"

"Your prints from Yanaka. The ones you submitted for the exhibition."

"What about them?"

"They're the best work this club has ever produced. I've been running this club
for three years. I've seen every print that's come through this darkroom. Yours
are—" He paused. He was choosing his words with the care of someone who
understood that praise, like photography, was most effective when it was
precise. "—yours are the real thing."

Caspar received this the way he received all praise: quietly, with a small nod,
the stoic architecture absorbing the compliment the way a wall absorbed rain.
But Isabelle, who was not in the room, would have seen the micro-expression—the
fractional softening around his eyes, the half-second relaxation of his jaw—that
meant the words had reached him.

"Thank you, senpai," he said.

shoe lockers, through the gate. The late-afternoon light was golden. The green

## IV.

Thursday. Isabelle went to work.

This was the other life—the one that existed after school, after the shoe
lockers and the gate and the walk home, in the gap between being Shin Ji-won of
Sakuragaoka High and being Shin Ji-won of Shin Entertainment, the CEO's daughter
who was officially employed as a part-time personal assistant and translator and
who was, in practice, the lowest-ranking staff member in an organization her
mother owned.

The Shin Entertainment Tokyo office was in Roppongi—a sleek, glass-fronted
building on a side street, six stories, the lobby done in the minimalist
black-and-white aesthetic that was the company's visual signature. Isabelle
arrived at 4:30 PM, changed into her work clothes in the staff bathroom (black
trousers, white blouse, the same uniform worn by every junior staffer,
aggressively unremarkable), clipped her employee ID to her chest pocket—**Shin
Ji-won, Part-Time Assistant, Tokyo Office**—and reported to the third floor.

Her supervisor was a woman named Tanaka Mika—mid-thirties, sharp, with the
compressed energy of someone who processed information at a speed that most
computers would envy. Tanaka-san knew who Isabelle was. Everyone in the building
knew who Isabelle was. The CEO's daughter, working part-time, filing documents
and organizing schedules and fetching water for meeting rooms—the knowledge sat
in the air like a weather system, invisible but felt, influencing every
interaction without being explicitly referenced.

Tanaka-san handled this by treating Isabelle exactly like every other part-time
assistant—which was to say, with brisk professional courtesy and zero
accommodation.

"Shin-san. Meeting room C needs water. Then I need you to collate the Q3 brand
partnership proposals—they're on the shared drive, folder labeled 'Q3-BP.' Print
two copies. Highlight the Korean-language sections; they'll need translation for
the Tokyo team."

"Yes, Tanaka-san."

Isabelle fetched the water. She filled five glasses from the dispenser in the
pantry, placed them on a tray, and carried the tray to Meeting Room C, where
three senior executives were reviewing a presentation about VESPER's upcoming
Asian tour schedule. She set the glasses on the table—one at each place
setting—and left.

The door was closing behind her when she heard one of the executives say: "The
Seoul dates are confirmed. The Tokyo Dome show is pending—we need the venue hold
extended by Friday."

Tokyo Dome. VESPER was playing Tokyo Dome. The knowledge landed in her chest
with a weight that was half excitement and half vertigo—Tokyo Dome was the
venue, the cathedral of Japanese live music, the space where the biggest acts in
Asia performed for 55,000 people under a white domed roof that turned every
concert into an event with the gravitational mass of a small planet.

Her girls were playing Tokyo Dome.

She walked back to her desk. She sat down. She opened the shared drive. She
found the Q3-BP folder. She began collating.

The work was mundane—sorting, printing, highlighting, the administrative labor
that kept an entertainment company running. She performed it with the same
precision she brought to everything: pages aligned, highlights clean, the Korean
sections identified and marked with color-coded tabs that Tanaka-san had not
requested but that would save approximately twelve minutes of review time and
that communicated, in their quiet efficiency, a level of organizational thinking
that was somewhat above the pay grade of a part-time assistant.

At 6 PM, she heard voices in the hallway. Familiar voices. The specific
frequency of post-rehearsal exhaustion expressed in Korean—low, slightly hoarse,
the voices of people who had been singing and dancing for hours and who were now
navigating the transition from performance mode to human mode.

VESPER was in the building.

They were here for a meeting—a schedule review, probably, or a concept briefing
for the tour. They were in the hallway, thirty feet away, and Isabelle could
hear them the way she could hear the opening bars of a song she knew by heart:
Yuna's low murmur, Minji's crisp cadence, Haeun's warmth, Jiwoo's quiet.

She stayed at her desk. She did not go into the hallway. She was, at this
moment, not Ji-won-ah, not unnie, not the fifth person in the practice room. She
was Shin Ji-won, part-time assistant, and the part-time assistant did not leave
her desk to greet the talent.

The voices passed. A door opened and closed—Meeting Room A, down the hall. The
sound of chairs, of water being poured, of a presentation beginning.

Isabelle highlighted a Korean paragraph about a cosmetics partnership with
Waldstein Botanicals.

She paused.

She read the paragraph again. _Waldstein Botanicals—proposed brand ambassador
partnership with VESPER. Phase 1: social media integration. Phase 2:
limited-edition product collaboration. Phase 3: full campaign, Q1 next year._

Waldstein Botanicals. Caspar's mother's company. Partnering with VESPER.
Isabelle's mother's group.

She stared at the page. The corporate architecture—the architecture she and
Caspar had acknowledged on the sidewalk in Minami-Aoyama, the architecture that
wanted them together, that had placed them at the same school, that was playing
a long game with their lives—was here, on this page, in black and white. Their
mothers' companies were forming a partnership. VESPER would promote Waldstein
Botanicals. Waldstein Botanicals would sponsor VESPER. The money would flow. The
brands would merge. The families would draw closer.

And at the center of the Venn diagram, like always, were two fifteen-year-olds
eating Korean food on a bench behind a gymnasium, pretending to be normal.

She highlighted the section. She placed the tab. She continued collating.

At 7 PM, she finished. She left the printouts on Tanaka-san's desk. She changed
out of her work clothes. She walked to the elevator.

The elevator doors opened. Jiwoo was inside.

They looked at each other. The fluorescent light of the elevator was
harsh—unforgiving, institutional, the kind of light that made most people look
tired and that made Jiwoo look like a painting of someone who was tired, which
was different, which was the specific gift of a face that was beautiful under
any conditions.

Jiwoo was in practice clothes—black leggings, an oversized hoodie, her hair in a
loose bun. She was holding a water bottle in one hand and her phone in the
other. She looked exhausted. She looked exactly the way Isabelle remembered her
looking at 2 AM in the practice room—the bones of her face sharper, the shadows
under her eyes deeper, the particular beauty of fatigue that came from doing
something you loved until your body protested.

"언니," Jiwoo said.

"지우야."

The elevator doors closed. They were alone. The car descended.

Jiwoo stepped forward and leaned her head against Isabelle's shoulder.

The gesture was the same. The exact gesture—the wordless lean, the weight of her
head, the contact that said everything words couldn't. Isabelle stood still. She
didn't raise her hand. She didn't speak. She let Jiwoo lean, the way she had let
her lean in the practice room, the way you let someone rest against you when
they trusted you enough to stop holding themselves up.

Five seconds. The elevator descended two floors.

"보고 싶었어요," Jiwoo said. Muffled against Isabelle's shoulder. _I missed
you._

"나도." _Me too._

"캐스퍼 씨 영상 봤어요. 세 번." _I watched Caspar's video. Three times._

"알아. 유나 언니가 말해줬어." _I know. Yuna told me._

"동작이 진심이었어요. 기술보다 마음이 보였어요." _His movements were sincere. I
saw more heart than technique._

Isabelle felt the words settle. _More heart than technique._ From Jiwoo—who was,
at nineteen, already being called a generational dance talent, who evaluated
movement the way Caspar evaluated light, with the precise, unforgiving eye of
someone who understood the difference between performing and meaning it—this was
not a casual observation. It was a diagnosis. Caspar's dancing was sincere. His
fandom was sincere. His heart was visible in his body, the way it was visible in
his photographs, the way it was visible in everything he did once you learned to
look past the stoic surface.

The elevator reached the ground floor. The doors opened.

Jiwoo lifted her head. She looked at Isabelle—the full, direct gaze, the quiet
eyes that saw everything and said little.

"행복해 보여요, 언니," Jiwoo said. _You look happy, unnie._

"그래?" _Do I?_

"네. 스위스 이후로 처음 봐요. 그때 언니 눈에 뭔가 없어졌었는데. 지금은 돌아온 것
같아요." _Yes. For the first time since Switzerland. Back then, something in
your eyes went away. Now it seems like it's come back._

Isabelle looked at Jiwoo. Eighteen—nineteen, technically. The girl who had
leaned her head against Isabelle's shoulder in a practice room and who was now,
in an elevator lobby, telling her that the light in her eyes had returned.

"돌아왔어," Isabelle said. _It came back._

Jiwoo nodded. She pulled her hoodie tighter. She shifted her weight from one
foot to the other—the dancer's weight shift, the redistribution of balance that
preceded movement.

"가야 돼요. 밴이 기다리고 있어요." _I have to go. The van is waiting._

"응. 가." _Yeah. Go._

Jiwoo walked toward the lobby's side exit—the staff entrance, the one that led
to the parking area where the agency's transport vans waited. She was four steps
away when she turned.

"언니."

"응."

"다음에 마들렌 만들어줘요." _Make me madeleines next time._

"약속할게." _I promise._

Jiwoo smiled. Not a big smile—Jiwoo's smiles were never big—but a real one, the
kind that moved from her mouth to her eyes to the slight tilt of her head, the
kind that communicated, in its gentle entirety, the love of someone who
expressed love through proximity and presence and the small, repeated acts of
showing up.

She turned. She walked through the door. The door closed.

Isabelle stood in the lobby for three seconds. Then she walked out into the
Roppongi evening, the neon and the traffic and the city pressing in from all
sides, and she took out her phone.

> **신지원:** 방금 지우 만났어. 회사에서.
>
> _Just ran into Jiwoo. At the office._

> **류태성:** 어땠어?
>
> _How was it?_

> **신지원:** 엘리베이터에서 내 어깨에 기대었어. 아무 말 없이.
>
> _She leaned her head on my shoulder in the elevator. Without saying anything._

> **류태성:** 지우답다.
>
> _That's so Jiwoo._

> **신지원:** 그리고 네 영상 봤대. 세 번. "기술보다 마음이 보였다"래.
>
> _And she said she watched your video. Three times. "More heart than
> technique."_

The reply took thirty seconds. Longer than normal. When it arrived, it was a
single character:

> **류태성:** ...

Then:

> **류태성:** 이자벨.
>
> _Isabelle._

> **신지원:** 응.
>
> _Yeah._

> **류태성:** 나 좀 앉아야 될 것 같아.
>
> _I think I need to sit down._

She laughed. On a sidewalk in Roppongi, surrounded by salarymen and neon and the
infinite, indifferent energy of Tokyo at night, she laughed at her boyfriend who
needed to sit down because the youngest member of his favorite group had seen
his heart in his dancing.

> **신지원:** 앉아. 그리고 심호흡해.
>
> _Sit. And take a deep breath._

> **류태성:** 하고 있어.
>
> _I'm doing it._

> **신지원:** 잘 자, 남자친구.
>
> _Good night, boyfriend._

> **류태성:** 잘 자, 여자친구.
>
> _Good night, girlfriend._

She locked the phone. She walked toward the station. The evening was
warm—genuinely warm now, the Tokyo spring fully surrendered to the approaching
summer, the air thick with the green scent of new leaves and the lingering
floral sweetness of jasmine from someone's rooftop garden.

She walked, and she thought about Ren's spikes and the photography exhibition
and the Waldstein Botanicals partnership proposal on Tanaka-san's desk and
Jiwoo's head on her shoulder and the word _uri_ that contained, in its single
syllable, all the weight and all the lightness of the life she was building.

The station swallowed her. The train carried her home. The city continued its
infinite, indifferent, beautiful rotation around the axis of fourteen million
lives, one of which was hers, and all of which mattered, and none of which knew
the full shape of any of the others.

Which was, she thought, exactly how it should be.

For now.
