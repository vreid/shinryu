# Chapter 5: Daikanyama

---

## I.

Saturday arrived the way Saturdays do in Tokyo in early April—slowly, softly,
the light coming up over the eastern skyline in gradients of pewter and rose,
the city waking in layers: first the trains, then the convenience stores, then
the joggers, then everything else.

Caspar was awake before all of it.

He had not slept well. This was not unusual—the hotel year had restructured his
sleep architecture into something fragmented and shallow, the product of twelve
months of early shifts and late shifts and the particular hypervigilance of
someone who had spent a year being responsible for other people's comfort while
neglecting his own. He'd learned to function on five hours. He'd learned to lie
in the dark with his eyes open and his mind running and to call it rest.

Last night, his mind had run in circles. Not anxious circles—Caspar didn't do
anxiety, or rather, he did it silently, internally, in a compartment that didn't
connect to his external behavior. These were _planning_ circles. What to wear.
Which route to walk. Which camera to bring (the M6, obviously; this was not a
question but a confirmation). Whether to arrive early or on time. What to say
when they ran out of the safe, careful topics they'd been trading all week and
hit the bedrock of the thing they hadn't discussed.

He got up at 6. He ran. Four kilometers, same route, same river path, same
moss-covered wall glowing green in the early light. When he returned, Auntie
Park was in the kitchen with the focused intensity of someone preparing for an
operation.

"Shower," she said, without looking at him. "Then come here."

He showered. He dried his hair. He walked into his bedroom and found, laid out
on the bed with the care of a museum installation, an outfit.

Dark navy chore jacket—unlined cotton twill, the fabric soft from washing, the
kind of garment that looked effortless and cost a quiet fortune. He recognized
it: Margaret Howell, from the Tokyo flagship, purchased by his mother during her
last visit. Beneath it, a white Oxford shirt—not the school blouse but a proper
Oxford, with a button-down collar and a weave dense enough to drape without
wrinkling. Dark charcoal trousers, slim-cut, cuffed at the ankle. White leather
sneakers that had been cleaned overnight—he could see the faint dampness where
Auntie Park had wiped the soles.

He stood in his towel and looked at the outfit.

"Auntie," he called.

"What."

"I can dress myself."

"You can dress yourself for school. This is not school." She appeared in the
doorway, arms crossed, wearing an expression that communicated an entire
philosophy of presentation. "This is Saturday. In Daikanyama. With a girl."

"It's not a—"

"Wear the jacket. Tuck the shirt in. Leave the top button open. Your hair—" She
studied him, tilting her head. "Your hair is fine if you push it back. Don't let
it fall in your face. You have a good face. People should see it."

"Auntie."

"What."

"Thank you."

Her expression softened—a tiny loosening around the mouth, a half-degree change
in the angle of her eyebrows. The Auntie Park equivalent of a bear hug. "Don't
be late. And take the camera. She told you to bring the camera."

"How do you know she told me to—"

"Get dressed."

He got dressed.

The outfit was, he had to admit, exactly right. The jacket sat on his shoulders
with the relaxed precision of a garment that had been chosen by someone who
understood that the best menswear didn't announce itself—it simply fit, the way
a well-composed photograph simply worked, the elements in balance, the tension
resolved. The white shirt against his skin was clean and bright, and the open
collar exposed the line of his throat in a way that he didn't think about
because thinking about it would mean thinking about Isabelle looking at it and
that was a corridor his mind was not currently equipped to walk down.

He pushed his hair back. It fell slightly forward. He pushed it back again.

He put on the sneakers. He slipped the Leica M6 into the soft cloth sleeve and
placed it in the interior pocket of the jacket, where it sat against his ribs—a
familiar weight, the camera's body warm against his through the layers of cloth.

He checked the film. Portra 400. Color. Thirty-six exposures. He'd loaded it
last night, sitting on his bed, winding the leader onto the take-up spool by
feel, the mechanical click of the sprocket engaging a sound that never failed to
settle something in his chest.

Color film. He almost never shot color. He'd shot Tri-X 400—black and white,
high contrast, the film of photojournalists and street photographers—for almost
the entire hotel year, because black and white was honest and austere and didn't
lie about what it was. Color was different. Color was emotional. Color was the
warmth of skin in afternoon light and the specific green of moss on stone and
the way honey-blonde hair caught the sun and turned into something that made
your hands forget what they were doing.

He'd loaded color film because today was a color day.

He didn't examine this decision further.

## II.

Across the city, Isabelle was standing in front of her bathroom mirror,
performing skincare.

This was a ritual. Not a routine—a _ritual_, with the solemnity and the
precision and the non-negotiable sequence of steps that distinguished it from
mere habit. Eight products, applied in a specific order, at specific intervals,
with specific techniques that Helena Waldstein's R&D team had developed and that
Isabelle had been taught by a woman in a lab coat in the Waldstein Botanicals
Seoul research facility who had held Isabelle's face in both hands, turned it
toward the light, and said, in Korean, "You have your mother's bone structure
and your father's skin. We can work with this."

Step one: oil cleanser. A clear, viscous liquid that smelled faintly of camellia
and dissolved overnight sebum into a milky emulsion. She massaged it in
circles—thirty seconds on the forehead, thirty on each cheek, fifteen on the
chin, ten on the nose. Rinse.

Step two: water cleanser. A low-pH gel that foamed into a cream. She worked it
across her face with her fingertips, the lather fine and dense, the kind of foam
that felt like touching a cloud and probably cost roughly the same per gram.

Step three: toner. Three layers, patted in with her palms, each layer absorbed
before the next was applied. The toner was the base—the hydration foundation
that everything else built on. Helena called it "the canvas." The texture was
like touching liquid silk.

Step four: essence. Step five: serum. Step six: eye cream—a separate product,
applied with the ring finger in a tapping motion along the orbital bone, never
dragged, because the skin around the eyes was thinner than tissue paper and
responded to force the way tissue paper responded to force. Step seven:
moisturizer. Step eight: sunscreen—SPF 50, PA++++, a formula that managed to
provide full UV protection without the chalky white cast that made most
sunscreens look like you'd dipped your face in flour.

She finished. She looked at herself in the mirror.

The face that looked back was calm, luminous, and almost unreasonably
symmetrical. The skincare had done what skincare always did for her—evened the
texture, smoothed the tone, created the water-light glow that Sora had
identified as possible photosynthesis. But beneath the surface, in the muscles
of her jaw and the set of her shoulders, there was a tension that no amount of
camellia oil could dissolve.

She was nervous.

This was unusual. Isabelle van Rijn did not, as a rule, get nervous. She had
trained with four of the most intimidating women in Asian entertainment. She had
performed choreography in front of industry executives whose opinions could end
careers. She had walked into a Tokyo public high school on her first day and
maintained complete composure while every head in the courtyard turned to stare
at her. She was, by any reasonable metric, a person who had stress-tested her
own poise and found it structurally sound.

But today she was meeting Caspar. Not in school. Not in uniform. Not with a bell
scheduled to end the interaction after fifty minutes. Today she was meeting him
in the open, in civilian clothes, with no structure and no time limit and no
excuse to leave when the conversation got too close to the things they'd been
circling all week.

She went to her bedroom. The cream cashmere sweater was on the bed—Auntie Choi's
recommendation, laid out with the same curatorial precision that Auntie Park had
applied to Caspar's outfit five kilometers away. Isabelle picked it up. The
cashmere was extraordinary: Loro Piana, baby cashmere, the fibers so fine that
the fabric felt like holding warm air. It was the color of heavy cream, with a
neckline that sat just below the collarbone and sleeves that fell to
mid-forearm, revealing the clean lines of her wrists and hands.

She paired it with high-waisted straight-leg jeans—Japanese selvedge denim, raw
and unwashed, the indigo deep and even—and white leather sneakers that were,
except for the small embossed logo on the heel (which was not visible at normal
social distances), indistinguishable from the ones sold at any department store
in Shibuya.

She dried her hair. It fell in its natural waves—heavy, honey-colored, reaching
past her shoulder blades. She considered tying it back. She decided against it.
She told herself this decision had nothing to do with the fact that Caspar had
once, in Switzerland, said—without looking at her, while adjusting the aperture
on his camera—that her hair "did something in the light" that he couldn't
describe and didn't want to, because describing it would ruin it.

She told herself this and did not believe herself for a single second.

Makeup: minimal. Concealer where needed (one small spot near her left jaw, a
minor blemish that the skincare would resolve by Monday but that she was not
willing to present to Caspar on a Saturday). A single coat of mascara—brown, not
black, because black looked harsh in daylight and brown enhanced the hazel-green
of her eyes without announcing that enhancement was happening. Lip balm. That
was it.

She stood in the full-length mirror. The cream sweater glowed against her skin.
The jeans fit the way good denim fit—structured but not stiff, the fabric
holding her frame with the quiet confidence of a garment that had been made by
people who understood bodies. Her posture was straight, her shoulders open, her
weight centered.

She looked like Isabelle van Rijn. Not Shin Ji-won, the careful, filtered
version who navigated Sakuragaoka High with measured fluency and deliberate
ordinariness. Isabelle. The real one. The one who had grown up in a house in
Wassenaar with eighteen-foot ceilings and a father who carried her on his
shoulders and a mother who loved her from behind a desk. The one who had danced
in Seoul and baked in Paris and kissed—tried to kiss—a boy on a Swiss terrace
and had spent a year learning to carry the weight of the refusal.

She picked up her bag. Small, leather, crossbody—a Celine that her mother had
sent last Christmas, the logo subtle enough to be invisible to anyone who wasn't
looking for it. Inside: phone, wallet, lip balm, the cotton pouch of skincare
(because you never knew), a small notebook, a pen.

She walked to the genkan. Her shoes were on the rack—the white sneakers, cleaned
by Auntie Choi. She slipped them on.

The apartment was empty. Auntie Choi had left for Shin-Ōkubo an hour ago.
Hayashi-san was in his room, working. The silence was total—just the distant hum
of the building's ventilation system and the faint, rhythmic pulse of Tokyo
existing fourteen stories below.

Isabelle opened the front door.

She stepped into the hallway. The elevator was waiting. She pressed the button
for the lobby.

The doors closed. She watched herself in the elevator's mirrored wall—a tall
girl in cream cashmere and raw denim, her hair catching the overhead light, her
expression composed, her hands slightly too still at her sides.

She breathed.

_It's just coffee,_ she thought. _It's just a café. It's just Caspar._

The elevator reached the lobby. The doors opened.

She walked out into the Saturday afternoon.

## III.

Daikanyama in April was a neighborhood that seemed to have been designed by
someone who understood the relationship between architecture and mood.

The streets were narrow, tree-lined, shaded by a canopy of zelkova and cherry
that filtered the afternoon light into a green-gold dapple. The buildings were
low—two, three stories, set back from the sidewalk behind hedges and bamboo
screens and the occasional stone wall covered in climbing hydrangea not yet in
bloom. The shops were the kind that didn't need signs: a ceramics studio with a
single vase in the window, a bookshop with a hand-lettered name on the glass, a
florist whose arrangements spilled onto the sidewalk in controlled riots of
color that somehow looked effortless.

It was the kind of neighborhood that rich people built for themselves when they
wanted to feel like they weren't rich—when they wanted the textures and the
quiet and the carefully curated imperfection of a life that looked simple but
cost a fortune to maintain. Isabelle recognized this. She had grown up in it, in
Wassenaar, where the gardens were "natural" and the houses were "modest" and
both of these words meant the opposite of what they said.

The café was on a side street, half a block from the main road. It was called
**Sarutahiko Coffee**—a specialty shop with a small storefront, a wooden counter
visible through the glass, and the particular aromatic signature of freshly
ground single-origin beans that hit Isabelle from six meters away and made her
mouth water.

Caspar was already there.

He was standing outside, leaning against the wall beside the door with his hands
in the pockets of his navy jacket, one foot flat against the wall behind him,
his posture communicating the studied casualness of someone who had arrived
early and was pretending he hadn't. His hair was pushed back—slightly, not
aggressively, just enough to reveal the full plane of his forehead and the sharp
line of his brow. The white Oxford shirt was open at the collar. The Leica was
not visible, but Isabelle knew it was there—she could see the faint asymmetry in
the jacket's drape where the camera sat in the interior pocket, a small bulge
over his left ribs.

He saw her.

The seeing was immediate—not a scan, not a search, but a direct, instantaneous
lock, the way a compass needle finds north. His eyes found her face from thirty
meters away and stayed there, tracking her approach, and Isabelle could feel the
quality of his attention on her skin like sunlight.

She walked toward him. The Daikanyama sidewalk was uneven in places—old
flagstone, slightly buckled by tree roots—and she navigated it without looking
down, her dancer's spatial awareness mapping the surface through the soles of
her sneakers while her eyes held his.

Twenty meters. Ten. Five.

She stopped in front of him.

They looked at each other.

He was—and she allowed herself, for the first time in a week, to fully register
this—beautiful. Not handsome, which was a word that implied assessment,
evaluation, the conscious act of judging someone's features against a standard.
Beautiful, which was something else—something involuntary, something that
bypassed the evaluative brain entirely and landed in the body, in the chest, in
the specific cluster of nerve endings that responded to light and symmetry and
the particular arrangement of a face that you had been looking at since you were
eleven years old and that had never, not once, not even during the worst of the
silence, stopped being the face you looked for in every room.

His pale blue eyes were steady. His jaw was set. His lips were slightly parted—a
millimeter, maybe two, the resting state of a mouth that was deciding what to
say.

"Hi," she said.

"Hi."

A pause. The word _hi_ had been their opening move all week—the same greeting,
the same monosyllable, deployed in hallways and on benches and at the start of
LINE conversations, a small, consistent ritual that was becoming, through
repetition, something more than casual. It was a touchstone. A confirmation.
_Hi: I am here. I see you. We are in the same place again and I am not going to
pretend otherwise._

"You're early," she said.

"You're on time."

"I'm always on time."

"I know. That's why I'm early."

She looked at him. He looked at her. Something passed between them—not words,
not yet, but a communication that operated below language, in the register of
faces and bodies and the way two people who knew each other deeply could
exchange entire paragraphs in the space between one breath and the next.

"Should we go in?" she said.

"After you."

He held the door. She walked through. The interior of the café was small—eight
seats at the counter, four two-top tables along the wall, the space dominated by
a La Marzocca espresso machine and a pour-over bar where a barista in a leather
apron was performing what appeared to be a highly ritualized extraction
involving a gooseneck kettle and a ceramic dripper. The light inside was warm,
amber, coming through the front windows at a low angle and catching the steam
that rose from the machine in slow, spiraling columns.

They sat at a table by the window. Across from each other. The table was
small—barely sixty centimeters square—and their knees were close enough that
Isabelle could feel the warmth from his leg without contact.

A menu appeared. Isabelle studied it—single-origin pour-overs listed by farm
name and processing method, espresso drinks, a small selection of pastries. She
ordered a café latte with oat milk. Caspar ordered a pour-over—an Ethiopian
natural process from a farm in Yirgacheffe.

"You drink pour-over now?" she said, after the barista had taken their order and
retreated to the machine.

"The hotel."

"The hotel had pour-over?"

"The head barista was from Melbourne. She was very—" He paused, selecting.
"—particular."

"Particular how?"

"She believed that espresso was 'the fast food of coffee.' She said this to me
on my first day while I was carrying a tray of cappuccinos to the terrace. She
made me throw them out and start over with a V60."

Isabelle processed this image: Caspar, in a hotel uniform, carrying a tray
through a service corridor, being stopped by an Australian barista who made him
re-brew six cups of coffee using a method he'd never heard of. She could see it
perfectly—his face, the controlled blankness, the way he would have received the
instruction without protest and then executed it with the precision of someone
who, once shown the correct way to do something, would never do it incorrectly
again.

"Did she teach you?"

"She taught me extraction times, water temperature, grind size, and pour
technique. She was—" Another pause. "—the best teacher I had at the hotel. And
the least impressed by anyone."

"Including you?"

"Especially me. She called me 'the tall quiet one.'"

"That's accurate."

"She said I had 'the energy of a cathedral.'"

Isabelle looked at him. "A cathedral."

"Her words. I think she meant it as a compliment. Cathedrals are—" He moved his
hand, a small gesture, searching. "—cold. But intentional."

"And beautiful."

He looked at her. The word sat between them on the small table, next to the
sugar dispenser and the folded napkins. _Beautiful._ She had said it casually,
as an addendum, a completion of his thought—_cathedrals are cold but intentional
and beautiful_—but the word had landed on him, she could see it, the way his
eyes changed when something reached him, a barely perceptible dilation, the
aperture opening.

"Yes," he said. "And beautiful."

The coffee arrived. Isabelle's latte in a wide ceramic cup, the foam poured in a
single clean rosetta. Caspar's pour-over in a slim glass, the liquid amber and
translucent, the surface catching the window light.

He picked up the glass. He held it to the light, examining the clarity the way a
vintner examined wine. Then he drank—a small sip, the liquid held in his mouth
for a moment before swallowing. His eyes narrowed fractionally. He nodded.

"Good?" Isabelle asked.

"Very good. Bright. Floral. There's a stone-fruit note in the finish—apricot,
maybe."

"Apricot." She smiled. "Like the jam."

"Like the jam." He set the glass down. "You noticed that too."

"The Wachauer Marillenmarmelade. Your mother sent it. Auntie Choi gave it to me
on the first morning."

"My mother sends things. It's her—" He paused. "—communication style. She can't
be here, so she sends pieces of herself. The jam. The shampoo. The toaster."

"The toaster?"

"You have one too. In your apartment. German-made, no brand, brushed steel. My
mother's supplier."

Isabelle stared at him. "How do you know what toaster I have?"

"I don't. I know what toaster my mother would choose. And since both apartments
were outfitted by her Tokyo office, I'm assuming they're identical."

"They are. I looked at it on the first morning and thought—" She stopped. She
picked up her latte. She drank. The foam was sweet and dense, the espresso
underneath bitter and bright, the combination exactly right.

"You thought what?"

"I thought it was the most German toaster I'd ever seen."

The corner of his mouth moved. Not a smile—not yet—but the tectonic precursor,
the shift that preceded the event. This was happening more frequently now,
Isabelle noticed. Over the course of the week, the distance between _stoic
resting face_ and _almost-smile_ had been shortening, the threshold lowering,
the expression arriving faster and lingering longer. She was tracking this the
way a seismologist tracked tremors—noting the frequency, the magnitude, the
intervals, building a dataset from which she could eventually predict the real
thing.

"My mother designed the toaster," Caspar said.

"She _designed_ it?"

"She commissioned it. From an industrial designer in Darmstadt. She wanted a
toaster that matched the aesthetic language of the Waldstein brand—minimal,
functional, no visible branding, high-quality materials. The designer made
twelve prototypes. My mother rejected eleven."

"What was wrong with the eleven?"

"The seventh one had a logo. The ninth one had a visible seam. The third one—"
He stopped, and this time the almost-smile broke through, a real expression,
small but unmistakable, reshaping the lower half of his face in a way that made
Isabelle's chest do something structural. "The third one was too shiny. She said
it looked 'aspirational.' She wanted it to look 'inevitable.'"

"'Inevitable.'"

"Her word. She uses it about everything. A good product should feel
inevitable—like it couldn't have been any other way. Like it exists because it
has to, not because someone decided to make it."

Isabelle thought about this. She thought about Helena Waldstein—a woman she'd
met many times, at family dinners and corporate events and the occasional
weekend in Munich, a woman whose presence communicated a specific, unwavering
aesthetic conviction the way a lighthouse communicated light. Helena Waldstein
did not enter rooms. She _clarified_ them. She made everything around her look
more intentional simply by being in proximity to it.

"My mother is the opposite," Isabelle said. "She doesn't care about the object.
She cares about the story."

"The story?"

"She says a product—a song, an artist, a brand—is only as good as the story
people tell about it. The object is just the anchor. The story is the value."
Isabelle traced the rim of her cup with her fingertip. "That's how she runs the
agency. She doesn't manage artists. She manages narratives. Every debut, every
comeback, every public appearance is a chapter in a story she's writing."

"VESPER," Caspar said.

The name dropped into the conversation like a stone into still water. Isabelle
felt the ripple move through her—a tightening in her abdomen, a sharpening of
attention, the reflexive activation of the filter that she kept between her real
life and her school life.

"VESPER is her biggest project," Isabelle said, carefully. "They debuted
recently."

"I know."

"Sora is obsessed with them."

"I know."

"Do you—" She stopped. She looked at him. He was holding his pour-over glass,
looking into it, and there was something in his expression—a very subtle
tension, a guardedness, the look of someone who was standing near a door he
wasn't ready to open.

"Do I what?"

"Do you follow them? VESPER?"

The question was a test. She knew it was a test. She was deploying it with the
same precision she deployed the language filter—a calibrated probe, designed to
reveal exactly as much as the response warranted.

Caspar drank his coffee. He set the glass down. He looked at her with his pale
blue eyes and said, with the measured cadence of a diplomat navigating a
minefield:

"I've heard their music."

"And?"

"It's good."

"Good."

"The choreography is strong. The visual concept is cohesive. The vocals—" He
stopped. He was doing the thing he did when he was editing his own speech in
real time, cutting away the words that would reveal too much. "The vocals are
competent."

"Competent." Isabelle watched him. She could see the effort—the carefully
constructed nonchalance, the modulated vocabulary, the way he was describing a
group he clearly knew more about than he was willing to show. It was the same
energy she brought to discussing them with Sora—the same filter, the same
performance of casual familiarity. They were both lying about VESPER, she
realized. Both of them, for different reasons, from different angles, hiding
different truths behind the same wall of studied indifference.

She decided not to push. Not today. Today was for other things.

"My mother built them from auditions," she said, steering the conversation to
safer ground—ground that was still personal, still revealing, but that didn't
require either of them to admit anything they weren't ready for. "She spent
three years scouting and training. Four members, from hundreds of candidates.
She chose each one personally."

"Your mother has good instincts."

"My mother has terrifying instincts. She can watch a thirteen-year-old sing for
thirty seconds and know whether they'll be a star or not. She says it's not
about talent. It's about—" She searched for the word. "—endurance. The ability
to be looked at every day for years and not break."

Caspar was quiet. He looked at her—not at her face, but at her, the full person,
with the kind of attention that photographers brought to subjects they were
trying to understand. She could feel him reading her—the way she held her cup,
the way she sat, the slight forward lean of someone who was sharing something
that mattered and watching for the impact.

"You've been looked at," he said. Quietly. Not a question.

She met his eyes. "What do you mean?"

"Your composure. The way you walk into a room. The way you can control exactly
what your face shows and when. You've been trained to be looked at. Not by
ballet. Something else."

The air between them shifted. She could feel it—the same atmospheric thickening
she'd felt on the bench, the same charge, the same sense of proximity to a truth
that would change the shape of everything.

"Caspar—"

"You don't have to tell me."

She stopped. He was looking at her with an expression she knew—the one he wore
when he was giving someone space, when he was stepping back from a door he'd
identified but wasn't going to open without invitation. The hotel face. The face
of a boy who had learned, through a year of forced invisibility, that the most
respectful thing you could do for another person was to let them choose when to
be seen.

She looked down at her coffee. The rosetta had dissolved into the milk, the
pattern gone, the surface a uniform pale brown.

"Not today," she said.

"Okay."

"But soon."

"Okay."

She looked up. He was still watching her, and the expression on his face was not
curiosity or frustration or impatience. It was something simpler and more
devastating than all of those: it was trust. He trusted her to tell him when she
was ready. He would wait. He had been waiting for a year. He would wait longer.

She wanted, suddenly and with a force that surprised her, to reach across the
sixty-centimeter table and touch his hand. Just his hand. Just the backs of his
fingers, resting beside his glass, the knuckles slightly reddened from—what? The
cold? The running? The year of hauling luggage? She wanted to touch them and
feel the texture of them and know that this—this conversation, this café, this
slow and careful reconstruction of the space between them—was real and not a
projection, not a hope, not a dream she was having in the Minami-Aoyama
apartment while the German toaster hummed in the kitchen.

She didn't touch his hand.

She drank her coffee.

"Tell me about the hotel," she said.

## IV.

He told her.

Not all of it—not the worst of it, not the 2 AM shifts or the guest who had
thrown a shoe at him or the night he'd stood in the staff locker room with his
hands shaking and his jaw clenched so tight his molars ached, holding himself
together through sheer mechanical force because there was no one to call and
nothing to do and the only option was to put on a clean shirt and go back out.
He kept those stories locked. They belonged to a version of him that he was
still integrating, still learning to carry, the way you learned to carry a
scar—not by hiding it, but by understanding the wound that made it.

He told her the other parts. The parts that had light.

He told her about Gerhard, the fitness instructor, the retired ski racer who had
taken one look at Caspar's untrained frame and said, in Austrian-accented
German, _"You are built like a question mark. We will make you into an
exclamation point."_ About the morning stretches on the terrace overlooking the
valley, the mountains pink with alpenglow, Gerhard counting reps in a voice that
could have narrated a nature documentary. About the way his body had
changed—slowly, incrementally, the shoulders broadening, the core stabilizing,
the endurance building—until he could carry two thirty-kilo suitcases up four
flights of stairs without his heart rate rising above 120.

He told her about Maggie, the Australian barista, who had taught him to taste
coffee the way he'd learned to see light—not as a single impression but as a
composition of elements: acidity, sweetness, body, finish, the way each variable
interacted with the others to create something greater than its parts. "She said
coffee was 'the closest thing to photography in liquid form.' I think she was
half-joking. But I understood what she meant."

He told her about the photography. The way it had started—not as an interest but
as a survival mechanism, a way to process the experience of being invisible.
"When you're a porter, people don't look at you. They look through you. You're
furniture. You're the door. You're the thing that carries the bag." He turned
his glass on the table, a slow rotation, the light moving through the liquid.
"At first, it was terrible. I couldn't—I wasn't used to not being seen. At Le
Parcours, I was always seen. I was a Waldstein. I was a Ryu. I was a name before
I was a person. And then, at the hotel, I was nothing. I was 'you there.' I was
'the boy.'"

He paused. Isabelle was listening. She was listening the way she always
listened—with her whole body, leaned slightly forward, her eyes on his face, her
hands wrapped around her cup, her breathing slowed to match his rhythm. She had
always been the best listener he'd ever known. It was her gift—the ability to
create, through sheer quality of attention, a space where the other person felt
safe enough to be honest. The space she'd described in their LINE conversation:
_People want to tell me their stories. I just listen and they talk._

He was talking.

"The camera changed it," he said. "When I started carrying it—just a small
point-and-shoot at first, then the Leica—the invisibility stopped being
something that happened to me and became something I chose. I wasn't being
ignored. I was observing. I was outside the frame, looking in, deciding what
mattered enough to capture."

He looked at her. "You gave me that word. Observing. You said it to me once, in
Switzerland. You said I wasn't quiet—I was observing. You said most people
listen to respond. I listen to understand. You were twelve."

"I remember."

"That stayed with me. At the hotel, when I was standing in the corridor at
midnight, ironing tablecloths, I would think about that. _I'm not invisible. I'm
observing._ It was—" He stopped. He looked down at his hands. "It was the thing
that kept me from losing myself."

The café was quiet. The barista was cleaning the pour-over station, the soft
clink of glass against ceramic the only sound. The afternoon light had
shifted—deeper now, more amber, the shadows on the table longer and softer.

Isabelle set her cup down. She looked at him with an expression he hadn't seen
in a year—an expression that belonged to the before, to Switzerland, to the
version of them that had existed on the other side of the terrace. It was
unguarded. Open. The filter down, the composure loosened, her face showing
exactly what she felt without curation or control.

"I'm sorry," she said.

"For what?"

"For not being there. For the year. For all of it."

"You don't need to—"

"I do." Her voice was quiet but certain, the certainty of someone who had
rehearsed this moment—not the words, but the feeling, the decision to say them.
"I spent a year in Seoul being busy. Training, working, filling every minute so
I didn't have to think about—" She paused. "About what happened."

The terrace. Switzerland. The unspoken thing, moving closer now, rising to the
surface like an image in a developing tray.

"You turned away," she said.

The words were simple. Three words. Subject, verb, adverb. But they contained
the entire event: the cold evening, the stone terrace, the Alps turning pink,
her face lifting toward his, the angle of approach, the moment of
proximity—close enough to feel breath, close enough to count eyelashes—and then
his head turning, a small rotation, five degrees, maybe ten, enough to redirect
her trajectory from his mouth to the cold air beside his jaw.

He turned away.

"Yes," he said.

"Why?"

He looked at her. The question was not accusatory. It wasn't angry. It was the
question of someone who had carried a weight for a year and needed, finally, to
set it down.

"Because I wanted to," he said.

She blinked. "You wanted to turn away?"

"No." He set both hands flat on the table. He looked at them—his hands, the
knuckles slightly rough, the fingers long and steady, the hands of a
photographer and a porter and a fifteen-year-old boy who was trying, with
everything he had, to say something true. "I wanted to kiss you. I wanted to so
badly that it—I couldn't—" He closed his eyes. Opened them. "I was fourteen. You
were fourteen. And the wanting was so much bigger than I was. I didn't know what
to do with it. I didn't know how to—hold it. So I turned away. Because the only
thing I could think of that was worse than not kissing you was kissing you and
not being—enough. Not being ready. Not being someone who deserved to be your
first kiss."

The café was perfectly still. The barista had stopped cleaning. The light held
its angle, the shadows held their length, and the air between them held its
charge.

Isabelle was looking at him with an expression he couldn't read. Her eyes were
bright—brighter than usual, the hazel-green deepened by something that might
have been emotion or might have been the angle of the light, and he couldn't
tell the difference and it didn't matter because both possibilities meant the
same thing.

"Caspar," she said. Her voice was barely above a whisper. "You were enough."

"I wasn't—"

"You were enough." Firmer now. The quiet certainty of a girl who had spent a
year processing a rejection and arriving, through all the evidence available to
her, at a conclusion. "You were always enough. The problem wasn't you. The
problem was timing."

"Timing."

"We were fourteen. We were in a Swiss boarding school. We were about to be sent
to different countries for a year. The timing was—" She let out a breath.
"—catastrophic."

"And now?"

The question hung. The most important question. The one that everything—the LINE
conversations, the benches, the strawberries, the madeleines, the
forty-centimeter gaps and the careful reconstructions and the nightly _good
night, Isabelle, good night, Caspar_—had been building toward.

Isabelle looked at him. She looked at him for a long time—five seconds, ten, an
eternity compressed into the space between two heartbeats. Her expression was
open, unfiltered, the most vulnerable he had seen her since the terrace.

"Now," she said, "I want to find out."

## V.

They left the café at 3:45.

The afternoon had settled into the particular golden quality that Tokyo achieved
in early spring—the light low and warm, the shadows long, the air carrying the
faint sweetness of the last cherry blossoms mixing with something greener and
newer, the first stirrings of the leaves that would replace them.

They walked. Not toward anything—away from the café, into the Daikanyama side
streets, through the neighborhood's quiet grid of boutiques and residences and
hidden gardens. They walked side by side, close but not touching, their
footsteps finding a shared rhythm on the flagstone sidewalk.

Caspar took out his camera.

The Leica M6 emerged from his jacket pocket in a single practiced motion—cloth
sleeve off, lens cap removed, the camera raised to his eye in one continuous
gesture that was as natural to him as breathing. He didn't announce it. He
didn't ask permission. The camera was simply there, an extension of his body,
and through it, he began to see.

A bicycle parked against a stone wall, its basket holding a single paper bag,
the shadow of the wheel thrown across the flagstones in a perfect ellipse.

_Click._

A cat—gray, long-haired, sitting on a windowsill two stories up, its eyes
half-closed, its posture suggesting it had been sitting there since before the
building existed.

_Click._

A florist's bucket on the sidewalk, three bundles of ranunculus—white, peach,
deep coral—the petals layered so densely they looked like crumpled tissue paper,
the stems wrapped in brown kraft paper that was slowly darkening with water.

_Click._

Isabelle watched him work. She had seen him photograph before, in
Switzerland—quick, furtive shots taken between classes, the camera appearing and
disappearing from his bag like a secret. But this was different. The hotel year
had changed not just his body but his eye. His movements were more deliberate
now, more patient. He didn't snap. He _waited_—for the light to shift, for the
composition to resolve, for the moment when the elements in the frame aligned
and the image became, in Helena Waldstein's word, _inevitable._

"You're better," she said, after the sixth or seventh frame.

He lowered the camera. "Better?"

"At this. At seeing. You've always been good at it, but now you're—" She
searched for the word. In Dutch, there was a term: _doordacht._ Thoughtful in
the sense of having been thought through completely, every element considered,
nothing left to chance. She couldn't find the Japanese equivalent. "—more
intentional."

He looked at her. The camera was in his hands, at chest level, the lens pointed
slightly upward. She could see herself reflected in the front element—a tiny,
distorted Isabelle, inverted in the curved glass.

"The hotel taught me to wait," he said. "As a porter, you spend most of your
time waiting. Waiting for guests, waiting for orders, waiting for the shift to
end. Most people hate waiting. I learned to use it. To watch while I waited. To
see what was there instead of thinking about what I wanted to be there."

"That sounds like photography."

"It is photography. It's also—" He paused. He raised the camera slightly. Not to
his eye—just a few centimeters, a reflexive gesture, the way a writer might
touch a pen. "—how I got through the year without you."

The sentence landed quietly. No emphasis, no weight, no particular inflection.
He said it the way he said everything—with the stripped-down directness of
someone who had learned that saying fewer words more precisely was better than
saying more words less carefully. And the effect was proportional to the
restraint: the sentence hit Isabelle in the center of her chest and expanded.

_The year without you._

Not: the year apart. Not: the year we didn't talk. _The year without you._ As if
she were an element of his world—like light, like air, like the chemical trays
in his darkroom—whose absence had to be survived, worked around, compensated
for.

She stopped walking. He stopped beside her. They were on a quiet street—no cars,
no pedestrians, just the two of them and a row of cherry trees whose petals were
falling in the late-afternoon light, drifting across the sidewalk in slow
spirals.

"Take my picture," she said.

He looked at her. His expression didn't change—the stoic architecture held—but
something moved behind his eyes, a rapid sequence of thoughts that she could
track even if she couldn't read them: surprise, consideration, want, restraint,
decision.

"You want me to take your picture."

"Yes."

"Why?"

"Because you see things that other people don't. And I want to know what you see
when you look at me."

The words were precise. She had chosen them the way she chose ingredients—each
one weighed, measured, essential to the final product. She stood on the sidewalk
in her cream cashmere sweater with her hair in the late-afternoon light and she
asked the boy who had turned away from her kiss to show her, through the only
language he fully trusted, what she looked like to him.

Caspar raised the camera.

He didn't ask her to pose. He didn't tell her to look at the lens. He lifted the
Leica to his eye, and through the rangefinder's window, he found her—not the
center of the frame but slightly to the left, off-axis, the cherry trees behind
her soft and out of focus, the light catching her hair and her jaw and the
collar of the sweater. She was looking at him. Not at the camera—at him, the
person behind it, the person she'd known for four years and missed for one and
was now, slowly, carefully, letting back in.

He focused. The rangefinder's split-image aligned—two halves of her face
converging, merging, becoming one.

He pressed the shutter.

_Click._

The sound was small and precise, a mechanical whisper, the mirror-less Leica
making none of the slapping noise of a reflex camera. Just the shutter curtain
opening and closing, a fraction of a second, 1/125th, the light passing through
the lens and striking the film and recording, in silver halide crystals, the
exact arrangement of photons that had bounced off Isabelle van Rijn's face at
4:07 PM on a Saturday in April in Daikanyama.

He lowered the camera.

She was still looking at him.

"하나 더." _One more._

He raised the camera again. This time she turned—not away from him, but ninety
degrees, presenting her profile to the lens. She looked down the street, at the
cherry trees, at the falling petals, her expression settling into something
private and unperformed, the face she wore when she didn't know she was being
watched.

Except she did know. And the knowing was the point—the deliberate gift of an
unguarded moment, offered to the one person she trusted to receive it.

_Click._

He lowered the camera. He wound the film advance—a single, smooth throw of the
lever, the satisfying mechanical resistance of the sprocket engaging the next
frame.

"Thank you," he said.

"Thank you for what?"

"For letting me."

She turned back to face him. They were standing close—closer than the bench,
closer than the café table, close enough that she could see the individual
threads of the Oxford shirt's weave and the tiny scar on his left earlobe where
he'd caught it on a luggage rack at the hotel (she didn't know this yet, she
would learn it later, she would touch it with her fingertip and ask and he would
tell her).

"Caspar."

"Yeah."

"Next Saturday. Same time."

"Same place?"

"Different place. I'll choose."

"Okay."

"And bring the pictures. When you develop them. I want to see."

"You'll see them."

She looked at him for three more seconds. Then she turned and began walking—back
toward Aoyama, back toward the apartment, back toward the orderly world of
Auntie Choi and skincare routines and the quiet space where she processed the
things that happened to her.

But this time, after ten steps, she turned around.

"캐스퍼."

He was still standing where she'd left him, the camera in his hands, the cherry
blossoms falling around him in the golden light.

"응." _Yeah._

"스위스에서 그때," she said. _On the terrace in Switzerland._ "네가 고개 돌리기
전에 반 초 정도. 그때 네 눈 봤어. 그리고 그때 알았어."

_Half a second before you turned away. I saw your eyes. And I knew._

"뭘 알았는데?" _Knew what?_

She smiled. The real one. The full one—not the controlled, partial expression
she deployed at school, but the smile that used her entire face, that lifted her
cheekbones and crinkled her eyes and showed the single shallow dimple on her
left side that he had cataloged at age eleven and never forgotten.

"나도 똑같은 마음인 거." _That you felt the same way I did._

She turned and walked away.

Caspar stood on the sidewalk in Daikanyama with a Leica M6 in his hands and
cherry blossoms in his hair and the full, unfiltered weight of a smile he would
spend the rest of his life trying to deserve.

He raised the camera.

He photographed the empty street where she had been standing, the flagstones
still holding the faint impression of her footsteps, the light still warm, the
petals still falling.

_Click._

Frame 9 of 36.

Twenty-seven left.

He was going to need every one.
