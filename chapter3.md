# Chapter 3: Anywhere

---

## I.

Caspar woke at 5:14 AM to the sound of his own breathing and the pale gray light
of pre-dawn Tokyo filtering through curtains he'd forgotten to close.

He lay still. The ceiling was white, featureless, unmarked—the kind of ceiling
that existed in expensive apartments precisely because it had been engineered to
look like nothing, every seam and joint and electrical conduit hidden behind a
surface so smooth it seemed to have been poured rather than built. He stared at
it and let his mind do what it was going to do, which was replay the LINE
conversation from the previous evening in its entirety, word by word, in the
original Korean, with full emotional annotation.

_어디든._

_Anywhere._

He had typed that word lying on this bed, in this exact position, staring at
this exact ceiling, and the word had felt, at the moment of sending, like
jumping off something without knowing how far down the ground was.

He sat up. The apartment was silent—Auntie Park didn't start her kitchen
operations until 5:30, a schedule she maintained with atomic-clock precision. He
swung his legs off the bed, placed his feet on the heated hardwood floor
(another Helena Waldstein specification; she believed cold floors caused
illness, a conviction unsupported by medical science but enforced with the
authority of someone who funded medical science), and stood.

His body was stiff. The hotel year had left him with the particular morning
soreness of someone whose muscles had been built through labor rather than
training—a tightness in the shoulders, a low ache in the lower back, the tendons
in his forearms still holding the memory of thirty-kilo suitcases hauled up
narrow staircases. He stretched, reaching for the ceiling, feeling the vertebrae
in his spine separate and resettle. Then he dropped, palms flat on the floor,
hamstrings burning, holding the position for thirty seconds because the hotel's
staff fitness instructor—a retired Austrian ski racer named Gerhard who believed
flexibility was a moral virtue—had drilled this into him with the patience of a
man who had all winter.

He dressed in running clothes: black compression tights, a gray long-sleeve
technical shirt (unbranded, merino wool blend, purchased from a German outdoor
company that sold exclusively through a single store in Munich and did not
advertise), and a pair of running shoes that had been broken in on alpine roads
and still carried, in the treads, microscopic fragments of Bavarian gravel.

He left the apartment at 5:22.

The streets of Hiroo in the early morning were a study in negative space. No
traffic. No pedestrians. Just the clean geometry of sidewalks and low walls and
the occasional security light throwing a cone of yellow onto perfectly swept
concrete. The cherry trees that lined his street were shedding faster now—the
petals accumulating in drifts along curbs and in the crevices of storm drains,
the branches above showing the first green hints of the leaves that would
replace them.

He ran.

His route took him south through the residential blocks, past the embassy row
with its flags hanging limp in the windless air, down a long slope toward the
Furukawa River, and then east along the riverbank path. The path was narrow,
concrete, bordered on one side by a metal railing and on the other by a
retaining wall covered in moss that glowed emerald in the half-light. His
footsteps were the only sound. His breathing found its rhythm—in for three
strides, out for two—and the thoughts that had been circling since he woke began
to straighten into something more linear, more navigable.

Today he would eat lunch with Isabelle.

The thought arrived clean, unadorned, and he let it sit without attaching
expectation to it. This was a skill the hotel had given him—the ability to hold
a fact in his mind without immediately constructing a narrative around it. At
the Alpenhof, every shift had begun with a briefing: here are today's VIP
arrivals, here are the room assignments, here is the event schedule. Facts. Data
points. You received them, you filed them, and you moved through the day
responding to what actually happened rather than what you'd imagined would
happen.

Today he would eat lunch with Isabelle. That was the fact. Everything else—what
they would say, how it would feel, whether the year-long silence would crack
open or calcify further—was projection, and projection was the enemy of
presence.

He ran four kilometers. By the time he returned to the apartment, the sky had
shifted from gray to pale blue and Auntie Park was awake. He could hear her in
the kitchen before he opened the door—the hiss of the gas range, the particular
clatter of the stone pot being set on the burner, the low murmur of a Korean
morning news broadcast playing from the small television she kept on the
counter.

He showered. The bathroom was large—too large for a single teenager, designed
for the kind of adult who considered a rain shower and a soaking tub to be basic
necessities rather than luxuries. He used the rain shower, standing under the
water with his eyes closed, letting the heat work into his shoulders. The
shampoo was Waldstein Botanicals, naturally—his mother shipped it in quarterly
cases, a formula from the clinical line that smelled like eucalyptus and
cedarwood and probably cost more per bottle than his homeroom teacher earned in
a day. He tried not to think about this. He failed.

He dressed for school. The gakuran fit differently today—or maybe he was
standing differently in it, holding himself with a fractionally altered posture,
the knowledge of the coming lunch shifting his center of gravity by some
imperceptible degree. He buttoned the collar. He pushed his hair back. He looked
at himself in the mirror and saw a person who was pretending to be a normal
student with roughly the same conviction that a wolf might pretend to be a large
dog.

The kitchen table held breakfast: rice,된장찌개, grilled mackerel, pickled
cucumber, seasoned seaweed, and two slices of the Munich pretzel.

"You ran," Auntie Park said. Not a question.

"Four K."

"You should eat more protein after running. I'll add eggs tomorrow."

"You don't need to—"

"I'll add eggs tomorrow."

He sat down and ate. The mackerel was perfect—skin crisped, flesh flaking apart
in clean white layers, the salt crust giving way to the rich, oily sweetness
underneath. He ate it with rice and seaweed, wrapping small portions in the dark
green sheets the way his father had taught him, the way his father's mother had
taught his father, a technique that crossed generations and oceans and corporate
mergers and arrived intact at a kitchen table in Hiroo where a fifteen-year-old
boy was trying not to think about a girl.

"Your bento is ready," Auntie Park said. She placed the wrapped box on the
table. Then she placed a second, smaller container beside it.

Caspar looked at the second container. It was a square Tupperware, sealed with a
blue lid.

"Fruit," Auntie Park said. "Strawberries. The ones from Tochigi."

He knew these strawberries. They were _Skyberry_—a premium cultivar grown in
Tochigi Prefecture, each one the size of a child's fist, perfectly symmetrical,
red as arterial blood, sweet with a floral complexity that had no business
existing in a piece of fruit. They retailed for approximately 3,000 yen per box
of twelve. Auntie Park bought them at the Kinokuniya supermarket in Aoyama,
where the produce section looked like a jewelry store and the prices confirmed
it.

"Those are for two," Auntie Park said.

Caspar looked at her.

Auntie Park's face was neutral. She was wiping the counter with a cloth, not
looking at him, her posture communicating the careful nonchalance of someone who
had just done something deliberate and was waiting to see if it would be
acknowledged.

"Two," he repeated.

"In case you eat lunch with someone. It's polite to share."

She still wasn't looking at him. The cloth made slow circles on the granite
countertop.

"Auntie."

"What."

"How do you know I'm eating lunch with someone?"

Auntie Park set the cloth down. She turned to face him. Her expression was the
one she used when he asked questions whose answers she considered self-evident—a
particular arrangement of eyebrows and mouth that communicated _I love you and
you are being very stupid right now._

"Because Choi-ssi called me last night."

Choi-ssi. Auntie Choi. Isabelle's housekeeper.

"She called you."

"She calls me every evening. We discuss the households. We coordinate."

"You coordinate."

"Shopping. Schedules. Whether your apartments need maintenance. Whether you've
eaten properly. Whether either of you seems—" She paused, selecting the word
with the precision of a jeweler selecting a setting. "—unsettled."

Caspar stared at her. The full picture assembled itself: Auntie Park and Auntie
Choi, connected by a nightly phone call that had apparently been running for the
duration of the gap year, exchanging intelligence across the two households like
a parallel diplomatic channel, monitoring the emotional weather of two
fifteen-year-olds with the systematic thoroughness of a joint surveillance
operation.

"Does Isabelle know?" he asked.

"Does she know what?"

"That you and Auntie Choi talk."

"Of course she knows. She's not stupid. Neither are you, usually."

Caspar picked up the Tupperware of strawberries. He placed it in his school bag
next to the bento.

"Thank you, Auntie."

"Don't thank me. Share the strawberries."

## II.

The morning at Sakuragaoka High passed with the compressed, slightly surreal
quality of a second day—everything from yesterday just familiar enough to
navigate without a map, just unfamiliar enough to keep the senses sharpened. The
bell schedule was the same. The shoe lockers were the same. The hallway smelled
the same: floor polish, chalk dust, the faint vegetal sweetness of cherry
blossoms carried in on shoes and coat hems.

Isabelle arrived at 8:17. She had timed it precisely—early enough to seem
diligent, late enough to avoid the initial crush of arriving students. The gate
was quieter than yesterday. No ceremony, no banner. Just the ordinary Tuesday
rhythm of a school settling into its year.

She found her desk. Yuki was already there, reading a different book—a slim
paperback with a cover illustration that suggested literary fiction—her pencil
case aligned, her posture immaculate. She looked up when Isabelle sat down.

"Good morning, Ji-won."

"Good morning."

"You look rested."

"Thank you."

"It wasn't a compliment. It was an observation. You look rested, which means you
slept, which means your first day didn't cause you significant anxiety. That's
useful data."

Isabelle considered this. "Useful for what?"

"For calibrating how worried I should be about you." Yuki turned a page. "New
students sometimes have a difficult adjustment period. I was assigned to the
class welcome committee in middle school. I take integration seriously."

"Am I being integrated?"

"You're being observed. Integration comes later." The faintest ghost of a smile.
"You'll know when it starts."

Sora arrived at 8:24, breathless, her bag half-open, her school ribbon tied at
an angle that suggested it had been done on a moving train. She dropped into her
seat with the gravitational inevitability of a collapsing star.

"I overslept. I was watching VESPER fancams until 1 AM. Don't judge me."

"Judging you," Yuki said, not looking up.

"Yuki, their choreography for 'Glass Hour' has this part where Minji does a body
roll and then Jiwoo comes in with—okay, you know what, I'll show you at lunch."

"Please don't."

"Ji-won, back me up. You said you listen to some K-pop."

"I said that," Isabelle confirmed.

"See? Ji-won is cultured. Ji-won understands. Ji-won—" Sora stopped. She was
looking at Isabelle's face with the sudden, granular attention of someone who
had noticed something and was in the process of deciding whether to mention it.
"Ji-won, are you wearing makeup?"

"No."

"You're not wearing _any_ makeup?"

"No."

Sora leaned closer. Her eyes moved across Isabelle's face with the analytical
intensity of a dermatologist conducting an assessment. "Your skin is—what is
your skin? What is happening with your skin right now? It's _glowing._ Are you
photosynthesizing?"

"It's just skincare."

"What skincare? What products? I need names, brands, price points."

Isabelle thought about the eight-step Waldstein Botanicals clinical regimen
sitting in a cotton pouch in her school bag. She thought about the fact that the
moisturizer alone retailed—in the markets where it was available, which were
few—at approximately 45,000 yen for 50ml. She thought about the fact that it was
not yet available to the public because Helena Waldstein had personally halted
the launch twice, insisting that the texture needed refinement, and that the
only people who currently possessed it were Helena herself, Isabelle, three
members of the Waldstein Botanicals R&D team, and, almost certainly, Caspar.

"It's Korean," Isabelle said. "My mother's friend makes it."

"What's the brand?"

"It doesn't really have a brand. It's—small."

Sora narrowed her eyes. "Small. Like indie? Like Olive Young indie?"

"Something like that."

Sora looked unconvinced but was distracted by the arrival of Ōtsuka-sensei, who
began the morning homeroom with announcements about club recruitment, the
upcoming health check schedule, and a reminder that indoor shoes must be worn at
all times inside the building. Isabelle settled into her seat, arranged her
notebook, and let the rhythm of the school day close around her like a warm,
ordinary shell.

At 10:35—five minutes before the first break—she took out her phone under her
desk.

She opened LINE. Caspar's chat.

> **신지원:** 점심. 어디서 만날까?
>
> _Lunch. Where should we meet?_

She sent it. The "read" indicator appeared almost instantly, which meant he'd
had his phone out too. Waiting.

> **류태성:** 학교 뒤쪽에 벤치가 있어. 체육관 옆. 아침에 봤어. 사람 별로 없을
> 거야.
>
> _There are benches behind the school. Next to the gymnasium. I saw them this
> morning. Shouldn't be too many people._

She pictured it: the rear of the school building, the gymnasium's windowless
back wall, a row of benches under trees that were probably less photogenic than
the courtyard's cherry blossoms and therefore less popular. A good choice. A
Caspar choice—he had already scouted the location, already identified the sight
lines, already assessed the foot traffic. The photographer's eye, always
framing.

> **신지원:** 좋아. 12시 30분.
>
> _Good. 12:30._

> **류태성:** 12시 30분.

She locked the phone. The bell rang. First break.

## III.

12:28 PM.

Caspar excused himself from his desk with a nod to Kenji, who was in the middle
of an animated retelling of a YouTube video about competitive eating that had
somehow segued into a philosophical argument about whether hot dogs qualified as
sandwiches.

"Where are you going?" Kenji asked.

"Outside. To eat."

"Outside? It's nice out. Can I come?"

"I'm meeting someone."

Kenji's eyes widened fractionally. The "mystery contact" file reopened. "Meeting
someone. At lunch. On the second day of school. Ryu, you move fast."

"It's not—"

"I'm not judging. I'm impressed. Go. Eat. Be mysterious."

Caspar left. He walked through the hallway, down the stairs, and out the side
entrance that opened onto the narrow path running along the gymnasium's east
wall. The benches were where he'd seen them that morning—three wooden slat
benches set in a row under a stand of ginkgo trees, positioned against the
gymnasium wall in a patch of afternoon shadow. The ginkgoes hadn't leafed out
yet; their branches were bare and angular against the pale sky, like cracks in
porcelain.

He sat on the middle bench. He placed his bento on one side and the Tupperware
of strawberries on the other. He waited.

At 12:31, Isabelle came around the corner.

She was carrying her bento in one hand and a small thermos in the other. Her
hair was down—still that heavy, honey-colored wave that caught light like it was
hoarding it—and her school bag was slung over one shoulder at an angle that
managed to make a polyester backpack look like an accessory in a fashion
editorial. She walked with her dancer's walk, each step placed, her posture a
single clean line from the crown of her head to the ground.

She saw him. She didn't slow down. She didn't speed up. She maintained her
pace—steady, measured, the pace of someone who had decided exactly how this
approach would look and was executing it precisely.

She reached the bench. She stood for a moment, looking down at him.

The light was behind her. It came through the bare ginkgo branches and caught in
her hair and along the line of her jaw, and for a half-second Caspar had the
overwhelming, irrational urge to raise a camera that wasn't there—to capture the
exact quality of this light, this angle, this expression on her face, which was
composed of approximately equal parts warmth and wariness and something else
that he didn't have a word for in any of his four languages.

"Hi," she said.

"Hi."

She sat down. Not next to him—she left a space, roughly forty centimeters, the
width of the strawberry Tupperware. Deliberate. A measured distance that was
close enough to share a conversation and far enough to maintain the plausible
deniability of two classmates from adjacent homerooms who happened to be eating
in the same area.

She placed her thermos between her feet and opened her bento on her lap.

Caspar opened his.

For a moment, neither of them spoke. They sat side by side on a bench behind a
gymnasium in a Tokyo public high school, eating Korean food prepared by two
housekeepers who were almost certainly, at this exact moment, texting each other
updates, and the silence between them was not awkward or uncomfortable but
_loaded_—the silence of two people who had too much to say and were trying to
decide which words to spend first.

Isabelle broke it.

"Your bento looks good."

"Auntie Park."

"Obviously." She picked up a piece of bulgogi with her chopsticks, examined it,
ate it. "Auntie Choi used a different cut of beef this time. Thinner. I think
she's competing."

"They're always competing."

"They coordinate, apparently. Nightly phone calls."

"I know. Auntie Park told me this morning."

Isabelle turned her head to look at him. The forty-centimeter gap between them
felt, suddenly, like both too much and not enough. "She told you?"

"She also packed extra strawberries. For two."

A beat. Isabelle looked at the Tupperware of strawberries, which was sitting
between them on the bench like a small, expensive peace offering.

"Skyberry?"

"Tochigi."

She reached over, took one, and bit into it. The fruit was perfect—sweet and
bright, the texture firm, the juice running clean. She chewed. She swallowed.
She looked at the remaining half of the strawberry in her hand.

"Your mother's doing," she said. "These are from the Kinokuniya in Aoyama.
Auntie Choi buys them there too, but only on Wednesdays, when the shipment is
fresh. The fact that these are this good on a Tuesday means someone placed a
special order."

Caspar stared at her. "How do you know the Kinokuniya delivery schedule?"

"I went with Auntie Choi last week. She made the produce manager show her the
cold chain documentation."

"The cold chain documentation."

"She wanted to verify that the storage temperature between the farm and the
store didn't exceed four degrees Celsius. The manager looked like he was going
to cry."

The corner of Caspar's mouth moved. Not a smile—not yet—but the seismic
precursor to one, the tectonic shift that preceded the visible event.

Isabelle saw it. She looked away, but not before he saw the corresponding shift
in her own expression—a softening around her eyes, a release of tension in her
jaw, the micro-movements of a face that was trying very hard to remain composed
and was losing the fight by degrees.

They ate.

The food was good. It was always good—Auntie Park and Auntie Choi did not
produce food that was merely acceptable; they produced food that was correct,
which was a higher standard. Caspar ate his japchae and his seasoned burdock
root and his rice, and Isabelle ate her bulgogi and her pickled radish and her
egg omelette, and the silence between them shifted from loaded to something
gentler—the silence of two people who were remembering, slowly, what it felt
like to be in each other's company.

"The school is different from Le Parcours," Isabelle said. She used the French
pronunciation—_le par-KOOR_—the way they'd always said it, the name carrying the
weight of four years of shared history: dormitories with lake views, classes in
four languages, a student body composed entirely of children whose last names
appeared on buildings and foundation boards and the Forbes list.

"Very different."

"The uniforms are worse."

"Much worse."

"But the people are better."

Caspar looked at her. She was staring straight ahead, at the gymnasium wall, her
chopsticks resting on the edge of her bento. Her profile was clean and
precise—the line of her nose, the curve of her lips, the soft angle where her
jaw met her ear. She was stating something she believed.

"I met a girl," she said. "Yuki. Takahashi Yuki. She sits next to me. She—"
Isabelle paused, searching for the right framework. "She organizes her pencil
case the way your mother organizes a product launch. Everything aligned.
Everything intentional. She told me she's 'observing' me before deciding whether
to integrate me."

"Integrate you."

"Her word."

"I like her already."

Isabelle almost smiled. Almost. The muscles moved, the warmth arrived in her
eyes, and then she caught it and held it, keeping it just below the surface,
visible only to someone who knew exactly where to look.

"And there's Sora," she continued. "Yamamoto Sora. She's—" Isabelle tilted her
head, considering. "—a VESPER fan."

Something changed in Caspar's posture. A slight stiffening. A fractional
adjustment in the angle of his shoulders. It was, by Caspar's standards, a
volcanic eruption.

"A VESPER fan," he said. His voice was level. Controlled. The voice of a person
who had just heard the name of the group whose entire discography he had
memorized, whose fan chants he could execute with military precision, whose
photocards he collected with the dedication of a museum curator, and who was now
performing the most demanding role of his life: a person for whom VESPER was
just a name.

"She played their debut fancam during lunch yesterday," Isabelle said. Her tone
was conversational. Light. She was not looking at him, which meant she was
absolutely looking at him, in the peripheral way that dancers and photographers
both mastered—tracking movement and expression without direct gaze. "She wants
to take me to a fan event in Shibuya next month."

"Hm."

"She says Seo Yuna is her bias."

"Hm."

"Do you know VESPER?"

The question hung in the air between them. A trap. A gift. A door that could
open into a room he absolutely could not let her see.

"I've heard of them," he said.

Isabelle ate a strawberry.

The silence that followed was specific—the silence of someone deciding whether
to push, weighing the pleasure of discovery against the risk of premature
revelation. She decided, apparently, not to push. She placed the strawberry stem
on the lid of her bento and picked up another.

"There's also Hina," she said. "Ogawa Hina. She's quiet. She sits in the back.
She watches."

"Watches what?"

"Everything." Isabelle looked at him directly for the first time since she'd sat
down. Her hazel-green eyes held his. "She watches the way you watch. Through a
viewfinder. She saw you looking at me during the assembly."

Caspar's jaw tightened. A half-second contraction of the masseter muscle,
visible only at the hinge of his jaw, beneath the ear. "Yuki told you."

"Yuki told me you looked at me for four seconds. Yuki keeps count."

"It wasn't four seconds."

"How long was it?"

He didn't answer. He ate a piece of rolled egg omelette. He chewed slowly,
deliberately, buying time the way he used to buy time in Switzerland when
Isabelle asked questions that required honest answers he wasn't ready to give.

"Caspar."

His name. In Korean-accented German, the way she said it—_KAS-par_—with the
first syllable sharp and the second soft, like a door opening.

"It was long enough," he said.

She looked at him. He looked at her. The forty centimeters between them on the
bench hummed with something that was not quite electricity and not quite heat
but was definitely not nothing.

"I told Yuki I didn't know you," Isabelle said.

"Do you know me?"

The question came out before he'd decided to ask it. It sat between them, plain
and enormous, and Isabelle received it the way she received everything—with
stillness, with attention, with the controlled grace of someone who understood
that the most important moments required the least movement.

"I used to," she said.

"And now?"

She put the lid on her bento. She stacked it neatly, aligned the chopsticks on
top, and placed her hands in her lap. Her posture was perfect. Her face was
calm. But her fingers, laced together on her knees, were pressing into each
other with a force that whitened the skin around her knuckles.

"Now I want to find out," she said.

## IV.

They sat for another eight minutes. The conversation shifted—eased, really, like
a muscle releasing after a sustained contraction. They talked about the school.
They talked about their homeroom teachers (Morimoto-sensei's pen-clicking habit;
Ōtsuka-sensei's warmth). They talked about cleaning duty (Caspar mentioned
Kenji's motivational leadership style; Isabelle laughed—a short, quiet sound,
but a laugh, and the sound of it moved through Caspar's chest like a warm
current).

They talked about the housekeepers.

"Auntie Choi starched my blouse twice," Isabelle said. "She said the fabric was
'not what she's used to.' I think she's grieving."

"Auntie Park ironed a crease into my trousers. She said a man without a crease
is a man without self-respect."

"They would get along terrifyingly well."

"They already do. That's the problem."

They talked about their tutors—both named Hayashi, both mild-mannered, both
visibly overwhelmed by the responsibility of serving as legal guardians for
children whose actual parents could purchase the school district.

They did not talk about Switzerland.

They did not talk about the terrace, or the kiss, or the year of silence.

These subjects were present—they sat at the edges of every sentence, visible in
the periphery the way a mountain range was visible from a valley: always there,
always defining the landscape, impossible to ignore and unnecessary to name.
They would be addressed. They both knew this. But not here, not on a bench
behind a gymnasium with twelve minutes of lunch period remaining and the distant
sound of Kenji's voice carrying through an open window somewhere above them.

At 12:47, Isabelle stood.

"I should go back. Sora will launch a search party."

"Will she?"

"She has my LINE. She'll text every thirty seconds until I respond with proof of
life."

Caspar stood too. They faced each other. The ginkgo branches made a lattice of
shadow across the space between them, the bare twigs throwing patterns on the
concrete that looked, from a certain angle, like a net.

"Thank you for the strawberries," she said.

"Thank Auntie Park."

"I will. Through Auntie Choi. Through the nightly diplomatic channel."

He almost smiled again. The precursor was stronger this time—the muscles engaged
more fully, the expression almost broke the surface—and Isabelle watched it
happen with the focused attention of someone witnessing a rare atmospheric
phenomenon.

"내일도?" she said quietly. _Tomorrow too?_

He nodded. "내일도."

She turned and walked away. Same pace, same posture, same deliberate placement
of each step. She rounded the corner of the gymnasium and was gone.

Caspar stood by the bench for three more seconds. Then he packed his bento,
picked up the Tupperware of strawberries—three left, untouched—and walked back
toward the building.

He was halfway down the side path when his phone buzzed.

LINE. Not Isabelle.

Kenji.

> ABANDONED. **けんじ🔥:** Daiki won't talk to me because he's coding.
> **けんじ🔥:** Ren won't talk to me because he's Ren. **けんじ🔥:** You left me
> ALONE with my THOUGHTS, Ryu. Do you know how dangerous that is?

Caspar typed:

> **류:** I ate outside. It was nice.

> **けんじ🔥:** "Nice." You ate outside and it was "nice." That's the most
> suspicious sentence I've ever read. That's what spies say in movies before
> they reveal they have a secret identity. **けんじ🔥:** Tomorrow you're eating

> **류:** Maybe Thursday.

> **류:** I have plans.

> **けんじ🔥:** PLANS. HE HAS PLANS. Daiki, are you seeing this?

> **だいき:** I'm coding.

> **けんじ🔥:** DAIKI HE HAS PLANS.

Caspar locked his phone. He was smiling. Not almost—actually smiling, a small,
private expression that used only the lower half of his face and that he allowed
to exist for exactly four seconds before smoothing it away.

He walked back into the building, changed into his indoor shoes, and climbed the
stairs to the second floor.

In the classroom, Kenji looked at him from across the room with the narrowed
eyes of a detective who had just received a crucial piece of evidence.

Caspar sat down. He opened his textbook. He did not look at the door across the
hall.

## V.

That evening, Isabelle baked.

This was her method. Other people journaled, or ran, or played music. Isabelle
processed the emotional texture of her days by measuring flour.

The kitchen in the Minami-Aoyama apartment was, by any objective standard,
extraordinary. It had been designed by a kitchen consultant from Paris—a man
whom Isabelle's father had met at a dinner in Geneva and whose firm specialized
in professional-grade residential kitchens for clients who took cooking
seriously. The countertops were Carrara marble, honed to a matte finish. The
range was a La Cornue, matte black, six burners plus a plancha, the kind of
stove that looked like it had been forged rather than manufactured. The oven was
convection, triple-glazed, with a temperature accuracy of plus or minus one
degree Celsius. There was a stand mixer—a KitchenAid Professional, in matte
white—and a marble pastry slab that stayed naturally cool, and a set of copper
bowls that had been Isabelle's fourteenth birthday gift from her father, who
understood that his daughter did not want jewelry or clothes but rather the
specific tools required to produce a perfect choux pastry.

Auntie Choi had retreated to her room. She did not interfere with Isabelle's
baking. She had tried, once, early in the arrangement, to supervise—hovering at
the edge of the kitchen with suggestions about temperature and timing—and
Isabelle had turned to her with the calm, immovable authority of a
fifteen-year-old who had been trained by a Meilleur Ouvrier de France and said,
"Auntie, I love you, but if you touch my ganache, I will cry." Auntie Choi had
withdrawn. They had not revisited the matter.

Tonight: madeleines.

Isabelle tied her hair back, washed her hands, and began. The recipe was from
memory—she hadn't used a written recipe in over a year, not since the patisserie
course in Paris (a two-week intensive at a school near the Bastille, officially
a birthday gift, unofficially her father's way of making sure she had a skill
that was entirely, uncomplicated hers, unconnected to her mother's entertainment
empire or the family banking dynasty or any of the other enormous, impersonal
structures that defined her life).

Eggs. Sugar. Whisked until pale and thick, the ribbon stage, the mixture falling
from the whisk in a slow, continuous stream that held its shape on the surface
for three seconds before dissolving. This was the foundation. You could not rush
it. The air incorporated into the batter during this stage would determine the
texture of the final product—the particular light, yielding density of a proper
madeleine, the way it gave under the teeth before releasing its flavor.

Flour, sifted twice. Folded in by hand, the spatula cutting through the batter
in broad, confident strokes—down, across, up, turn the bowl. Beurre noisette:
butter melted in a small saucepan until the milk solids browned and the kitchen
filled with the smell of roasted hazelnuts. This was the smell that made it—the
deep, nutty warmth that would perfume the madeleines and distinguish them from
the merely competent versions sold in every bakery in France.

She folded the browned butter into the batter. She covered the bowl with plastic
wrap. She placed it in the refrigerator.

Then she waited.

The batter needed to rest. Two hours minimum. The cold would firm the butter,
set the structure, and create the thermal shock that would produce the
characteristic bump—the _bosse_—when the chilled batter hit the hot oven. This
was non-negotiable. The bosse was the signature of a madeleine, the small dome
on the back of the shell-shaped cake that separated a real madeleine from a
pretender, and Isabelle would sooner skip the entire exercise than produce a
flat-backed madeleine.

She cleaned the kitchen. She washed the bowls, the whisk, the spatula, the
saucepan. She wiped the marble counter. She folded the towel. The work was
meditative—the repetitive physical labor of cleaning as a counterweight to the
emotional labor of the day—and by the time she finished, the kitchen was
spotless and her mind was quiet.

She sat at the counter and opened her phone.

LINE. Several unread messages.

Sora had sent a series of VESPER-related links: a new interview, a
behind-the-scenes clip from a music show, a fan-edit set to a slowed-down
version of "Glass Hour" that had accumulated 2 million views in three days.

> **そら🌙:** This edit DESTROYED me **そら🌙:** The way they edited Jiwoo's
> part with the rain effect??? **そら🌙:** I am UNWELL

Isabelle watched the fan edit. It was good—the editing was smooth, the pacing
matched the music, the color grading gave the footage a cinematic warmth that
elevated the original broadcast quality. She recognized the source material: the
M Countdown performance from the previous week, filmed from the upper-right
balcony angle. Jiwoo's part—the dance break before the final chorus, where she
isolated from the group and performed a solo sequence—was treated with a layered
rain effect that made her look like she was dancing in a storm.

Isabelle remembered that dance break. She remembered learning it. She remembered
the way Jiwoo had stopped mid-rehearsal, walked over to where Isabelle was
standing by the mirror, and said, without preamble, _"Your extension is longer
than mine. Show me how you hold the last count."_ And Isabelle had shown her—the
ballet technique, the way you stretched through the fingertips as if reaching
for something just beyond your grasp—and Jiwoo had practiced it silently for
forty-five minutes and then performed it on stage three days later, and it had
become one of the most-clipped moments of the debut performance.

Isabelle watched the fan edit again. She watched Jiwoo's fingers at the end of
the sequence—stretched, reaching, the technique borrowed from a Dutch-Korean
girl who was sitting in a Tokyo apartment watching a fan edit of her own
influence.

She typed to Sora:

> **지원:** The rain effect is pretty. She's a good dancer.

> **そら🌙:** "Good dancer." Ji-won. JI-WON. She is a GENERATIONAL TALENT.

> **지원:** 😊

She closed Sora's chat and navigated to Caspar's.

Their lunch conversation replayed in her mind—not the words, exactly, but the
texture of it. The way the silence between them had shifted from heavy to
comfortable. The way he'd almost smiled twice and she'd watched both times with
the greedy attention of someone witnessing something rare. The way his voice
sounded when he spoke Korean to her—lower, softer, the consonants less
percussive than his German, the vowels rounder, the overall effect like
listening to someone speak in a language they associated with safety.

She typed:

> **신지원:** 마들렌을 만들고 있어.
>
> _I'm making madeleines._

> _Really? The lemon ones? The ones you made in Paris?_

She stared at the screen. He remembered. Of course he remembered—she'd sent him
photos from the patisserie course, back before everything broke, before the
terrace, back when their friendship was still simple enough that she could text
him a picture of a cooling rack full of golden shell-shaped cakes and he could
reply with a single 👍 and it meant _I'm proud of you, I wish I was there, save
me one._

> **신지원:** 레몬은 아니고 클래식. 뵈르 누아제트.
>

> **류태성:** 하나 가져와.

She read the message. Four words. An imperative. No _please_, no softening—just
the direct, unadorned request of someone who had once been close enough to ask
for things without asking, who was testing, carefully, whether that proximity

> **신지원:** 내일 점심에.

Isabelle set the phone down. She went to the refrigerator, removed the batter,
and began piping it into the madeleine mold—a French steel pan with twelve
shell-shaped wells, seasoned with years of use, the surface dark and nonstick
from accumulated butter and heat. She filled each well three-quarters full. She
placed the pan in the preheated oven.

Nine minutes. She set the timer.

While she waited, she leaned against the counter and looked out the kitchen
window at the Tokyo night. The city was a field of light—amber streetlamps,
white office towers, the red pulse of aircraft warning beacons on distant
high-rises. Somewhere out there, across the grid of streets and train lines and
rivers, Caspar was in his apartment in Hiroo, probably in his darkroom, probably
staring at negatives, probably thinking about tomorrow.

The timer went off.

She opened the oven. The madeleines were perfect—golden brown, fragrant, each
one bearing a pronounced bosse that rose from the back of the shell like a
small, proud hill. She turned them out onto a wire rack. The kitchen smelled
like browned butter and vanilla and the faintest trace of something floral that
she couldn't identify and didn't try to.

She let them cool. She selected four—the best four, the ones with the most
symmetrical bosses and the most even coloring—and placed them in a small
container lined with parchment paper. She sealed the container and placed it in
her school bag, next to the cotton pouch of skincare.

Then she typed one final message:

She locked the phone. She turned off the kitchen lights. She walked to her room,
the apartment quiet around her, the city luminous beyond the glass, and she
thought: _This is how it starts. Not with a grand gesture. Not with a
confrontation. With strawberries and madeleines and the slow, patient work of
learning to be near each other again._

She thought: _Tomorrow._

She thought: _어디든._

_Anywhere._

## VI.

In Hiroo, Caspar was in his darkroom.

The red safelight cast the small space in its familiar monochrome
glow—everything reduced to shades of crimson and shadow, the chemical trays
glinting dully, the enlarger standing in the corner like a mechanical bird. He
was developing a new roll: shots from this morning's run, taken on the Leica M6
that he'd slipped into his jacket pocket on the way out, unable to resist.

The negatives hung on the wire, dripping fixer solution onto the catch tray. He
leaned forward with the loupe.

Frame 1: The Furukawa River path at dawn, the retaining wall's moss a dark mass
against the lighter water. Frame 2: A vending machine on a corner in Hiroo, its
blue-white glow the only light source in the frame, the surrounding buildings
dark silhouettes. Frame 3: Cherry blossom petals accumulated in a storm drain, a
small galaxy of pale shapes against the dark metal grate.

Frame 4: The school gate.

He'd taken it from across the street, before Hayashi-san had parked, through the
car window. The gate was centered in the frame, the stone pillars flanking it,
the welcome banner a pale rectangle of text. Through the gate, the courtyard
path was visible, lined with cherry trees in bloom. The light was early-morning
flat, shadowless, the kind of light that made everything look provisional, as if
the scene might dissolve if you looked at it too hard.

There were no people in the frame. He'd waited for the gap between arriving
students, timing the shutter to capture the gate empty. It looked like an
entrance to something that hadn't started yet. A threshold. A beginning.

He marked the frame for printing.

He moved to the enlarger, set the negative in the carrier, and switched on the
lamp. The image appeared on the baseboard, projected in light—the gate, the
pillars, the cherry trees, all rendered in the reversed tonality of a
photographic negative, the darks bright and the brights dark. He adjusted the
focus. He adjusted the aperture. He placed a sheet of fiber-based paper on the
baseboard and made the exposure.

Into the developer tray. The image emerged slowly—the gate appearing first, the
darkest element, then the pillars, then the trees, the details resolving out of
the white paper like a memory surfacing from static. He watched it come. This
was the part he loved most—the emergence, the moment when the latent image
became visible, when the thing that had been hidden was finally allowed to
exist.

He transferred the print to the stop bath. Then the fixer. Then the wash.

He clipped the wet print to the drying line and stepped back.

The school gate. Empty. Waiting.

He looked at it in the red light and thought about Isabelle standing in that
gate, framed by those pillars, her hair catching the morning light. He thought
about the way she'd nodded to him in the entrance hall—that small, controlled
gesture that had contained, in its precise economy, more emotional information
than most people conveyed in entire conversations. He thought about the bench
behind the gymnasium and the strawberries and the forty-centimeter gap between
them and the way she'd said _Now I want to find out_ with her knuckles turning
white.

He thought about madeleines.

He thought about the fact that she had baked for him—not for the school, not for
her new friends, not for the general purpose of having baked goods. For _him._
Four madeleines, the best ones, selected and packed and placed in her school bag
with the same care that she brought to everything, the same precision, the same
quiet, furious attention to detail that had once made him realize, on a Swiss
terrace at the age of fourteen, that he was looking at the person he would never
stop looking at.

He left the darkroom. He sat on the edge of his bed. His phone was on the
nightstand, the screen dark.

He picked it up and navigated to a different app—not LINE, but a fan community
app, one of several he used under an anonymous handle that bore no resemblance
to any of his real names. His profile: **@frame36** (a reference to the last
frame on a roll of 35mm film, always his favorite, always slightly overexposed
because the film was running out and the camera didn't know it yet).

He scrolled through the VESPER fan feed. New content from today: a preview photo
for the group's upcoming magazine cover shoot, four women in coordinated
monochrome outfits, standing in a white studio, their poses sharp and editorial.
Yuna in the center, her chin tilted down, her eyes looking up through her lashes
with an expression that managed to be simultaneously inviting and
unapproachable. Minji on the left, body angled away from the camera but face
turned toward it, her jawline catching the studio light. Haeun in the back,
slightly elevated, arms crossed, her smile warm but controlled. And Jiwoo on the
right, profile shot, one hand raised near her face, fingers extended in a
gesture that—

Caspar stopped scrolling.

He looked at Jiwoo's hand. The fingers. The extension. The way the line traveled
from her wrist through her palm to the tips of her fingers, reaching for
something beyond the frame.

That wasn't a K-pop gesture. That was ballet.

He'd seen that extension before. He'd seen it in the practice room footage that
fans had dissected frame by frame, in the debut performance clips, in every
subsequent choreography. It was one of Jiwoo's signatures—the long, elegant
reach that gave her movements a quality that the fans described as "ethereal"
and that the dance analysis accounts broke down into skeletal diagrams and
muscle engagement charts.

But Caspar was a photographer, and photographers saw provenance. They saw the
origin point of a gesture, the lineage of a movement, the way a technique
migrated from one body to another. And that extension—that reach—was not native
to K-pop choreography. It came from classical dance. It came from somewhere
else.

He thought about Isabelle's gap year. Seoul. Her mother's agency. She'd been
vague about it. She was always vague about it.

He thought about her hands—the way she held a whisk, the way she folded a
batter, the way she'd rested her fingers on the edge of the shoe locker on the
first day with a dancer's unconscious grace.

He closed the app. He put the phone down.

He stared at the ceiling.

_What were you doing in Seoul, Isabelle?_

He didn't ask. Not tonight. Tonight was for madeleines and strawberries and the
slow, careful reconstruction of a bridge that had been standing in pieces for a
year.

He turned off the light.

In Minami-Aoyama, twelve kilometers and a hundred unasked questions away,
Isabelle did the same.
