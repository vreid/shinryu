# Chapter 1: Before the Gate

---

## I.

The toaster in the Minami-Aoyama apartment had been imported from a workshop in
Darmstadt.

It was a stupid thing to notice, but Isabelle noticed it anyway—the way the
brushed steel caught the early morning light slanting through the
floor-to-ceiling windows, the barely-there hum it made as it worked two slices
of high-protein German rye down to a shade just past golden. There was no brand
name on it. No logo. Just a small engraved serial number on the underside that
she'd found one evening while flipping it over out of boredom, and a finish so
precise that it felt like touching the hood of a car that cost more than most
Tokyo apartments.

It was the most German toaster she had ever seen. Which meant it was his
mother's doing.

Helena Waldstein did not believe in visible logos. She believed in _materials_.
In the quiet confession of a hand-stitched seam, the weight of a door handle
milled from solid brass, the particular matte grain of Bavarian oak that said
nothing to most people and everything to the ones who mattered. Isabelle had
grown up orbiting that philosophy—had internalized it so deeply that she could
now identify a Loro Piana cashmere by the drape alone, or spot the difference
between a Smeg and whatever this engineered-in-a-Black-Forest-laboratory thing
was that Helena had undoubtedly had shipped to Tokyo in a crate with more
customs paperwork than a piece of fine art.

The toast popped.

Isabelle caught both slices on a small ceramic plate—Arita-yaki, hand-painted,
another quiet import—and reached for the French butter that Auntie Choi kept in
a dedicated compartment of the refrigerator, wrapped in wax paper like a
newborn.

"You are not eating that with just butter."

Auntie Choi's voice came from the kitchen island behind her, sharp as a cleaver.
She was a compact woman in her late fifties with a center-parted bob that had
never, in the years Isabelle had known her, moved a single millimeter out of
place. She wore a navy linen apron over a white cotton blouse, and her posture
suggested she had been standing at attention since roughly 1987.

"I'm going to put jam on it," Isabelle said.

"Which jam."

"The apricot."

"The Bonne Maman or the one from Vienna?"

Isabelle paused, butter knife hovering. "There's one from Vienna?"

"Mrs. Waldstein sent a case." Auntie Choi opened a cabinet and produced a jar
with a handwritten label in German, the glass heavy and faceted like something
from an apothecary. "It arrived Tuesday. I was told it's from the same supplier
that provides the Sacher Hotel."

Isabelle took the jar, turned it in her hands. The label read _Wachauer
Marillenmarmelade_ in slanted cursive. She unscrewed the lid and the scent hit
her—bright, tart, almost floral, the kind of apricot that tasted like it had
been picked by someone who had opinions about soil acidity.

She thought of Caspar.

The thought arrived the way it always did—without permission, fully formed,
slotting into place behind her sternum like a key turning in a lock she hadn't
realized was there. Not the memory of his face, exactly, but the _architecture_
of him: the way he stood with his weight slightly back on his heels, the way his
jaw tightened a half-second before he said something that mattered, the way his
pale eyes tracked a room the way a camera tracked light—finding the angles no
one else was looking at.

She spread the jam on her toast with more force than was strictly necessary.

"Sit down and eat properly," Auntie Choi said. "Your skirt is pressed and
hanging on the bathroom door. I have starched the blouse twice because the
fabric is—" She paused, searching for a diplomatic word. "—not what I'm used
to."

"It's a public school uniform, Auntie."

"I'm aware."

The disapproval in those two syllables could have peeled paint. Auntie Choi had
spent twenty-three years managing households for families whose children
attended schools with stone crests and Latin mottos. The concept of a
polyester-blend sailor-collar blouse was, to her, a kind of violence.

Isabelle bit into her toast. The jam was extraordinary. Of course it was.

---

The uniform was laid out on the bathroom door with the precision of a museum
exhibit.

White blouse, pressed to knife-edge creases. Navy pleated skirt, hemmed to
exactly four centimeters above the knee—the Sakuragaoka standard, which Auntie
Choi had confirmed by calling the school's administrative office twice and then
measuring with a tailor's tape. Navy knee-high socks. Brown leather loafers that
Isabelle had bought herself from a department store in Shinjuku, deliberately
mid-range, deliberately unremarkable.

She stood in front of the bathroom mirror in a towel and studied herself with
the detached, clinical eye of someone who had been looked at—professionally,
appraisingly, by makeup artists and choreographers and camera operators—for an
entire year.

Five foot ten at fifteen. That was the first problem.

The second problem was everything else.

Her face had changed during the year in Seoul. Not dramatically—she was still
recognizably herself—but the baby fat had refined into something more angular,
more deliberate. Her cheekbones had sharpened. Her jawline had narrowed into a
clean oval. Her skin—and this was the one thing she could genuinely credit
Helena Waldstein's lab for—had taken on that particular luminous quality that
Korean beauty forums called _mul-gwang_, water-light, the complexion that looked
like it was being lit from inside. Ten months of a skincare regimen that cost
more per milliliter than most perfumes, applied with religious discipline under
the supervision of four women who treated a clogged pore the way NATO treated a
border incursion.

She dried her hair. It fell past her shoulders in heavy waves—natural,
caramel-blonde, the color of raw honey held up to a window. It was too much hair
for a Japanese high school. She knew this. She'd considered cutting it, had even
made an appointment at a salon in Omotesandō, and then cancelled it because
she'd remembered—unwillingly, viscerally—the way Caspar used to look at her
hair. Not with desire, not at fifteen, but with the focus of someone seeing a
color he wanted to photograph.

She left it down.

The blouse was, as Auntie Choi had warned, not great fabric. Isabelle buttoned
it to the collar and tucked it into the skirt, then spent a full minute
adjusting the way it sat across her shoulders. The pleated skirt swung just
above her knees. She clipped her school ID to the breast pocket—**Tokyo
Metropolitan Sakuragaoka High School, Class 1-3, Shin Ji-won**—and turned
sideways.

She looked like a model doing a high school editorial.

This was going to be a problem.

She reached for her school bag—a standard navy randoseru-style backpack, bought
at the same Shinjuku department store—and then paused, staring at the bathroom
counter. Her daily skincare routine was eight steps. Eight small bottles and
jars, each one from Waldstein Botanicals' unreleased clinical line, each one
labeled in Helena's own handwriting with instructions in German that Isabelle
had long ago memorized.

She packed three of them into a small cotton pouch and zipped it into the bag's
inner pocket. A girl had priorities.

---

The kitchen smelled like sesame oil and seaweed when she emerged.

Auntie Choi had laid out a full Korean breakfast at the counter: steamed rice,
doenjang-jjigae in a stone pot still bubbling from the stove, three kinds of
banchan in small white dishes, and a perfectly fried egg with edges just
starting to crisp. Beside it, a glass of cold barley tea.

"Eat everything," Auntie Choi said. She was already washing the stone pot she'd
used for the jjigae base, scrubbing it with the focus of someone defusing a
bomb. "You cannot go to your first day at a new school on toast and Austrian
jam."

"German-Austrian. The Wachau Valley is—"

"Eat."

Isabelle sat on the counter stool and ate. The jjigae was perfect—rich, earthy,
the fermented depth of aged doenjang balanced against the clean brightness of
zucchini and tofu. Auntie Choi had been cooking for the Shin family since before
Isabelle was born. Every meal was a small, uncomplicated act of love expressed
through exact measurements and absolutely no sentimentality.

"Your tutor confirmed with the school yesterday," Auntie Choi said, drying her
hands on the linen towel she kept folded over her left shoulder. "Hayashi-sensei
will be listed as your local guardian for all administrative purposes. He will
attend the entrance ceremony. He will sit in the parents' section. He will not
speak to other parents unless directly addressed, in which case he will say that
he is a family friend."

"Got it."

"Your father called this morning."

Isabelle's chopsticks paused over the banchan. "When?"

"Five-thirty. He was in Geneva. He asked me to tell you—" Auntie Choi consulted
a note written on a small pad by the phone. "—'Break a leg, schatje. Don't eat
too much bread.' He also wired your monthly allowance. It is in the account."

Isabelle smiled into her rice. _Schatje._ Her father—Willem van Rijn, whose
signature could move currency markets and whose handshake had closed real estate
deals on four continents—still called her the same Dutch pet name he'd used when
she was three and he'd carry her on his shoulders through the gardens in
Wassenaar.

"And my mother?"

A pause. Auntie Choi folded the towel.

"Your mother's office sent a gift basket. It's on the table in the hallway."

Isabelle didn't turn around. She knew what it would be: a beautifully wrapped
box from whichever luxury brand was currently courting Shin Entertainment for a
sponsorship deal, containing something expensive and age-inappropriate—a
handbag, probably, or a set of jewelry she couldn't wear to school—accompanied
by a printed card with her mother's signature in her assistant's handwriting.

Shin Hee-yeon loved her daughter. Isabelle had never doubted this. But Shin
Hee-yeon loved her daughter the way she loved her artists—fiercely,
strategically, and from behind a desk.

"Okay," Isabelle said. She finished her rice.

## II.

Across the city, in a renovated mid-century apartment on a tree-lined street in
Hiroo, Caspar Waldstein was standing in his darkroom staring at a strip of 35mm
negatives and trying not to think.

The darkroom was a converted walk-in closet that his mother's Tokyo interior
designer had reluctantly agreed to modify. The original plans for the apartment
had called for the closet to house a rotating wardrobe system that could be
controlled via an app. Caspar had asked for blackout curtains, a wet bench,
three chemical trays, and an enlarger. The designer, a woman from Kyoto with
frameless glasses and a visible aversion to analog anything, had complied with
the strained grace of someone performing a mercy.

The negatives hung from a wire strung between two hooks. He leaned forward,
squinting through a loupe at the tiny inverted frames. Mountains. The Bavarian
Alps in winter. The long shadow of the Zugspitze falling across a snowfield. A
luggage cart parked outside the hotel's service entrance, one wheel broken, the
brass fixtures dull with use.

And then, near the end of the roll—one he'd almost forgotten about—a figure.

Small in the frame, standing near a window in the hotel's staff corridor. A
silhouette, really. A girl's silhouette, caught in the blue light of a winter
afternoon, her hair loose around her shoulders, looking at something outside the
frame that he couldn't remember anymore.

It wasn't Isabelle. He'd taken it months before the thought of Tokyo, months
before the letter from his parents that had rearranged everything. It was a
guest's daughter, a teenager his age, waiting for her family. He'd raised the
camera on instinct—the light was right, the composition was accidental and
perfect—and she'd moved away before the shutter closed, leaving only the
impression of a person where a person had been.

But it looked like Isabelle.

Everything looked like Isabelle.

He stepped back from the negatives, pulled the blackout curtain aside, and
walked into the kitchen, where Auntie Park was aggressively portioning rice into
a bento box.

"Sit," she said, without turning around.

Auntie Park was the opposite of compact. She was a tall woman with broad
shoulders and a perm that she maintained with a devotion that bordered on
spiritual. She wore an apron printed with small cartoon cats—a gift from Caspar
that she had received with a single raised eyebrow and then worn every day
since. Her Korean was louder than Auntie Choi's. Her cooking was better. Caspar
would never say this out loud, but he suspected Isabelle's Auntie Choi knew it
too, and that it was a source of quiet, permanent war.

The kitchen table held a full breakfast: fluffy white rice, kimchi-jjigae with
thick-cut pork belly, seasoned spinach, fried anchovies in a sweet soy glaze,
and a rolled egg omelette sliced into perfect coins. There was also,
inexplicably, a single German pretzel on a separate plate.

Caspar sat down and looked at the pretzel.

"Your mother sent them," Auntie Park said. "A box of twelve. Frozen, from
Munich. She said you should have something familiar on your first day."

He picked up the pretzel. It was warm—Auntie Park had reheated it in the oven,
and the crust had crisped back to almost the right texture, though the salt
crystals had dissolved slightly. It tasted like the bakery two streets from
their house in Bogenhausen, the one where his father would stop every Saturday
morning and buy six, carrying them home in a paper bag that left grease spots on
his overcoat.

"Thank you," Caspar said.

"Eat the real food too. I didn't wake up at four-thirty to watch you eat bread."

He ate. The kimchi-jjigae was, as always, a controlled explosion—the aged kimchi
sour and deep, the pork belly rendered soft, the broth brick-red and complex in
a way that seemed to violate thermodynamics. He ate methodically, the way he did
everything: one dish at a time, finishing each before moving to the next, his
chopstick technique precise enough that Auntie Park had once, in a rare moment
of praise, said it was "not embarrassing."

"Your uniform is on the chair," Auntie Park said. She was now packing the bento
with the intensity of an engineer solving a spatial problem. Rice on the left.
Main in the center. Banchan tucked into silicone cups along the right side. A
small container of sauce, sealed with plastic wrap and then a rubber band,
because Auntie Park did not trust lids. "I ironed the trousers with a crease.
The school regulations do not specify a crease, but a man without a crease in
his trousers is a man without self-respect."

Caspar did not argue with this.

"Hayashi-san will drive you. He will attend the ceremony. He will introduce
himself as your guardian. He has been briefed."

"Briefed by whom?"

"By me." Auntie Park set the bento box lid in place with a decisive click. "And
by your mother's Tokyo office. And by the school's vice-principal, who called
yesterday to confirm your enrollment and who sounded, if I may say so,
confused."

"Confused about what?"

"About why a student transferring from a Swiss boarding school is attending a
metropolitan public high school instead of one of the international schools. I
told him that your family values a Japanese education and wishes for you to
integrate fully into Japanese society." She paused. "He did not sound less
confused."

Caspar finished his egg omelette. He thought about explaining the real
reason—that his parents wanted him here, specifically, at this specific school,
in this specific ward, for reasons that had nothing to do with educational
philosophy and everything to do with a girl whose family name appeared on the
same boardroom agendas as his—but Auntie Park already knew. Auntie Park knew
everything. She simply chose to frame it as a matter of educational values
because Auntie Park believed that the way you described a situation was the
situation, and if you described a manipulative corporate marriage arrangement as
"a family that values Japanese education," then that's what it was.

He stood up and took his dishes to the sink.

"Leave them," Auntie Park said.

"I've got it."

"You are not washing dishes on the morning of your first day of school. Sit
down. Go put on your uniform. Do something with your hair."

"What's wrong with my hair?"

Auntie Park looked at him. Caspar's hair was thick, straight, and dark
ash-blonde—a color that existed on the exact boundary between European and East
Asian and committed fully to neither. It fell across his forehead and over his
ears in a way that, in Switzerland, had been considered fashionably undone, and
which in the Bavarian hotel had been considered a violation of grooming
standards, and which here, in Tokyo, would be considered—

"Too long," Auntie Park said.

"The school regulations say it can't touch the collar."

"It is touching the collar."

"It's near the collar."

"Go." She pointed toward the hallway with a spatula. "And tuck in your shirt.
All the way. Japanese schools check."

---

The uniform felt strange.

Not uncomfortable—it was fine, a standard black gakuran-style uniform with a
standing collar and brass buttons, paired with straight-leg dark trousers and
plain white indoor shoes for the school building. The fabric was middleweight
wool-poly blend, the kind of material that his mother would have, in another
context, gently set on fire. But it fit him well enough. The trousers broke
cleanly over his shoes. The jacket sat square on his shoulders—broader now than
they'd been a year ago, broadened by months of hauling 30-kilo suitcases up four
flights of stairs in a hotel that considered elevators for staff to be a moral
failing.

He buttoned the collar and looked at himself in the full-length mirror in his
bedroom.

The problem was the same problem it had always been: he did not look like a
normal high school student. He looked like a still from a European fashion
campaign that happened to be set in a Japanese school—all sharp angles and pale
coloring and the kind of bone structure that made strangers stare and then look
away when he stared back.

This was fine in Switzerland, where half the students at Institut Le Parcours
were the children of diplomats and oligarchs and looked like minor royalty. It
was fine in Bavaria, where the hotel uniform—a gray wool vest and dark
trousers—had rendered him functionally invisible, just another body moving
through the service corridors.

It would not be fine here.

He pushed his hair back, let it fall, pushed it back again. Then he picked up
his school bag—a black nylon backpack, unbranded, purchased from a Muji in
Roppongi—and walked out of the bedroom.

Auntie Park was waiting in the genkan with his outdoor shoes aligned perfectly
on the step, toes facing the door. Beside them, a small paper bag.

"Your bento," she said, handing it to him. "And there is a separate container
with fruit. And a thermos of barley tea. Do not lose the thermos, it was
expensive."

Caspar looked at the thermos. It was a Zojirushi, matte black, the kind you
could buy at any home goods store in Tokyo for about 3,000 yen. In the context
of Auntie Park's world, this qualified as a significant investment.

"Thank you, Auntie."

"Don't thank me. Do well." She straightened the collar of his jacket with both
hands, tugging the fabric flat with the practiced efficiency of someone who had
been adjusting Caspar's collars since he was six. Her fingers lingered for half
a second on the brass button at his throat. "You look handsome. Your mother
would cry."

"My mother doesn't cry."

"Your mother cried when you left for the hotel. She called me from the car. She
was in the back seat of a Mercedes in Munich traffic and she was crying so hard
that the driver asked if she needed a hospital." Auntie Park smoothed a
non-existent wrinkle from his shoulder. "She told me to never tell you. So I'm
telling you."

Caspar felt something shift behind his ribs. He didn't move. He didn't let it
reach his face.

"Go," Auntie Park said. She stepped back. "Hayashi-san is downstairs."

## III.

The car was a black Toyota Crown, current model, with tinted windows and an
interior that smelled like leather conditioner and green tea.
Hayashi-san—mid-thirties, rectangular glasses, a navy suit that fit like it had
been cut for a man whose primary job was to be unremarkable—was already behind
the wheel.

"Good morning, Tae-sung-kun."

"Good morning."

"Shall we review the route?"

"I know the route."

Hayashi-san nodded. He pulled away from the curb with the smooth, unhurried
competence of someone who had spent time driving for people who did not like to
be jostled. The streets of Hiroo slid past: quiet residential blocks, low
apartment buildings screened by manicured hedges, the occasional European
embassy set behind iron gates.

Caspar watched the city through the window. Tokyo in early April was doing the
thing it did every year—the sakura were in the last days of full bloom, and the
petals were beginning to fall, drifting across sidewalks and windshields in
slow, lateral spirals that made the morning light feel textured, almost
granular. A woman in a gray coat was walking a Shiba Inu past a convenience
store. A delivery truck was double-parked outside a florist. Two salary-men in
identical dark suits stood at a crosswalk, their faces lit blue by their phones.

He wanted to photograph it. His fingers twitched against his bag, where his
Leica M6 sat wrapped in a soft cloth inside a padded compartment. But he didn't
reach for it. Not today. Today he was supposed to be a normal student arriving
at a normal school, and normal students did not carry hand-assembled German
rangefinder cameras worth more than their parents' car.

The phone in his pocket buzzed.

He took it out—an iPhone, matte black case, no stickers, no charms, screen
protector slightly scratched in the lower left corner from when he'd dropped it
on the cobblestones outside the hotel in Garmisch. There was a LINE
notification.

He stared at it.

The notification was from a group chat. A family group chat—his parents, his
mother's assistant, Auntie Park, Hayashi-san, and himself. His mother had sent a
photo of a bouquet of white tulips on her desk in the Seoul office, captioned:
**First day! I'm with you in spirit, Schatz. 화이팅! 💙**

His father had replied with a single emoji: 👍

This was, for Ryu Ji-seok, an extravagant display of emotion.

Caspar typed a response— **감사합니다** —and then closed the app. And then
opened it again. And then navigated, with the slow reluctance of someone
approaching a door they weren't sure they wanted to open, to his private chat
history.

The chat with Isabelle.

It was still there. Their last exchange—from months ago, from before the gap
year, from the week after everything had cracked—sat at the bottom of the thread
like a fossil preserved in amber.

> **Isabelle:** I'm not angry at you.
>
> **Isabelle:** I need you to know that.
>
> **Isabelle:** I'm just not ready to talk about it yet.

He had not replied. He had read the messages—she would have seen the blue
checkmarks—and he had not replied, because he hadn't known what to say that
wouldn't make it worse, and because the only honest reply would have been _I
think about it every day, the exact angle of your chin when you leaned in, the
way the light was behind you so your hair looked almost white, the half-second
when I could feel your breath on my mouth before I turned my head, and I turned
my head not because I didn't want to but because I wanted to so much that it
scared me, and I am fifteen and I do not have the language for any of this—_

He closed the app.

He put the phone in his pocket.

"We'll arrive in about twelve minutes," Hayashi-san said from the front seat.
"The entrance ceremony begins at nine. I'll park in the visitor lot."

"Okay."

"Are you nervous?"

Caspar considered this. Nervous was not the right word. He had spent a year
carrying luggage for people who screamed at him in four languages. He had been
dressed down by a head concierge who believed that a scuffed shoe was a moral
deficiency. He had stood in a freezing service corridor at eleven at night,
ironing tablecloths for a banquet he would not attend, while somewhere above
him, in the ballroom, people who were exactly like his parents drank champagne
and laughed at jokes that weren't funny.

He was not nervous about high school.

He was nervous about a five-foot-ten girl with hazel-green eyes who made apricot
jam taste like a memory.

"No," he said.

## IV.

Isabelle saw the school before she felt it.

Hayashi-san—her Hayashi-san, a different man from Caspar's, younger, with a
softer face and a habit of humming enka songs under his breath while
driving—pulled the car to the curb on the sloping street that ran along the
school's southern wall. She could see the gate from here: a plain metal
structure, painted gray, flanked by two stone pillars with the school name
carved in clean kanji. **東京都立桜丘高等学校.** A banner hung between the
pillars—white fabric, navy text: _Welcome, New Students._

Beyond the gate, a paved path led through a courtyard lined with cherry trees in
full bloom. The petals were falling steadily, accumulating in pale drifts along
the edges of the path, and the morning light filtered through the canopy in
shifting patterns that made the ground look like it was breathing.

Students were streaming through the gate in clusters of two and three—new
first-years in crisp uniforms, their faces carrying that particular blend of
excitement and dread that was universal to the first day of high school in any
country. Some walked with their parents. Some walked alone, headphones in, eyes
forward, performing the careful nonchalance of someone who was terrified.

Isabelle watched them from behind the tinted glass.

They were small. That was her first thought, and she immediately felt guilty for
it, because it wasn't a judgment—it was just a fact. She was five-ten in flat
shoes, and the average Japanese fifteen-year-old girl was maybe five-two,
five-three. She was going to walk through that gate and every head in the
courtyard was going to turn, and it wasn't going to be subtle.

"Ready?" Hayashi-san asked.

"Yes."

She opened the door.

The air hit her first—cool, slightly damp, carrying the green-sap scent of
cherry blossoms and the faint industrial tang of city morning. It smelled
nothing like Wassenaar, which smelled like cut grass and the North Sea, and
nothing like Seoul, which smelled like street food and concrete and the
particular floral sweetness of a hundred skincare products applied in a room
with bad ventilation. It smelled like _Tokyo._ Clean and busy and oddly neutral,
as if the city itself was withholding judgment.

She stepped out of the car, swung her bag onto one shoulder, and straightened
her skirt.

Hayashi-san appeared beside her, tugging at the cuffs of his suit jacket. He was
nervous—she could tell by the way he kept touching his glasses, pushing them up
the bridge of his nose even though they hadn't moved. This was his first
official act as her registered guardian, and the weight of the role—the sheer,
bureaucratic enormity of standing in for the combined authority of Willem van
Rijn and Shin Hee-yeon at a public school parents' meeting—was sitting visibly
on his shoulders.

"You'll be fine," she said, in Japanese. She kept her accent carefully
flattened—standard Tokyo Japanese, textbook-clean, with none of the fluency or
idiomatic ease that would give her away. She'd practiced this. Hours with the
private tutor in Seoul, drilling the precise calibration of sounding competent
but not native. The "language filter," she called it. A deliberate veil of
slight hesitation and occasional pauses, just enough to make people speak slowly
and clearly to her, just enough to make them assume she was catching up when she
was actually three sentences ahead.

They walked through the gate.

The courtyard was louder than it looked from the car. Voices layered over each
other—excited chatter, the sharp laughter of boys showing off, a teacher's
amplified voice from somewhere inside the gymnasium calling for new students to
assemble by class. The cherry trees rustled overhead, dropping petals onto hair
and shoulders and open palms.

A group of girls near the entrance noticed her. She could feel it—not the
looking, exactly, but the quality of the silence that preceded it. That
half-second vacuum where conversation just _stopped_, replaced by a specific
frequency of attention that she'd learned to recognize in Seoul, in the practice
rooms, in the moments before VESPER walked into a space and the air itself
seemed to rearrange.

_Don't look at them. Walk naturally. You're just a student._

She walked naturally. Her posture—trained into her by five years of ballet in
Amsterdam and then brutally refined by a Korean choreographer named Park Jisoo
who believed that a dancer's spine should be a single unbroken line from skull
to tailbone—made this more difficult than it should have been. There was a way
to walk casually, and then there was the way Isabelle walked, which was the way
people walked on runways and in palace corridors: from the center of the body,
hips level, shoulders back, each step placed rather than taken.

She reached the shoe-locker area inside the main building's entrance and found
her assigned cubby. Changed into the white indoor shoes. Placed her outdoor
loafers on the shelf. The locker smelled like wood polish and new rubber.

And then, from behind her—quiet, unremarkable, just another body in the stream
of students flowing through the entrance hall—she heard a voice.

Not speaking to her. Not speaking to anyone. Just a voice, low and even, saying
something to a teacher at the registration desk, and the particular cadence of
it—the unhurried precision, the way each syllable was placed like a stone in a
wall—reached through the noise and the cherry-blossom air and the careful
architecture of her composure and hit her directly in the chest.

She knew that voice. She knew it the way she knew the weight of a whisk in her
hand or the opening bars of VESPER's debut single or the exact feeling of a cold
December evening in Wassenaar when the light turned silver over the dunes.

Isabelle did not turn around.

Her fingers rested on the edge of the shoe locker. She could feel the grain of
the wood under her fingertips, the slight imperfection where the varnish had
pooled near the hinge. Her heartbeat had done something she didn't appreciate—a
syncopation, a missed step in the rhythm, the kind of thing that Park Jisoo
would have caught and corrected with a sharp clap.

_He's here._

She closed the shoe locker.

She picked up her bag.

She turned around.

---

## V.

Caspar was standing seven meters away.

He was at the registration desk, bent slightly at the waist to write something
on a form, a pen held in his left hand. His school jacket was buttoned to the
collar. His hair fell across his forehead in a way that Auntie Park would have
considered a personal failure. His profile—the sharp line of his nose, the clean
angle of his jaw, the way his ear sat close to his head—was exactly the same as
the last time she'd seen it, which was the last time she'd seen anything
clearly, which was a cold evening on a stone terrace in Switzerland while the
Alps turned pink behind them and her whole chest felt like it was made of glass.

He was taller.

That was the first thing. He'd grown—at least five centimeters since
Switzerland, maybe more—and it wasn't just height. His shoulders had broadened.
His neck had thickened slightly. The jacket, which should have looked boxy and
shapeless the way it did on the other boys, sat across his upper body with the
quiet tension of fabric being asked to accommodate more muscle than it had
planned for.

_Hauling luggage,_ she thought. _The hotel. He was carrying suitcases._

She knew this because her mother's assistant had told her, in passing, during a
phone call about something else entirely, and Isabelle had held the phone to her
ear in the vocal booth at Shin Entertainment's studio and said nothing while the
information detonated quietly in her chest. Caspar. Beautiful, precise, careful
Caspar, who had once refused to carry his own bag through an airport because a
porter was "already coming." Working as a bellboy. Invisible. Serving people who
were exactly like them.

It had changed him. She could see it even from here—in the set of his shoulders,
in the economy of his movements, in the way he held the pen without flourish and
stood at the desk without expectation. Something in his posture had been
stripped down, simplified, like a building that had lost its ornamental facade
and discovered that the structure underneath was more interesting.

He finished writing. He straightened.

He turned.

Their eyes met.

The noise of the entrance hall continued. Students shuffled past. A teacher
called out class numbers. Someone dropped a shoe and laughed. The cherry petals
blew through the open doors in a small, lazy eddy and settled on the tile floor.

None of this mattered.

Caspar was looking at her with an expression she had never seen on his face
before. Not surprise—his face didn't do surprise; it did adjustments,
micro-recalibrations, the aperture of his attention narrowing or widening by
fractions. This was something else. This was the look of someone who had walked
into a room expecting nothing and found the one thing they'd been refusing to
hope for.

His pale blue eyes held hers. His lips parted by a millimeter. His left hand,
still holding the pen, tightened almost imperceptibly around it.

Isabelle held his gaze. Her face was calm. Her posture was perfect. Her heart
was doing something structurally unsound.

Three seconds passed.

Four.

Five.

And then, because she was Isabelle van Rijn, and because she had spent a year
being trained by four of the most poised women in Asia, and because she would
rather die than let him see her fall apart in the entrance hall of a Tokyo
public high school at nine o'clock in the morning, she did the only thing she
could do.

She gave him a small, controlled nod.

Not cold. Not warm. Just: _I see you. I know you're here. We will deal with
this._

Caspar received the nod.

A beat passed. Something moved behind his eyes—a flicker, a decision being made
and locked into place.

He nodded back.

Same calibration. Same restraint. Same subtext: _I see you too._

Then he turned and walked toward the gymnasium, his bag over one shoulder, his
stride unhurried, his back straight, the falling cherry blossoms catching in his
dark ash-blonde hair like pale confetti at a funeral.

Isabelle watched him go.

She realized she was still holding the edge of the shoe locker. Her knuckles
were white.

She let go.

She breathed.

And then she followed him—seven meters behind, the exact distance between them
unchanged—into the first day of the rest of everything.

## VI.

The entrance ceremony at Sakuragaoka High was held in the gymnasium, a large,
echoey space with a scuffed hardwood floor and basketball hoops retracted
against the ceiling on hydraulic arms. Folding chairs had been set up in
rows—students in the front, parents and guardians in the back—and a stage at the
far end held a podium, a Japanese flag, and a spray of white flowers in a vase
that looked like it had been purchased at a home center.

Isabelle found her seat. Class 1-3. She was in the fourth row, near the aisle,
between a girl with a sharp bob who was sitting with her hands folded in her lap
like a newsreader, and a girl with her phone half-hidden under her thigh,
scrolling through something with her thumb.

Caspar was not in Class 1-3.

She knew this because she had scanned the class rosters posted on the board near
the entrance while maintaining a facial expression of polite disorientation, and
his name—Ryu Tae-sung—had been in Class 1-2. Adjacent, but separate.

_Of course,_ she thought. Because their parents were strategic, not stupid.
Putting them in the same class would have been too obvious. This way, it looked
like coincidence. _Oh, what a surprise, the Shin girl and the Ryu boy are at the
same school. How unexpected. What a small world._

She could almost hear her mother's assistant typing the email.

The ceremony began. The principal—a compact man with silver hair and a voice
that carried the gentle authority of someone who had been explaining school
rules for thirty years—welcomed the new students. He spoke about community.
About effort. About the cherry blossoms outside, which he used as a metaphor for
the fleeting beauty of youth, which Isabelle suspected he used every single
year. The teachers were introduced. The school song was sung. The student
council president—a third-year boy with aggressive posture and an undercut—gave
a speech about tradition and responsibility that was clearly memorized to the
punctuation marks.

Isabelle listened. She listened the way she always did—with her full attention,
absorbing not just the words but the spaces between them, the cultural
assumptions embedded in the phrasing, the particular rhythm of formal Japanese
ceremony that was so different from anything she'd grown up with in Europe. In
the Netherlands, a school welcome speech would have been informal, scattered
with jokes, maybe accompanied by someone's parent bringing in baked goods. In
Seoul, it would have been sharp and aspirational, framed around achievement.
Here, it was _communal_. It was about belonging to a group. About the
collective. About fitting in.

She was the tallest person in the student section by at least ten centimeters.
She was aware of this. She was aware of the glances—quick, curious, not
unkind—from the students around her. The girl with the bob had looked at her
twice, each time with an expression of controlled interest. The girl with the
phone had looked at her once, her eyes widening for just a moment before she
looked away and typed something rapidly with both thumbs.

After the ceremony, the new students were directed to their homerooms. The
hallways were narrow and loud, filled with the particular chaos of two hundred
teenagers trying to find rooms they'd never been in before. Isabelle followed
the signs for Class 1-3, walking against the current of students, her height
making her a fixed point in the flow, like a lighthouse.

The homeroom was on the second floor. It was a standard classroom—desks arranged
in rows, a blackboard at the front, windows along one wall overlooking the
courtyard and the cherry trees. The light came in at a low angle and striped the
desks with gold.

Isabelle found her assigned seat—third row from the back, second from the
window—and sat down. She placed her bag on the hook attached to the side of the
desk. She folded her hands. She looked out the window.

The cherry trees were shedding. The courtyard was turning white.

"Excuse me."

The voice was small and precise. Isabelle turned.

The girl with the sharp bob was standing beside the adjacent desk, one hand
resting on its surface. She had a narrow face, clear skin, and eyes that were
startlingly direct for someone whose voice was that quiet. She wore the school
uniform with an almost military neatness—blouse tucked, ribbon tied, not a
single pleat out of alignment.

"I'm Takahashi Yuki," the girl said. "I think I'm sitting next to you."

She gestured at the desk, where a small name card had been placed:
**高橋 ゆき.**

"Shin Ji-won," Isabelle said, offering a small bow from her seat. "Nice to meet
you."

"Shin—that's a Korean name?"

"Yes. I'm half-Korean, half-Dutch."

Yuki processed this. Her eyebrows rose by a fraction—not shock, not judgment,
just the recalibration of someone who liked to have her facts straight. "Dutch,"
she repeated. "You came from the Netherlands?"

"Most recently, from Seoul. But before that, yes."

"Your Japanese is good."

"Thank you. I've been studying for a while."

This was the filter in action. The slight hesitation before "a while." The
careful avoidance of anything that would sound too fluent, too idiomatic.
Isabelle watched Yuki's face for the small signs of relaxation that came when a
Japanese person decided they could speak normally around a foreigner who was
trying—the softening of the mouth, the shift from formal to polite-casual
register.

It came. Yuki sat down, smoothed her skirt over her knees, and placed a pencil
case on her desk with the precision of a surgeon laying out instruments.

"You're tall," Yuki said. Not as an observation. As an acknowledgment—the same
way you'd note the weather or the time. A fact to be filed.

Isabelle almost smiled. "Yes."

"Do you play volleyball? The school has a girls' team."

"I don't, actually. I used to do ballet."

Another fraction of an eyebrow. "Ballet."

"In Amsterdam. When I was younger."

Yuki nodded slowly. Something in her expression suggested she was building a
file—a mental dossier on the tall foreign girl who did ballet and came from the
Netherlands via Seoul and spoke Japanese with the careful politeness of someone
who was either very new or very deliberate. Isabelle suspected that Yuki's file
would be thorough, cross-referenced, and updated in real time.

She was going to like Yuki.

---

The homeroom teacher arrived. A woman in her forties named Ōtsuka-sensei, with a
warm face and a voice that split the difference between authority and
approachability. She welcomed them. She distributed class schedules. She
explained the cleaning rotation, the shoe-locker assignments, the cafeteria
procedures, the after-school club recruitment period that would begin next week.

And then she said: "Let's do self-introductions. Starting from the front row,
please stand, give your name, where you're from, and one thing you're interested
in."

A rustle of discomfort moved through the room. Self-introductions in Japanese
schools were a ritual—formulaic, brief, and governed by an unspoken rule that
you should be interesting enough to be remembered but not so interesting that
you stood out. It was a tightrope walk, and everyone knew it.

The first student stood. A boy with round glasses. "I'm Tanaka Hiroshi. I'm from
Setagaya. I like baseball."

Polite applause. He sat down. The next student stood.

"Yamamoto Sora." This was the girl who'd been scrolling her phone. She was
small, animated, with dyed brown hair that pushed the boundary of the school's
dress code and earrings she'd clearly removed just before entering the building,
the small red marks still visible on her earlobes. "I'm from Shibuya. I like
fashion and K-pop." She paused, grinned. "Especially VESPER."

Isabelle's expression did not change.

"They just debuted, and I've already streamed their single like three hundred
times." Sora clutched her hands to her chest. "Seo Yuna is my bias. She's
_unreal._"

Isabelle thought about Seo Yuna—the oldest member of VESPER, the one with the
low voice and the lazy cat-smile—sitting cross-legged on the practice room floor
at 2 AM, eating instant ramyeon with her training chopsticks and complaining
that her lower back hurt. She thought about Yuna stealing the last custard cream
from Isabelle's secret snack drawer and replacing it with a handwritten IOU on a
Post-it note that said _I owe you one (1) custard cream. Will repay in love and
loyalty. – Y_ with a small doodle of a cat.

She thought about how Seo Yuna was probably, at this exact moment, in a vocal
booth somewhere in Gangnam, doing her third take on a bridge vocal for the
group's second single, unaware that a girl in a Tokyo classroom was calling her
"unreal."

"That's nice," Isabelle said, when Sora sat back down and looked at her
expectantly, as if mentioning K-pop to the half-Korean girl was an invitation to
bond. "I've heard of them."

Sora's eyes widened. "You've _heard_ of them? They're—"

"Yamamoto-san," Ōtsuka-sensei said mildly. "Let's continue."

The introductions moved down the rows. Isabelle listened to each one, cataloging
names and interests and faces with the quiet discipline of someone trained to
learn a twelve-person choreography in two days. Yuki's introduction was a
masterpiece of controlled understatement: "Takahashi Yuki. Setagaya. I like
reading and organizational planning." Nothing else. The class applauded
politely. Yuki sat down and aligned her pencil case with the edge of her desk.

Then it was Isabelle's turn.

She stood. The room shifted—that small vacuum again, that intake of collective
attention. She was a head taller than the boy in front of her. Her hair caught
the window light and turned incandescent.

"I'm Shin Ji-won," she said. Her voice was steady, pitched carefully in the
middle register, the Japanese flowing clean and unhurried. "I'm half-Korean and
half-Dutch. I moved here from Seoul. I'm interested in baking."

She sat down.

A silence. Not a long one—two, maybe three seconds. But a silence nonetheless,
the kind that meant thirty-eight brains were simultaneously processing something
they hadn't expected and weren't sure how to categorize. The tall, startlingly
beautiful foreign girl who spoke calm Japanese and was interested in _baking._

"Thank you, Shin-san," Ōtsuka-sensei said.

From the desk beside her, Yuki leaned slightly toward her, her voice barely
above a whisper.

"Baking?"

"Mm. Pastry, mostly."

"What kind?"

"French. Some Viennese."

Yuki looked at her for a long moment. The file was being updated.

## VII.

The first break came at 10:40.

The room erupted into the particular social chaos of thirty-eight teenagers who
had been sitting still for ninety minutes and were now free to perform the
complex, instinctive work of forming alliances. Desks were pushed together.
Snacks appeared. Conversations ignited, overlapped, collapsed, reformed.

Sora materialized at Isabelle's desk like she'd been launched from a catapult.

"Shin-san. Ji-won-san? Can I call you Ji-won? You're from Seoul, right? Do you
actually listen to K-pop? Because I need someone to talk about VESPER with, and
Yuki—" She glanced at Yuki, who was reading a paperback novel with the focus of
someone ignoring a fire alarm. "—Yuki thinks pop music is a waste of cognitive
resources."

"I didn't say that," Yuki said, not looking up.

"You said, and I quote, 'I don't have the bandwidth for parasocial
relationships.'"

"That's different."

Isabelle watched them. There was something deeply, fundamentally _normal_ about
this exchange—the easy bickering, the teasing shorthand, the casual intimacy of
two people who had clearly known each other before today, probably from the same
middle school, probably from the same neighborhood. It was the kind of
friendship she had observed but never quite participated in—not in Wassenaar,
where her classmates were the children of ambassadors and executives, and not in
Switzerland, where friendship was conducted in five languages and mediated by
staff.

"You can call me Ji-won," Isabelle said. "And yes, I listen to some K-pop."

Sora's face lit up like a circuit completing. "Which groups? Wait—do you know
VESPER? You _have_ to know VESPER. Their debut was—" She pulled out her phone
and began scrolling with the speed of someone who had organized their photo
library by bias group. "Look, this is from their debut showcase—"

The screen showed four girls in coordinated black outfits, standing on a stage
bathed in blue-white light, their faces sharp and beautiful and impossibly
polished. The girl in the center—tall, with dark hair pulled back and eyes that
looked like they could cut glass—was mid-gesture, one hand extended toward the
camera, her expression caught between confidence and something softer.

That was Minji. Lee Min-ji. The leader. The one who woke up at 5 AM to stretch
and then made everyone breakfast and then cried during sad movies and then
denied crying and then cried again.

"They look talented," Isabelle said.

"Talented? Ji-won, they are the _future_—"

"Yamamoto-san." From the back of the room, a new voice. Quiet, observational,
with a slight rasp that suggested its owner did not speak often or loudly.
"You're going to scare her off on the first day."

Isabelle turned. A girl was sitting two rows back, near the wall, her chin
resting in her hand. She was small, unremarkable at first glance—brown hair in a
low ponytail, no accessories, uniform worn with the casual looseness of someone
who had stopped thinking about clothes a long time ago. But her eyes were sharp.
Dark and steady, with a quality of attention that reminded Isabelle of
Caspar—the sense that she was seeing more than she was showing.

"Ogawa Hina," the girl said, meeting Isabelle's gaze. "I sit back there.
Welcome."

"Thank you."

Hina nodded once, then returned to looking out the window with the serene
detachment of someone who had already decided how the social landscape of this
classroom was going to arrange itself and was waiting for everyone else to catch
up.

Isabelle looked at Yuki, who was still reading. She looked at Sora, who was now
showing VESPER dance practice videos to a mildly interested boy in the next row.
She looked at Hina, who was watching the cherry blossoms fall with the patience
of a painter waiting for the right light.

Something settled in her chest. Not warmth, exactly. Not comfort. Something more
provisional than that—the feeling of recognizing a space where she might,
eventually, be allowed to be a person instead of a performance.

Her phone buzzed in her bag.

She reached for it, angling the screen away from Sora's line of sight. A LINE
notification.

It was from Caspar.

One message. No preamble. No greeting. No context. Just five words, typed in
Korean, in the private language that belonged only to them:

> **류태성:** 네가 여기 있어.
>
> _You're here._

She stared at the screen. The characters blurred, then sharpened. The classroom
noise faded to a low hum. Her thumb hovered over the keyboard.

She typed:

> **신지원:** 너도.
>
> _You too._

She pressed send.

She put the phone away.

She looked out the window at the cherry blossoms, and for the first time in a
very long year, she let herself feel something without immediately trying to
name it.

_Chapter 1: End._
