# Chapter 11: Comeback

## I.

VESPER's second single dropped on a Friday at midnight.

Isabelle was awake. Not because she was waiting for it—she had heard the final
master three weeks ago, played through Bluetooth speakers in the Shin
Entertainment break room while a sound engineer explained the mix decisions to
her mother, who had listened with the expressionless focus of a woman evaluating
a product that represented eighteen months of development and several million
dollars of investment. Isabelle had sat in the corner, cross-legged on a
beanbag, wearing Jiwoo's practice hoodie, and listened to the song for the first
time in its finished form: the crisp, layered production; the vocal arrangement
that wove the four voices into a braid of harmony and counterpoint; the bridge
where Yuna's low register dropped into the mix like a stone sinking into water.

The song was called "Somebody Knows."

She was awake because she couldn't sleep. The week had been long—school, club
activities, two extra baking sessions (a batch of financiers for the table, a
practice run of mille-feuille that had collapsed during the fourth lamination
fold and that Auntie Choi had eaten in its entirety while murmuring "adequate"),
and the particular, low-grade exhaustion of maintaining two lives that ran on
parallel tracks and occasionally, with increasing frequency, threatened to
collide.

Her phone lit up at 12:01 AM.

The VESPER group chat detonated.

the ceiling and felt the particular compound emotion of being proud and homesick

for the practice room, happy that the song was beautiful, isolated because she

She read the message. _That's why._ That's why it was hard to believe. That's

careful handling.

## II.

By Monday morning, "Somebody Knows" had sixty-two million streams.

This number existed in Isabelle's awareness as a fact—a data point she received
via the VESPER group chat, where Yuna was posting hourly streaming updates with
the manic energy of a stockbroker watching a ticker, and via the Shin
Entertainment internal dashboard that her mother's assistant had shared with her
("for context," the assistant had written, which meant "your mother wants you to
see these numbers and feel the weight of what she built").

At school, the number existed differently. It existed as Sora.

Sora arrived at Monday morning homeroom in a state of transcendence.

"Ji-won. Yuki. Hina. Everyone. Listen. 'Somebody Knows.' Sixty-two million. In
_three days._ That's—that's the fastest-streaming K-pop comeback for a rookie group
in—I checked the records—in _two years._ They're charting in fourteen countries.
The music video has twenty-eight million views. Jiwoo's solo dance break is
trending globally. _Globally,_ Ji-won."

She was holding her phone in one hand and a printout—an actual printout, on
paper, produced from a home printer—of the streaming statistics in the other.
The printout was color-coded. There were graphs.

"Sora," Yuki said. "It's 8:15 in the morning."

"Time is irrelevant when history is being made, Yuki."

"Time is never irrelevant. That's what makes it time."

"The choreography," Sora continued, ignoring this with practiced ease. "Have you
seen the full dance practice video? It dropped yesterday. The choreography for
'Somebody Knows' is—it's completely different from 'Glass Hour.' It's more
mature, more individual, the formations are—"

"More complex," Isabelle said.

Sora looked at her. "Yes. Exactly. More complex. How did you know?"

"The teaser suggested it. The clips showed the members in separate positions
more often."

"Right! Right, and the movement vocabulary is—there's this turn sequence in the
second verse, where Minji does a full rotation and then transitions into a
floor-level movement, and the transition is so smooth it looks like—like she's
made of water."

Isabelle knew the transition. She had originated the transition. Park Jisoo had
built it from a sequence Isabelle had performed in the practice room—a ballet
turn modified for the contemporary style, the rotation controlled by the core,
the descent to floor level achieved through a controlled collapse of the knees
that looked effortless and required, in practice, approximately two hundred
repetitions to master.

"It's very clean," Isabelle said.

"'Very clean.' Ji-won, I'm going to start a tally of your understatements. I'm
going to make a graph. It's going to be a very tall graph."

The morning passed. Classes proceeded. The school's ordinary rhythms absorbed
the extraordinary fact of VESPER's ascent the way schools absorbed all external
cultural events—peripherally, incompletely, the awareness filtering through the
student body in uneven waves. Some students had heard the song. Some hadn't.
Some cared. Some didn't. The world outside the school gates was doing something
remarkable, and the world inside the school gates was doing math.

At lunch, the table convened.

Kenji had heard "Somebody Knows." He reported this with the enthusiasm of a
convert. "I listened to it on the train this morning. I listened to it _six
times._ The song is—it gets in your head. The chorus does this thing where—" He
hummed it. The melody was recognizable but pitched approximately a minor third
below the original key. "—it just _sticks._ I've been humming it all morning.
Morimoto-sensei asked me to stop during math."

"You were humming K-pop during math?" Sora said. Her expression was the
expression of someone watching a seed they'd planted begin to sprout.

"I wasn't humming K-pop. I was humming _VESPER._ There's a distinction."

"There really isn't."

"There is! VESPER is—VESPER is a _vibe._ VESPER is—" Kenji gestured broadly, his
hands encompassing the cafeteria, the school, the city, the concept of music
itself. "—VESPER is important."

Sora looked like she was going to cry. "Watanabe-kun."

"Yamamoto-san."

"Welcome to the fandom."

"I don't know if I'd call it a fan—"

"Welcome. To the fandom."

At the far end of the table, Caspar was eating his bento with the focused
attention of someone who was deliberately not participating in this
conversation. His face was the stoic mask—neutral, composed, the poker face that
the Alpenhof's head concierge would have approved of. But under the table, his
right knee was bouncing. A small, rhythmic motion—barely visible, controlled,
the physical manifestation of a person who was hearing his favorite group
discussed at his lunch table by people who did not know that he could perform
their choreography from memory and who definitely did not know that his
girlfriend had trained with them.

Isabelle placed her hand on his bouncing knee. Under the table. The contact
stilled him—the knee stopped, the rhythm absorbed into the warmth of her palm,
the kinetic energy converted into the specific, private heat of two people
sharing a secret in plain sight.

She squeezed once. _I know. I see you. It's okay._

He placed his hand over hers. _Thank you._

Ren, who had been eating his salmon in silence, said: "It's a good song."

The table turned to him. Ren offering a musical opinion was, in the social
physics of the group, the equivalent of a tectonic event.

"You listened to it?" Sora said.

"It was on the radio in the convenience store this morning. I heard it while
buying water." He paused. "The bridge is strong."

"Yuna's bridge," Sora confirmed.

"Yes. The vocalist has good control."

"Good—Ito-kun, she has _extraordinary_ control, she has—"

"She has good control," Ren repeated, in the tone of someone who had said what
he intended to say and was not going to elaborate. He returned to his salmon.

Daiki, who had been silent throughout, looked up from his phone. "The music
video's visual effects use a particle system that's similar to—"

"Daiki, no one cares about the particle system."

"Someone should."

Hina, at the edge of the table, ate her onigiri and watched everyone. Her gaze
moved around the group with the steady, sweeping attention of a lighthouse
beam—Kenji's enthusiasm, Sora's evangelical fervor, Ren's measured endorsement,
Daiki's technical fascination, Caspar's rigid composure, Isabelle's careful
equilibrium.

Hina's gaze lingered on Isabelle.

Hina's gaze moved to Caspar.

Hina's gaze returned to her onigiri.

She ate. She said nothing. But the shape of what she was seeing—the shape she
had identified weeks ago in the hallway, the expensive shape, the hidden
shape—was acquiring new contours. The VESPER connection. The gap year in Seoul.
The mother who ran an entertainment agency. The choreography that Isabelle
evaluated with professional precision while pretending to evaluate it with
friendly interest.

Hina saw the shape. She kept it to herself. This was her gift—not observation
alone (Yuki observed; Caspar observed; many people observed) but the rarer gift
of knowing which observations to keep.

## III.

Wednesday. The fan event.

Sora had been planning this for weeks—the VESPER fan event in Shibuya, the free
showcase that required early queuing, the event she'd invited Isabelle to on the
very first day of school, before she knew anything about Swiss boarding schools
or Korean housekeepers or the quiet architecture of a life built on foundations
that cost more than the building they were sitting in.

The event was scheduled for Saturday at 2 PM. Doors at noon. Sora's plan: arrive
at 9 AM, secure a front-row position, wait three hours, and experience what she
described as "the most important two hours of my life so far, with the
understanding that life is long and there may be more important hours later, but
I doubt it."

She presented this plan at Wednesday's combined lunch with the formal gravity of
a military briefing.

"I need volunteers," she said. "People willing to commit to a four-hour queue.
There will be no bathroom breaks, because bathroom breaks mean losing your spot,
and losing your spot means being behind the girls from Roppongi who I know are
also planning to queue early because I saw them coordinating on the fan forum."

"I'll come," Kenji said.

Sora looked at him. The speed of his response—instant, unhesitating, with no
apparent regard for the four-hour commitment or the lack of bathroom
breaks—registered on her face as a complex emotion that she did not appear to
know how to process. "You'll come?"

"Yeah. I like the music. And I've never been to a fan event. It sounds—" He
paused, selecting from a vocabulary that did not typically include the word he
was about to use. "—meaningful."

"Meaningful," Sora repeated.

"To you. It sounds meaningful to you. And I—" Kenji's ears turned pink. The
arterial shade from the first choreography lesson. "—I want to be there for the
things that are meaningful to my friends."

A silence. The kind of silence that happened when someone said something genuine
at a table that was accustomed to banter. The silence of recalibration—eight
people adjusting their understanding of Watanabe Kenji from "class clown" to
"class clown who occasionally, without warning, says things that make your chest
hurt."

"I'll come too," Isabelle said.

The table looked at her. Caspar looked at her. The look was brief—a flicker of
something, a question asked and answered in the shared frequency they had
developed—and then it was gone, replaced by the neutral composure of two people
who had agreed, at midnight in a LINE conversation, on how this would work.

Because they had discussed it. Sunday night, after the riverbank, after the
choreography lesson, after the train ride home with her head on his shoulder.
They had discussed the fan event the way they discussed everything—carefully,
precisely, with an awareness of the competing pressures and the potential
failure modes.

The calculus was simple: Sora wanted to go to the VESPER fan event. Isabelle
wanted to support Sora. But the fan event was a Shin Entertainment production,
and Shin Entertainment was Isabelle's mother's company, and the staff at the
event would include people who knew Isabelle's face, who had seen her in the
practice room, who knew that the tall Dutch-Korean girl standing in the fan
queue was the CEO's daughter and the group's unofficial fifth member.

Isabelle could not go to the front. She could not be recognized. She would
attend as a civilian—in the back, in the crowd, hidden by height difference and
a strategically lowered cap—and she would watch the group she loved perform from
the other side of the glass, as a fan instead of a family member, and she would
hold every emotion inside and let none of it reach her face.

Caspar had said: _"I'll come. I'll be right next to you."_

She had said: _"You don't have to."_

He had said: _"I want to."_

He had said: _"Besides, I want to see them perform live."_

She had laughed. He had laughed. Two fans. Two secrets. One event.

---

"Ryu-kun," Sora said, turning to Caspar. "You should come too."

"I'll be there," Caspar said.

Sora stared at him. The stare was the stare of a person whose social model was
being continuously updated by new data and who was beginning to suspect that the
tall, stoic boy from 1-2 was significantly more invested in VESPER than his
monosyllabic responses suggested.

"You'll be there," she repeated.

"Yes."

"Voluntarily."

"Yes."

"To see VESPER."

"To support my friends."

He glanced at Isabelle. The glance lasted half a second. It was, to anyone
except Hina, indistinguishable from a casual survey of the table. To Hina, it
was a complete sentence.

"Anyone else?" Sora looked around the table. "Yuki?"

"I'll observe from a distance," Yuki said. "I have reading to do."

"Yuki, you can read at a fan event. People do it."

"People do many things I choose not to replicate."

"Hina?"

Hina tilted her head. "I'll come."

Sora's eyes widened. Hina volunteering for a social event was, in the group's
experience, unprecedented. "You will?"

"I'm curious."

"About VESPER?"

"About the experience." Hina paused. "And about all of you experiencing it."

"Daiki? Ren?"

Daiki adjusted his glasses. "I'll analyze the visual production."

"That's a yes," Kenji translated.

Ren said: "I have a meet on Saturday."

"Excused," Sora said. "Your athletic commitments are respected by this table."

"Yuki?" Sora tried again.

Yuki looked up from her book. She looked at the table—at Sora's earnest face, at
Kenji's pink ears, at Isabelle's calm composure, at Caspar's neutral mask, at
Hina's quiet curiosity, at Daiki's analytical interest. She looked at the group
she had become part of without quite deciding to, the group that had formed
around the gravitational pull of two foreign students with expensive secrets and
that had become, despite its improbable composition, something real.

"Fine," Yuki said. "But I'm bringing my book."

## IV.

Friday evening. The night before the event.

Isabelle stood in the kitchen and made éclairs.

Not for the event—for the queue. Seven people standing in line for three hours
needed sustenance, and the kind of sustenance available at a Shibuya convenience
store was, in Isabelle's professional opinion, insufficient for a group that had
become accustomed to her output.

The éclairs were classic: choux pastry, piped into fingers, baked until golden
and hollow, filled with a crème pâtissière flavored with Madagascar vanilla, and
glazed with a dark chocolate fondant that set to a mirror-like sheen. She made
eighteen—enough for two each, plus extras for breakage and for Auntie Choi, who
had developed a particular fondness for the crème pâtissière and who would, if
left unsupervised, consume the entire batch of filling directly from the piping
bag.

As she piped the choux paste onto parchment-lined baking sheets—uniform fingers,
seven centimeters each, the spacing precise, the ridges from the round tip
smoothed with a wet finger because visible piping lines were a mark of
carelessness—her phone buzzed.

Caspar.

> **류태성:** 내일 걱정돼?

She stared at the screen. The question was precise and kind and it reached a
part of her that the careful architecture of her composure was designed to
protect—the part that missed them, that missed the practice room, that missed

hug Jiwoo and steal Yuna's snacks and tell Minji that the harmonies were perfect

She laughed. Alone in the kitchen at 10 PM, flour on her apron, choux paste
cooling on the baking sheets, the Tokyo night visible through the windows, she

She put the phone down. She put the éclairs in the oven. She set the timer. She
stood at the counter and looked at the dark kitchen window and saw, reflected in

expression on her face that she recognized from the photograph in her nightstand
drawer—the expression of someone who was about to be happy and who was, this
time, not afraid of it.

## V.

Saturday. Shibuya. 9 AM.

The venue was a mid-sized event space on a side street two blocks from Shibuya
Station—a building with a plain concrete exterior and a marquee that read, in
clean white text on black: **VESPER — "Somebody Knows" SHOWCASE EVENT.** A
banner hung below the marquee—the four members in their dark-academia concept
styling, the library set visible behind them, their faces sharp and composed and
impossibly beautiful.

Isabelle looked at the banner from across the street. Yuna's face, fifteen feet
tall, looking down at the Shibuya sidewalk through tortoiseshell glasses. Minji
beside her, angular and precise. Haeun's warm smile. Jiwoo's profile, one hand
raised, the fingers extended in the gesture that had come from Isabelle's ballet
training and had become, in the three weeks since the debut, one of the
most-referenced images in K-pop fan art.

She was wearing a cap—a plain navy baseball cap, pulled low, her hair tucked
into the back of a light jacket. No makeup except sunscreen. Dark jeans,
sneakers, the most anonymous version of herself she could assemble. Standing
next to Caspar—who was wearing a similar arrangement of nondescript clothing,
his hair hidden under a gray cap, the Leica deliberately left at home—she
looked, she hoped, like any other fan waiting in line.

The queue was already forty people long. Sora was vibrating.

"We're early enough," Sora said, assessing the line with the tactical eye of a
general surveying a battlefield. "If the venue holds two hundred, and they do
two sets—the showcase plus a meet-and-greet—we should be mid-section. Maybe
closer if people leave between sets."

"There's a meet-and-greet?" Kenji said.

"A high-five event. You walk past the members and they high-five you. It lasts
approximately 1.5 seconds per person. It is the most important 1.5 seconds of
most people's lives."

"One and a half seconds."

"One and a half seconds during which Seo Yuna's palm will touch my palm and I
will achieve a state of spiritual completion."

Kenji looked at Sora with an expression that contained multitudes—admiration,
bewilderment, a growing affection that he was not yet equipped to name. "You're
amazing," he said.

Sora blinked. "What?"

"Nothing. Where do we stand? Show me the optimal queue position."

They joined the line. Seven of them—Sora at the front, Kenji beside her, Daiki
behind them with his phone already recording observations, Yuki beside Daiki
with her book, Hina beside Yuki, and Caspar and Isabelle at the back, their caps
low, their faces angled down, two tall figures at the end of a queue of fans who
were, for the most part, significantly shorter and significantly louder and
significantly less burdened by the kind of secrets that made a simple fan event
feel like an intelligence operation.

Isabelle opened the éclair box.

The éclairs were distributed. The reaction was, at this point, familiar—the
initial surprise, the sensory assessment, the bite, the silence, the verdict
expressed through facial architecture rather than words. Kenji made a sound.
Sora photographed hers before eating it. Daiki examined the chocolate glaze with
professional interest. Yuki ate hers in three precise bites and said, "The crème
pâtissière is exceptionally smooth." Hina ate hers without comment but closed
her eyes during the second bite, which was, from Hina, the equivalent of a
standing ovation.

The hours passed. The queue grew behind them—eventually stretching around the
block, the line filling with fans holding lightsticks and photo cards and
hand-made signs. The energy was young, bright, crackling with anticipation.
Conversations overlapped in Japanese and Korean and English. Phone speakers
played "Somebody Knows" and "Glass Hour" in tinny, overlapping loops. The
morning sun moved across the Shibuya skyline, warming the concrete, casting
shadows that shortened and sharpened as noon approached.

At 11:30, the staff arrived.

Isabelle saw them before they saw her. A team of eight—event coordinators in
black T-shirts with the Shin Entertainment logo on the back, headsets on,
clipboards in hand. They emerged from the venue's side entrance and began moving
along the queue, distributing wristbands and explaining the event's rules.

She recognized two of them. Park Jihye, a junior coordinator who had been
assigned to VESPER's debut showcase and who had, on one occasion, asked Isabelle
to help carry a box of lightsticks from the storage room to the venue floor. And
Kim Taemin, a stage manager who had seen Isabelle in the practice room at least
a dozen times and who had once complimented her balance during a stage-blocking
rehearsal.

They were moving down the queue. Distributing wristbands. Getting closer.

Isabelle adjusted her cap. She angled her face toward Caspar, as if speaking to
him, using his height as a screen between her face and the approaching staff.

"다가오고 있어," she murmured in Korean. _They're coming._

Caspar shifted his position—a subtle adjustment, moving half a step to his left,
placing his body between Isabelle and the approaching coordinators. The movement
was unhurried, natural, the movement of a boyfriend adjusting his stance beside
his girlfriend, unremarkable to anyone who wasn't looking for it.

Park Jihye reached them. She was holding a stack of wristbands—blue paper bands
with the event logo printed in silver.

"Wristbands, please," she said, in Japanese, smiling the practiced smile of
someone who had been distributing wristbands since 6 AM. She held out two bands.

Caspar took them. "Thank you," he said.

Jihye's eyes moved to Isabelle—a reflexive glance, the brief visual check that
staff performed on every person in a queue, confirming presence and compliance.
The glance landed on Isabelle's face—partially obscured by the cap, angled away,
the features visible but shadowed.

The glance lasted half a second.

Jihye moved on.

Isabelle breathed. The wristband was on her wrist—blue paper, silver text, the
Shin Entertainment logo that she had seen on a thousand documents and business
cards and office walls. Her mother's logo. On her wrist. In a fan queue.

"괜찮아?" Caspar murmured. _You okay?_

"응." _Yeah._

"안 알아봤어." _She didn't recognize you._

"이번에는." _This time._

The queue moved. Noon arrived. The doors opened. The crowd surged forward—a
controlled surge, managed by the staff, the wristbands checked and counted and
the bodies channeled through the entrance into the venue's interior.

The venue was dark. Stage lights cut through the gloom—blue and white, sweeping
the space in slow arcs. A stage at the far end, raised, backed by a screen that
displayed the "Somebody Knows" concept art. Speakers mounted on rigging played
the intro track—an ambient, atmospheric piece that Isabelle recognized as the
album's interlude, a composition that Shin Entertainment's in-house producer had
created at 3 AM during a session that Isabelle had witnessed from a couch in the
control room, half-asleep, wrapped in a blanket, the bass frequencies vibrating
through the cushions into her bones.

They found positions in the mid-section. Sora had navigated the crowd with the
ruthless efficiency of a person who had been planning this moment for weeks,
securing a spot with a clear sightline to center stage. The seven of them stood
in a cluster—Sora and Kenji at the front, Yuki and Daiki behind them, Hina to
the side, and Caspar and Isabelle at the back, the tallest people in the venue,
their caps low, their faces in shadow, their hands linked between their bodies
where no one could see.

The lights dimmed. The crowd quieted. The interlude faded.

A beat. Two beats. Three.

The opening note of "Glass Hour" hit the speakers like a wave breaking. The
crowd erupted. Two hundred voices screaming, two hundred lightsticks raised, the
venue transforming in an instant from a dark room into a living thing—pulsing,
surging, the energy of collective anticipation cresting and breaking.

The four members of VESPER walked onto the stage.

Isabelle saw them.

Yuna—center-left, glasses gone, her dark hair pulled back, her stage presence
immediate and total, filling the space the way water filled a vessel.
Minji—right side, sharp, her jawline catching the stage light, her eyes scanning
the crowd with the practiced precision of a performer who was both present and
above. Haeun—back center, her smile warm even under the harsh blue lights, her
body already moving, always moving, the energy coiled and ready. And
Jiwoo—front-left, small, still, her face calm, her hands at her sides, the
fingers slightly extended—the extension, Isabelle's extension—waiting for the
music to tell her when to move.

The choreography began.

Isabelle watched. She watched with her whole body—the way she had watched in the
practice room, the way she watched everything that mattered, with the complete,
undivided attention of someone who understood every movement, every transition,
every breath mark and dynamic shift, because she had learned them alongside the
people performing them, because the choreography carried the imprint of her body
in its structure, because the dance was, in a way that no one in this venue
would ever know, partly hers.

Tears came. She didn't fight them. They arrived silently, rolling down her
cheeks in the dark, hidden by the cap, hidden by the crowd, hidden by the noise.
She was crying because they were beautiful. She was crying because she was
proud. She was crying because Jiwoo's solo dance break—the break she had helped
refine, the break that used the extension she had taught, the break that the
internet had called "ethereal"—was, seen live, in person, with the stage lights
and the bass and the crowd's roar, the most extraordinary thing she had ever
witnessed, and she was witnessing it from the audience, from the other side of
the glass, as a fan instead of a family member, and the distance between those
two identities was the distance between her public life and her private life,
and it was, in this moment, unbearable and beautiful and exactly right.

Caspar's hand tightened around hers. He was watching too. His face, in the
strobing stage light, was not stoic. It was not composed. It was open—the mask
down, the architecture suspended, the full, unguarded face of a boy who was
watching four women perform with a precision and a passion that had saved him
during the worst year of his life, and who was crying too, not visibly, not on
the outside—_only on the inside_, as he'd said—but with a fullness that Isabelle
could feel through his hand, through the pressure of his grip, through the way
his body was completely still in a room full of movement.

The set lasted forty minutes. Six songs. Two choreographies from the debut.
Three new songs. An encore of "Somebody Knows" that brought the crowd to a pitch
of intensity that made the floor vibrate.

When it ended—when the lights came up and the stage emptied and the crowd
exhaled—Sora was crying openly, Kenji's arm around her shoulders in a gesture so
natural it appeared to surprise both of them, and Yuki had closed her book and
was standing very still with an expression that suggested she was experiencing
an emotion she had not allocated bandwidth for and was in the process of
reclassifying her entire taxonomy of human experience.

Daiki was looking at his phone. "The light design used a tri-color LED array
with—"

"Not now, Daiki," Kenji said, gently.

Hina was watching Isabelle. In the post-show light—the venue's fluorescents
harsh after the stage's drama—Hina could see the tracks on Isabelle's cheeks.
The tears she hadn't wiped. The cap pulled low. The hand gripping Caspar's.

Hina said nothing. She looked, and she understood, and she kept what she
understood in the place where she kept all the shapes she saw—the quiet, private
archive of a girl who noticed everything and chose, with deliberate care, what
to reveal.

## VI.

The high-five event.

The line formed quickly—two hundred fans arranged in a single file that wound
through the venue's interior, terminating at a long table on the stage where the
four members of VESPER sat, flanked by staff, their faces bright and
professionally welcoming, their hands extended.

Sora was trembling. Literally trembling—her hands shaking, her breathing
shallow, the physiological response of someone approaching an experience they
had imagined a thousand times and that was now, finally, real.

"I can't," she said. "I can't do this. My hands are sweating. What if she thinks
I'm weird? What if I can't speak?"

"You can speak," Kenji said. "You are, in fact, incapable of not speaking. This
is one of your best qualities."

"What do I say? I have 1.5 seconds. That's—that's enough for maybe five words.
What are the five most important words I can say to Seo Yuna?"

"How about 'I love your music'?"

"That's four words. I have one left."

"Add 'lots.'"

"'I love your music lots.' That sounds like a child wrote it."

"You could say nothing and just experience it," Yuki suggested.

"Yuki, if I say nothing, I will regret it for the rest of my natural life."

The line moved. They shuffled forward. Isabelle's heart was beating in a pattern
she didn't recognize—not the steady rhythm of performance or the accelerated
pulse of proximity to Caspar, but something arrhythmic, irregular, the heartbeat
of a person who was walking toward four women she loved and who would have to
look into their faces and pretend she was a stranger.

She could leave. She could step out of the line. She could say she was tired, or
that her stomach hurt, or any of the small social lies that would allow her to
exit without explanation. Caspar would cover for her. The group would
understand.

But Sora was in the line. And Sora was nervous. And Sora had been planning this
for weeks. And Sora's hands were shaking. And Isabelle had decided, somewhere
during the performance, that being here—being in the line, being a fan, being
present for her friend's most important 1.5 seconds—mattered more than the risk.

The line moved. They reached the stage. The table was there—the four members
seated behind it, a staff member at each end managing the flow. The procedure
was simple: walk past, make brief eye contact, extend your hand, receive the
high-five, keep moving.

Sora went first.

She walked to the table. She reached Yuna. She extended her hand. Yuna
smiled—the lazy cat-smile, the one Isabelle knew by heart—and their palms met.

"I love your music," Sora said. Her voice cracked on _music._ "And you're
incredible. Thank you."

That was more than five words. The staff member at the end of the table was
already gesturing for her to move along. But Yuna—because Yuna was Yuna, because
Yuna operated on instinct and warmth and the absolute refusal to treat any fan
as a transaction—leaned forward and said:

"Thank you for being here. Your energy is beautiful."

Sora made it three steps past the table before the tears came. Kenji was
waiting. He put his arm around her shoulders again—the same natural gesture, the
same unconscious ease—and guided her to the side while she cried silently into
her hands.

Kenji went next. He high-fived each member with the enthusiasm of a man greeting
old friends. He said something to Minji that made her laugh—Isabelle couldn't
hear what—and he reached the end of the table grinning.

Yuki went next. She high-fived each member with precise, formal courtesy, as if
performing a diplomatic handshake. She said "Thank you for your work" to each
one, the same phrase four times, delivered with the systematic consistency that
was Yuki's love language.

Daiki went next. He said nothing. He high-fived. He moved on. Efficient.

Hina went next. She high-fived each member with her calm, unhurried presence.
When she reached Jiwoo—the youngest, the quiet one, the one whose extension came
from a Dutch-Korean girl who was standing four places behind in the line—Hina
paused. A fraction of a second. She looked at Jiwoo's hand, extended for the
high-five, and she looked at Jiwoo's face, and something passed between them
that Isabelle couldn't read from four bodies away.

Then Hina high-fived and moved on.

Caspar went next. He walked past the four members with his cap low and his face
neutral. He high-fived Yuna, Minji, Haeun, Jiwoo—four contacts, each one lasting
less than a second, his hand meeting theirs and releasing, the stoic face giving
nothing away, the fan inside him screaming at a frequency only Isabelle could
hear.

Then it was Isabelle's turn.

She walked to the table. She kept her head slightly down. The cap shadowed her
face. She extended her hand toward Yuna.

Their palms met.

Yuna's hand was warm. Small. The hand that Isabelle had held during a late-night
practice session when Yuna's voice had cracked and she'd been afraid she was
losing her instrument and Isabelle had sat with her on the practice room floor
and said, in Korean, _"Your voice is the most beautiful sound I've ever heard,"_
and Yuna had cried, and Isabelle had held her hand exactly like this, palm to
palm, and said, _"It's going to be okay."_

Yuna looked up. The lazy cat-smile began its automatic deployment.

The smile stopped.

Yuna's eyes widened—a fraction, a millimeter, the dilation so small it would
have been invisible to anyone who didn't know her face the way Isabelle knew her
face. A flash of recognition. A half-second of _what are you doing here, why are
you in the line, what is happening—_

Isabelle shook her head. A micro-movement. Imperceptible to the crowd, to the
staff, to the other members. But visible to Yuna, who was looking at her with
the specific, concentrated attention of someone who had just received a message
in a language only two people spoke.

_Don't. Not here. I'll explain later._

Yuna's smile completed its deployment. It changed—subtly, privately, the corners
lifting by a degree that only Isabelle would recognize as different from the
standard fan-event smile. The smile became real. Became personal. Became: _I see
you. I don't understand. But I see you, and I love you, and we will talk about
this later, and you owe me an explanation and also probably madeleines._

"Thank you for coming," Yuna said. The same words she'd said to two hundred
fans. But the tone was different. The tone was for Isabelle.

Isabelle moved on. Minji. Haeun. Their high-fives were warm and professional and
they did not recognize her—the cap, the lack of makeup, the context of a fan
event rather than a practice room. They saw a tall fan. They smiled. They
high-fived.

Jiwoo.

Isabelle reached Jiwoo. She extended her hand. Jiwoo looked up—the quiet eyes,
the calm face, the girl who leaned her head against Isabelle's shoulder without
words.

Jiwoo's hand met hers.

The recognition was instant. Not visual—tactile. Jiwoo recognized Isabelle's
hand. The shape of it, the size, the specific pattern of calluses on the palm
that came from whisking and folding and the particular grip of a pastry chef's
hands. Jiwoo had held this hand in the practice room. She knew it the way
dancers knew each other's bodies—through touch, through proximity, through the
physical vocabulary of shared movement.

Jiwoo's eyes widened. Her lips parted.

Isabelle squeezed once. _Not here._

Jiwoo closed her mouth. She squeezed back. The squeeze lasted a fraction of a
second longer than the standard high-five—a fraction that the staff didn't
notice and the crowd didn't see and that contained, in its brevity, the full
weight of ten months of shared exhaustion and shared joy and the specific,
irreplaceable bond of two people who had danced side by side until their bodies
spoke the same language.

Isabelle released. She walked past the table. She walked off the stage. She
walked to where Caspar was standing, at the edge of the crowd, his cap low, his
hand extended toward her.

She took his hand. She gripped it—hard, harder than she meant to, the pressure
of every suppressed emotion, every held-back word, every tear she hadn't let
herself cry since the performance ended, compressed into the force of her
fingers around his.

"밖으로 나가자," she said. _Let's go outside._

They walked out of the venue. Into the Shibuya afternoon. Into the noise and the
light and the irreducible, beautiful chaos of Tokyo on a Saturday.

Isabelle pulled off the cap. Her hair fell free—the honey-colored waves, the
light catching them, the full, undisguised Isabelle emerging from behind the
anonymity of the fan.

She looked at Caspar. He was looking at her with the expression she had come to
know as his truest one—not the stoic mask, not the photographer's analytical
gaze, but the face of a boy who saw her completely and loved what he saw.

"유나가 알아봤어," she said. _Yuna recognized me._

"알아." _I know._

"지우도." _Jiwoo too._

"알아." _I know._

"나중에 설명해야 돼. 왜 팬 줄에 있었는지. 누구랑 같이 왔는지." _I'll have to
explain later. Why I was in the fan line. Who I came with._

"그래." _Yeah._

"그 사람들한테 네 얘기 하게 될 거야." _I'm going to end up telling them about
you._

"괜찮아." _That's okay._

She looked at him. "VESPER한테 내 남자친구 얘기 하는 거 진짜 괜찮아?" _You're
really okay with me telling VESPER about my boyfriend?_

The corner of his mouth moved. The tectonic precursor. The shift.

"이자벨." _Isabelle._

"응." _Yeah._

"나 걔네 팬이야. 당연히 괜찮아." _I'm their fan. Of course it's okay._

She laughed. She pulled him close—her arms around his neck, her face against his
shoulder, her body shaking with laughter and relief and the specific, compound
joy of being known completely by someone who thought the funniest part of the
whole situation was that his girlfriend's idol-group sisters were about to find
out about him.

"유나가 난리 날 거야," she said, her voice muffled against his jacket. _Yuna is
going to lose her mind._

"알아." _I know._

"사진 요구할 거야. 프로필. 키. 혈액형." _She'll demand photos. A profile. Your
height. Your blood type._

"A형." _Type A._

She pulled back. She looked at him. "방금 자기 혈액형 말한 거야?" _Did you just
tell me your blood type?_

"유나한테 전해줘." _Pass it along to Yuna._

She kissed him. On a Shibuya side street, in the afternoon light, her cap in her
hand and his arms around her waist and the sound of the venue still thumping
behind them and the entire complicated, beautiful, impossible architecture of
their lives pressing in from every side.

When they separated, the rest of the group was emerging from the venue. Sora's
face was blotched with dried tears and incandescent with joy. Kenji's arm was
still around her shoulders. Yuki was carrying her book in one hand and a VESPER
lightstick in the other—a lightstick she had apparently acquired at some point
during the event and was now holding with the same precise, neutral grip she
used for everything, as if it were a piece of equipment rather than a
declaration of fandom.

Hina walked out last. She looked at Isabelle and Caspar—standing together, his
arm around her, her face flushed and bright, the cap off, the disguise shed.

Hina looked at Isabelle's face. She looked at the venue behind them. She looked
at the VESPER banner on the marquee.

And then Hina smiled. A real smile. The rarest expression in the group—rarer
than Caspar's, rarer than Yuki's ghost-smile, the smile of someone who had been
watching a story unfold from the beginning and who had just, in this moment,
seen the shape of it complete.

She said nothing.

She didn't need to.

_Chapter 11: End._
