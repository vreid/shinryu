# Chapter 14: Exhibition

---

## I.

The photography exhibition was scheduled for Friday, the last day of May.

Caspar spent the two weeks before it in a state of controlled obsession that
Isabelle recognized because she had lived inside a version of it herself—the
particular focus that descended when a project crossed from preparation into
execution, when the variables narrowed and the deadline hardened and the only
remaining question was whether the work was good enough.

He printed at the Ebisu lab. Eighteen images, selected from five rolls of
film—three from Yanaka, two from the daily Tokyo walks that had become his
practice, the way morning runs were Ren's practice and evening baking was
Isabelle's. The images were printed on heavyweight matte paper in two sizes: A4
for the wall display, A5 for the prints that would be sold. The lab
technician—the same man from the first visit, the one who had noted the
excellent exposure on Isabelle's portraits—printed them with the care of a
craftsperson handling material he respected.

The other club members contributed too. Fujita submitted seven images—landscapes
and architectural studies, well-composed, technically solid, the work of someone
who had been learning for three years and who was approaching the level where
technique stopped being a concern and vision began. The two second-year girls
submitted four images each—a series of flower close-ups and a set of portraits
of their friends. The first-year boy submitted three images of his cat, which
were, Caspar had to admit, surprisingly good—the cat's expression in the third
image conveyed a depth of existential weariness that bordered on philosophical.

Total inventory: thirty-six images for display. One hundred twenty A5 prints for
sale, bundled in sets of three for 1,500 yen per set—a price point that Isabelle
had calculated based on what she called a "student affordability analysis,"
which was, as far as Caspar could tell, a spreadsheet she had built in twenty
minutes that modeled the average weekly allowance of a Japanese high school
student against the perceived value of a physical photograph in a digital age.

"The key variable is impulse," she had explained, sitting cross-legged on his
bedroom floor while he sorted negatives at his desk. "1,500 yen is high enough
to feel like a real purchase but low enough that a student can buy it without
thinking too hard. Three prints per set creates the illusion of value—they're
not buying one thing, they're buying a collection. And the physical format
matters. Nobody pays for a digital image. But a printed photograph on good
paper—that's an _object._ That's something you can pin to your wall. It has
weight."

He had looked at her. She was wearing one of his T-shirts—a dark gray crew neck,
oversized on her, the hem falling to mid-thigh over her jeans—and her hair was
tied back with his camera strap's spare clip, and she was discussing pricing
strategy with the clinical precision of someone whose father ran a global
banking empire, and the combination of the borrowed shirt and the strategic
intellect had done something to his chest that he had chosen not to examine.

"You should run a company," he had said.

"I'm fifteen."

"So was your mother when she started."

She had looked at him. The look had contained something he couldn't photograph—a
flicker of recognition, of pleasure, of the specific gratitude that came from
being seen not as a daughter or a trainee or a student but as a person whose
mind worked in ways that were valuable and rare.

"Maybe someday," she had said. "After the madeleines."

---

The exhibition setup began on Thursday afternoon. The east hallway on the first
floor was commandeered—Fujita had secured permission from the vice-principal,
who had approved the event with the bemused enthusiasm of an administrator who
had never received a formal proposal from the photography club and who seemed
quietly delighted that someone was using the east hallway for something other
than storing cleaning supplies.

The display was Caspar's design. He approached it the way he approached
everything—with the spatial awareness of a photographer and the labor ethic of a
porter. The prints were mounted on foam board, hung from temporary wire at eye
level, spaced at precise intervals that gave each image room to breathe without
wasting wall space. He did this with a small hammer and a box of picture hooks
and the focused silence of someone who was building something that mattered.

The group helped. Kenji arrived after comedy club and appointed himself Head of
Logistics, a title he had invented and that consisted primarily of carrying foam
boards from the art room to the hallway and offering motivational commentary.
Ren helped hang prints—his height useful for the upper wire—and said nothing
while doing it, which was his highest form of contribution. Daiki built a small
sign for the sales table using the school's printer and a design template he had
coded in thirty minutes. Sora designed a poster—hand-drawn, colorful, featuring
a stylized camera and the text "PHOTOGRAPHY CLUB EXHIBITION & CHARITY SALE" in
lettering that managed to be both artistic and legible.

Yuki organized the sales operation. She arrived with a cash box, a receipt book,
and a spreadsheet she had prepared on graph paper that tracked inventory, sales,
and revenue in real time. The spreadsheet was color-coded. There were formulas.

"This is the most organized charity sale in the history of Japanese education,"
Kenji said, looking at the spreadsheet.

"Organization is not optional when money is involved," Yuki said.

Hina arrived last. She walked the length of the exhibition slowly, looking at
each photograph, her hands clasped behind her back, her expression—as
always—calm, attentive, seeing things that others would need to be told about.

She stopped at Caspar's Yanaka series.

The three prints hung in sequence: the temple gate, the bicycle shadow on the
stone wall, and the moss-covered grave marker. They were black and white—Tri-X
400, high contrast, the blacks rich and absolute, the whites clean and precise.
The compositions were spare, almost architectural, each image reduced to its
essential geometry: line, shadow, texture. They looked like the work of someone
who understood that the most powerful thing a photographer could do was decide
what to leave out.

"These are very good," Hina said.

Caspar, who was hanging the last print at the far end of the hallway, heard her.
He turned. "Thank you."

"The grave marker. The way the moss creates a second surface on the stone. It
looks like the stone is growing something."

He looked at her. Hina's observation was precise—not a compliment, not a
reaction, but an _analysis_, the identification of the specific visual mechanism
that made the image work. She saw the way he saw. Not through a viewfinder, not
through the language of photography, but through the same quality of
attention—the willingness to look at something long enough to see what it was
actually doing instead of what it appeared to be doing.

"That's exactly what I saw," he said.

Hina nodded. She moved on to the next print.

Isabelle watched this exchange from the sales table, where she was helping Yuki
arrange the A5 print sets in neat rows. She watched Hina's slow, careful
progress along the wall, and she thought: _Hina sees the way Caspar sees. And
Yuki sees the way I see—in systems, in patterns, in the architecture of how
things connect. And Sora sees the way the VESPER fans see—with passion, with
immediacy, with the full-body engagement of someone who loves something without
reservation. And Kenji sees the way the Alpenhof barista saw—with energy, with
openness, with the instinct that the most important thing about a person is the
thing they care about most._

She thought: _We chose well._

## II.

Friday. Exhibition day.

The east hallway opened at lunch. Students drifted in—curious, attracted by
Sora's poster, drawn by the novelty of a first-floor event that wasn't a fire
drill. They entered in ones and twos and small clusters, their indoor shoes
squeaking on the linoleum, their eyes adjusting from the fluorescent brightness
of the classrooms to the warmer, more deliberate lighting of the exhibition
space. Fujita had rigged two clip-on lights from the art room to the display
wire, angling them so the prints were illuminated from above, the way gallery
lighting worked—the images glowing against the institutional beige of the
hallway walls.

The first student to buy a print set was a second-year girl who had come in with
her friends, glanced at the display, stopped at one of Caspar's images—a vending
machine at dawn, its blue-white glow isolated against a gray cityscape—and said,
to no one in particular, "That's beautiful."

She bought a set. 1,500 yen, inserted into Yuki's cash box, recorded in Yuki's
spreadsheet, receipt issued.

The second student bought a set of Fujita's landscapes. The third bought the cat
photos. The fourth bought another of Caspar's—the Daikanyama café storefront,
the light catching the wooden door.

By the end of lunch, they had sold fourteen sets.

By the end of the after-school period, when the exhibition reopened for its
evening session—Fujita had negotiated extended access to the hallway until 6 PM,
arguing, successfully, that art required time—they had sold twenty-nine sets.

Caspar's images accounted for nineteen of them.

This was not a surprise to anyone in the group. What surprised them—what
surprised even Fujita, who had been advocating for Caspar's work since the day
he joined—was the way people responded. They didn't just buy. They _stood_. They
stood in front of the images and looked at them with the sustained, focused
attention that Caspar brought to the act of taking them, as if the photographs
were transmitting something—a quality of seeing, a depth of attention—that the
viewers could feel without being able to name.

A teacher stopped. Then another teacher. The vice-principal came through, looked
at the Yanaka series, and stood in front of the grave marker for forty-five
seconds without speaking.

"Ryu-kun," the vice-principal said. He was a thin man in a brown suit, with the
careful, unhurried speech of someone who had been managing a school for twenty
years and who had learned that the most important part of his job was noticing
things. "How long have you been photographing?"

"About a year."

"A year." The vice-principal looked at the print again. "This is a year's work?"

"This is the work I've selected from a year."

"That distinction matters." The vice-principal nodded. "The selection is the
art, isn't it? Not just seeing—deciding what's worth showing."

Caspar looked at the vice-principal with the faint surprise of someone
encountering understanding in an unexpected place. "Yes, sir."

"Continue, please." The vice-principal moved on.

Fujita, standing at the end of the hallway, looked like he was going to
levitate. He was holding his camera—the DSLR, the one he carried everywhere—and
he was photographing the exhibition, which was the photography club president's
equivalent of the moment a parent sees their child perform in public and cannot
contain the emotion.

At 5:45 PM, Yuki closed the cash box. She counted the money. She updated the
spreadsheet. She performed a final reconciliation.

"Total revenue," Yuki announced, to the group assembled around the sales table.
"43,500 yen."

The hallway was quiet.

"How much?" Kenji said.

"43,500 yen. Twenty-nine sets at 1,500 yen each."

43,500 yen. More than enough for Ren's spikes. More than enough for the fund.

Kenji looked at Ren. Ren looked at the floor. His hands were at his sides, his
fingers slightly curled, the posture of someone who was receiving information
that he hadn't prepared to process and who was managing the reception with the
only tool he had: stillness.

"Ren," Kenji said. Gently. Kenji's gentleness—rare, specific, deployed only in
moments that required it—had a physical quality, a softness in his voice that
was the acoustic opposite of his normal volume and that communicated, through
its contrast, the seriousness of what he felt.

Ren didn't look up. He was looking at the floor—the linoleum, the scuffed
surface, the institutional gray of a public school hallway where, for reasons he
didn't fully understand, a group of people he had known for six weeks had
organized an art exhibition to buy him running shoes.

"The fund will be administered through the school office," Isabelle said. Her
voice was calm, practical, the voice of someone presenting a system rather than
a gift. "Ōtsuka-sensei submitted the inter-club support application on Monday.
The administration approved it yesterday. The photography club's proceeds go
into the school athletics support fund. Any member of any athletic club can
apply for equipment support. The track team coach just needs to submit a request
form."

She paused. "I happen to have a blank request form, if anyone needs one."

She held it out. A single sheet of paper—A4, printed, the school's official
letterhead at the top, the form fields blank, waiting to be filled.

Ren looked at the form. He looked at Isabelle. He looked at Caspar, who was
standing beside the display, his hands in his pockets, his expression neutral,
his pale blue eyes steady and warm.

He looked at the group—at Kenji and Sora and Yuki and Daiki and Hina and Fujita,
who was still holding his camera, and the two second-year girls, who were
packing up their unsold prints, and the first-year boy with the cat photos, who
was sitting on the floor eating a rice ball.

"You did this," Ren said.

"The school did this," Isabelle said. "The photography club raised the money.
The school administers the fund. The track team applies. The system supports its
athletes."

"Isabelle." Ren used her first name. Not Shin-san. Not Ji-won. _Isabelle._ The
name he had never used before, the name that belonged to the private version of
her, the version that existed behind the filter. He used it now because the
moment required honesty, and honesty required the real name.

"You did this," he said again.

She looked at him. She could deny it. She could maintain the system's anonymity,
preserve the architecture, keep the mechanism invisible. But Ren was looking at
her with an expression she recognized—the expression of someone who understood
exactly what had happened and who was choosing, out of respect for the truth and
for the people who had enacted it, to acknowledge it without dismantling it.

"We did this," she said. "All of us."

Ren nodded. A single nod. The weight of it—the gratitude, the pride, the
particular emotion of a competitor who had been given the equipment he needed
not through charity but through the organized effort of people who believed he
deserved to compete at his best—settled into the nod and stayed there.

He took the form.

"Thank you," he said.

Two words. Maximum Ren. But the two words carried everything—the runs, the
training, the early mornings, the prefectural meet in two weeks, the spikes that
would carry him through the 800 and the 1500 without the lateral ankle stress,
the knowledge that his friends had seen a problem and solved it and done it in a
way that let him receive the solution without losing anything.

Kenji put his arm around Ren's shoulders. Ren did not shrug it off. This was, in
the body language of their friendship, the equivalent of a tearful embrace.

"HCCL," Kenji said. "We've got your back."

## III.

Saturday morning. Caspar went to work.

The Ryu Retail stockroom in Ginza occupied the basement level of a seven-story
building whose upper floors held one of Tokyo's most exclusive cosmetics and
lifestyle retail spaces—a temple of glass and polished stone where products were
displayed like museum pieces and customers were attended to by staff whose
training included courses in color theory, skin chemistry, and the art of making
someone feel that a 15,000-yen moisturizer was not a purchase but an investment
in their future self.

The basement was different.

The basement was fluorescent-lit, concrete-floored, and organized with the
industrial efficiency of a warehouse operation that processed two hundred
shipments per week. Steel shelving ran floor to ceiling. Cardboard boxes were
stacked in columns labeled with inventory codes. The air smelled like packing
material and the faint, residual sweetness of beauty products that had leaked
during transit.

Caspar worked here every Saturday from 8 AM to 1 PM.

He arrived through the staff entrance—a plain metal door at the back of the
building, accessed from a service alley that smelled like kitchen exhaust from
the restaurant next door. He changed into his work uniform in the staff locker
room: a gray polo shirt embroidered with the Ryu Retail logo, dark trousers,
safety shoes. The uniform was the same one worn by every stockroom
worker—identical, anonymous, the sartorial equivalent of the hotel porter's
vest. He clipped his employee badge to his chest pocket: **Ryu Tae-sung,
Part-Time Inventory Associate.**

His supervisor was a man named Oda—mid-forties, compact, with a shaved head and
the patient demeanor of someone who had managed stockroom operations for fifteen
years and who approached every shift with the calm inevitability of a man who
had seen everything that cardboard boxes could do and was no longer surprised by
any of it.

Oda knew who Caspar was. Of course Oda knew. The name _Ryu_ was on the building.
It was on the company's registration documents, on the annual report, on the
brass plaque in the lobby that read **Ryu Retail — A Division of Ryu BioPharma &
Chemical, Est. 1987.** Caspar's father was the chairman. His face appeared in
the corporate brochure. The family resemblance—the jaw, the cheekbones, the
particular way the Ryu men held themselves—was unmistakable to anyone who had
attended a company meeting.

Oda handled this knowledge the way a bomb disposal expert handled an unexploded
ordnance: carefully, quietly, with the absolute minimum of sudden movements. He
did not treat Caspar differently. He did not give him easier tasks. He did not
call him Ryu-sama or bow more deeply or adjust his management style by a single
degree. He treated him like what his badge said he was: a part-time inventory
associate, fifteen years old, here to scan boxes and stock shelves and learn
what a retail operation looked like from the concrete up.

"Morning, Ryu-kun."

"Morning, Oda-san."

"Shipment from Osaka arrived at seven. Eighty-four boxes. Skincare line—the new
ceramide range. I need them scanned, cataloged, and shelved in Zone C by noon."

"Understood."

Caspar scanned. The work was repetitive—open box, scan barcode, verify contents
against the manifest, log the entry in the inventory system, shelve the product
in the correct location. He performed it with the same precision he brought to
developing film: methodical, unhurried, each step completed fully before the
next began. The hotel had trained this into him—the understanding that
repetitive work was not mindless work, that each iteration was an opportunity
for accuracy, that the quality of a process was determined not by the first box
or the hundredth box but by every box in between.

He scanned. He shelved. The ceramide range was packaged in white boxes with the
Ryu Retail logo in silver—a clean, minimalist design that bore, Caspar noted, a
strong resemblance to the Waldstein Botanicals aesthetic. Both companies
occupied the same market tier. Both companies valued the same visual language.
Both companies were, according to a brand partnership proposal on a desk in a
Roppongi office building, about to become much more closely aligned.

He thought about this while shelving. He thought about the corporate
architecture—the families, the companies, the merger subtext, the way his hands
were stacking boxes that bore his father's name in a basement that his father
owned, and how this was both absurd and instructive, and how the hotel had given
him the ability to hold both of those things simultaneously without either one
winning.

yen per set and that had, through a system designed by his girlfriend, funded a
pair of running spikes for a boy who deserved to compete.

He thought: _This is what it means to be useful._

He thought: _This is what privilege pays back._

## IV.

Sunday. The day before the prefectural meet.

Ren came to school to pick up his spikes.

The spikes had been ordered through the school's athletics support
fund—processed by the administration office, approved by the track team coach,
delivered to the school in a plain cardboard box that bore no indication of its
origin beyond the manufacturer's name and a shipping label from a sporting goods
distributor. The system had worked exactly as Isabelle had designed it: the
money flowed from the photography exhibition through the school fund through the
administration office to the sporting goods order, each step documented, each
step legitimate, each step invisible.

Ren opened the box in the track team's equipment room. Caspar was not there.
Isabelle was not there. Neither of them needed to be—the system had done its
work, and the system did not require acknowledgment.

But Kenji was there. And Kenji, who understood the emotional architecture of the
moment even if he didn't understand the financial architecture behind it, stood
beside Ren while he opened the box and said nothing.

The spikes were inside. Carbon-fiber plate, lightweight mesh upper, the specific
model the coach had recommended. They were black with a thin white
stripe—understated, functional, the visual equivalent of Caspar's unbranded
backpack or Isabelle's convenience-store umbrella. Tools that didn't announce
themselves.

Ren picked one up. He turned it in his hand—feeling the weight, testing the flex
of the plate, pressing his thumb against the foam of the heel to check the
density. The foam was firm. New. Uncompressed. The heel drop was correct. The
kinetic chain from ankle to knee would be stable.

He put them on.

He stood. He shifted his weight from one foot to the other—the runner's
assessment, the body's confirmation of what the hands had already tested. The
fit was right. The support was right. The plate's energy return was evident in
the way the shoe responded to his weight shift—a slight spring, a readiness, the
mechanical promise of forward motion.

"Well?" Kenji said.

Ren looked at him. His expression was—for Ren—extraordinary. Not a smile,
exactly. Not gratitude, exactly. Something that combined both and transcended
them: the expression of a competitor who had been given what he needed to
compete, and who was going to use it.

"They're good," Ren said.

"They're good." Kenji grinned. "They better be. They cost forty-three thousand
yen's worth of photography."

"Forty-three thousand five hundred."

"Forty-three thousand five hundred yen's worth of photography and one very
emotional vice-principal."

Ren almost smiled. The muscles engaged. The expression formed, held for one
second, and resolved back into the baseline. But Kenji saw it. And Kenji, who
had been tracking the smile frequencies of his friends since the beginning—who
had noticed Caspar's three-hundred-percent increase, who had noticed Isabelle's
gradual thaw, who noticed everything because noticing was the real skill behind
the loudness—saw Ren's almost-smile and received it like a gift.

"Go win," Kenji said.

Ren laced the spikes. He stood. He walked to the door of the equipment room, the
carbon-fiber plates clicking softly on the concrete floor with each step.

At the door, he turned.

"Kenji."

"Yeah?"

"Tell them thank you. Everyone. The whole table."

"I will."

Ren left. The door closed. The sound of his new spikes faded down the hallway—a
light, precise, confident sound, the sound of someone moving forward.

## V.

Monday evening. Isabelle's apartment.

She stood in the kitchen, flour on her hands, and taught Caspar to make pâte
sucrée.

This had been his request—made on Saturday, via LINE, with the specific
directness that she had come to understand was his version of vulnerability.
_"Teach me something you love."_ Not photography—he had his own version of that.
Not dance—that was hers, private and complex. Baking. The thing that was
entirely hers, unconnected to family or fortune, the skill she had earned
through practice and precision and the particular joy of transforming simple
ingredients into something extraordinary.

He stood at the kitchen counter in her apartment, his sleeves rolled to his
elbows, his hands dusted with flour, and he listened.

"The butter has to be cold," Isabelle said. She placed a block of French
butter—Auntie Choi's refrigerator, the dedicated compartment—on the marble
counter. "Not frozen. Cold. You want it to be firm enough to hold its shape but
soft enough to incorporate. Feel it."

He pressed his thumb into the butter. "Like clay."

"Exactly like clay. Now cut it into cubes. Small. One centimeter."

He cut the butter. His hands were steady—the photographer's hands, the porter's
hands—and the cubes were uniform, each one a small, precise block of pale gold.
He worked quickly, without wasted motion, the knife moving through the butter
with the efficiency of someone who understood that precision was not about
slowness but about control.

"Now the flour." She measured it—200 grams, weighed on a digital scale that
Auntie Choi maintained at a calibration that would have satisfied a
pharmaceutical lab. "Sift it. Twice. The first sift is for texture. The second
is for air."

He sifted. The flour fell through the sieve in a fine, white cascade, building a
soft mound on the marble. He sifted again. The mound grew lighter, more aerated,
the particles suspended in microscopic pockets of air that would, during baking,
expand and create the tender, crumbly texture that distinguished pâte sucrée
from ordinary shortcrust.

"Sugar. Icing sugar, not granulated. Sixty grams." She handed him the jar. "It
dissolves faster. Granulated sugar leaves grains in the dough—you can feel them
when you bite. Icing sugar disappears."

He measured the sugar. He added it to the flour.

"Now make a well. In the center. Push the flour and sugar to the edges."

He made the well—a crater in the white mound, the marble visible at the bottom,
the walls of flour and sugar sloping gently inward. Isabelle added an egg yolk
and a splash of vanilla extract to the center.

"Start mixing. From the center. Use your fingertips—not your palms. Palms are
too warm. The warmth melts the butter too early and the dough becomes tough."

He mixed. His fingertips worked the egg yolk into the flour, drawing the dry
ingredients into the wet in concentric circles, the mixture transitioning from
loose and crumbly to cohesive, the butter cubes breaking down and distributing
through the dough in flat, irregular layers that would create the flaky, layered
texture of the final crust.

"Stop," she said.

He stopped.

"Feel it."

He pressed the dough. It was cool—the marble had kept the temperature down—and
slightly rough, the butter layers still visible as pale streaks in the pale
dough.

"It should feel like—" She searched for the comparison. "Like the inside of a
well-made jacket. Structured but not stiff. You should feel the layers."

"I feel them."

"Good. Now wrap it. Plastic wrap. Flat disc. Refrigerator. Thirty minutes."

He wrapped the dough. He placed it in the refrigerator. He stood at the counter
and looked at his flour-dusted hands with the expression of someone who had just
performed a task he had never attempted before and who was assessing his own
performance with the critical eye of a person accustomed to precision.

"How did I do?" he said.

"The butter cubes were excellent. The mixing was controlled. The dough
temperature is good." She paused. "You're a natural."

"I'm not."

"You are. Your hands—" She took his hands. She held them, flour and all, in the
warm light of the kitchen. She turned them over, examining the palms, the
fingers, the specific topography of calluses and lines that mapped his history:
the photographer's grip, the porter's strength, the runner's lean tendons. "Your
hands learn fast. They learned the camera. They learned the luggage. They
learned the dance choreography. And now they're learning dough."

He looked at her. She was standing close—close enough that he could see the
individual grains of flour that had landed on her cheekbone, small and white
against the luminous skin. Close enough that he could feel the warmth of her
hands around his. Close enough that the kitchen, with its marble counters and
its imported appliances and its German toaster and its nine-story view of the
Tokyo skyline, contracted to a space that contained only the two of them and the
flour on their hands and the dough chilling in the refrigerator and the
specific, irreducible fact of being together.

"이자벨." _Isabelle._

"응." _Yeah._

"고마워." _Thank you._

"뭐가?" _For what?_

"다." _Everything._

She looked at him. She looked at his flour-dusted hands in hers, and his pale
blue eyes steady on her face, and the expression he was wearing—the one that had
taken weeks to develop, the one that had emerged gradually from behind the stoic
architecture like a photograph resolving in a developer tray: tenderness,
unguarded and complete, the face of a boy who had been carrying the weight of
his own story for a year and who had found, in a kitchen in Minami-Aoyama with
flour on his hands and dough in the refrigerator, a person who helped him set it
down.

She kissed him. Gently, briefly—a press of her lips to his, tasting of vanilla
extract and the faint salt of flour, lasting two seconds, delivered in the warm
light of the kitchen with Auntie Choi's knife block to their left and the
Arita-yaki plates to their right and the German toaster that Helena Waldstein
had commissioned from a workshop in Darmstadt humming softly on the counter
behind them.

"다," she said. _Everything._

She stepped back. She wiped her hands on her apron.

"The dough needs thirty minutes," she said. "Do you want to see the photographs
while we wait?"

"Which photographs?"

"Yours. The ones from Daikanyama. I put them up."

She led him to her bedroom. On the wall opposite the bed—the wall that had been
blank, the wall that the interior designer had left neutral—three photographs
hung in a row, each one in a simple white frame, each one precisely spaced.

Frame 6. Frame 7. Frame 8.

Isabelle facing the camera. Isabelle in profile. The empty street.

He stood in the doorway and looked at his photographs on her wall. His
photographs, printed on Portra 400, the color film he had loaded because it was
a color day, the images of the girl who had asked _I want to know what you see
when you look at me_ and who had, apparently, liked the answer well enough to
frame it and hang it in the place where she slept.

"You framed them," he said.

"I framed them."

"On the wall."

"Where else would they go?"

He looked at the photographs. He looked at the girl in the photographs—the cream
sweater, the cherry blossoms, the open expression, the light. He looked at the
girl standing beside him—flour on her cheekbone, apron over her clothes, her
hair tied back, her eyes watching him watch the photographs with the calm,
patient attention of someone who understood that this moment required silence
and stillness and the full, undivided presence of two people standing in a room
where the walls showed them who they were to each other.

"이자벨," he said.

"응."

"벽에 걸어줘서 고마워."

_Thank you for putting them on the wall._

She leaned into him. Her shoulder against his arm. Her weight—slight,
deliberate, the dancer's controlled lean—resting against him the way Jiwoo
leaned against her in elevators, the way people leaned against the things that
held them up.

"당연하지," she said. _Obviously._

They stood in the doorway, looking at the photographs, and the dough chilled in
the refrigerator, and the evening light moved across the room, and the city
hummed fourteen stories below, and neither of them moved because neither of them
needed to, because they were exactly where they were supposed to be, and the
evidence was on the wall.
