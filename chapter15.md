# Chapter 15: The Last Frame

---

## I.

The prefectural meet was held at Komazawa Olympic Park, on a Saturday in early
June that arrived carrying the first real humidity of the year—a thickness in
the air that blurred the horizon and softened the edges of every building and
turned the sky from blue to the pale, luminous white of heated porcelain.

They went. All of them.

This was not discussed. It was not debated, proposed, or voted on. It happened
the way the combined lunch had happened, the way the exhibition had happened,
the way every collective act of the group had happened since week one: Kenji
announced it, and the announcement functioned as gravity, pulling everyone into
its orbit through the sheer mass of his conviction that this was what they were
going to do.

"Ren runs at 2:15," Kenji said, at Thursday's combined lunch. "The 800 meters.
Then the 1500 at 3:40. Komazawa Park. We're going."

"All of us?" Sora said.

"All of us. This is not optional. This is HCCL plus delegates operating at full
capacity in support of a founding member."

"I have homework," Daiki said.

"Bring it."

"I don't have a chair."

"Sit on the grass. People have been sitting on grass for thousands of years,
Daiki. It's a solved problem."

Daiki adjusted his glasses. "Fine."

Yuki closed her book. "I'll come. I want to understand the 800-meter race
structure."

"There's not much to understand," Hina said. "They run in a circle. Twice."

"All competitive systems have structure," Yuki said. "Even circles."

Isabelle looked at Caspar. The look was brief—the daily check-in, the
micro-exchange that confirmed alignment. _We're going. Obviously._

He nodded.

On Saturday, they assembled at Komazawa Station at 1:30 PM. Seven people in
civilian clothes—no uniforms, no school identifiers, just a cluster of
fifteen-year-olds navigating the exit turnstiles with the particular energy of a
group that had somewhere to be and someone to support.

Kenji had made a banner.

This fact became apparent when he emerged from the station holding a rolled-up
piece of white fabric that he unfurled with the ceremony of a flag-bearer at an
international ceremony. The banner was hand-painted—Kenji's own work, done the
previous evening with poster paint on a bedsheet that his mother had, according
to Kenji, "donated to the cause after only moderate resistance." It read, in
large, uneven characters:

**いけ！蓮！走れ！**

**GO! REN! RUN!**

Beneath the text, a crude but recognizable drawing of a running figure with
Ren's distinctive close-cropped hair, its legs blurred to suggest speed, its
expression—if a stick figure could have an expression—conveying intense
determination.

"Kenji," Sora said. "Did you paint this yourself?"

"I did. Last night. Between 11 PM and 1 AM. The proportions are approximate."

"The proportions are heroic."

"Thank you. I was going for heroic."

They walked to the stadium. Komazawa Olympic Park's athletic complex was a relic
of the 1964 Tokyo Olympics—a concrete stadium with tiered seating, the track
surface a dark red polyurethane, the infield green and manicured. The stands
were partially filled: families of athletes, coaches, a scattering of students
from various schools. The air smelled like sunscreen and rubber and the
particular ozone tang of competition—adrenaline made atmospheric.

They found seats in the mid-section of the home straight, directly opposite the
finish line. Kenji unrolled the banner and held it across three laps, recruiting
Sora and Daiki to hold the ends. The banner flapped in the humid breeze, the
painted text and stick figure visible from the track.

Isabelle sat beside Caspar, their thighs touching on the concrete bench. She was
wearing a white cotton dress and canvas sneakers and she had brought a bag
containing eight éclairs (one per person, Ren's saved for after the race, kept cold
in an insulated bag with a gel pack because Auntie Choi did not permit pastry to
travel without temperature control). Caspar was wearing a linen shirt and dark
shorts and the Leica was around his neck—this was, after all, a visual event,
and Caspar attended all visual events armed.

Yuki sat on Isabelle's other side, a notebook open on her knee, already
recording observations about the track layout, the lane assignments, the warm-up
protocols of the competitors. Hina sat beside Yuki, her legs stretched out, her
face tilted toward the white sky, her posture suggesting that she was
simultaneously present and elsewhere, attending to the event through some
internal channel that did not require her eyes to be pointed at the track.

The 800 meters was at 2:15.

---

## II.

The competitors assembled on the track. Eight runners, each one lean and coiled
and wearing the focused expression of someone who had been training for this
specific moment for months. They wore their school colors—singlets and shorts in
various combinations of blue, red, white, green. Ren was in lane four.
Sakuragaoka's colors were navy and white: a navy singlet, white shorts, the
school name printed small on the chest.

On his feet: the new spikes. Black with a white stripe. Carbon-fiber plate. The
spikes that had arrived through a system built from photographs and goodwill and
the specific, stubborn conviction that a runner should not have to race on
damaged equipment.

Caspar raised the Leica.

Through the viewfinder, he found Ren—crouched in the starting position, his
hands on the track surface, his weight forward, his eyes fixed on the curve
ahead. The stadium light was diffused by the overcast sky, flat and even, the
kind of light that eliminated shadows and rendered everything in clean, even
tones. The red track surface was vivid against the green infield. Ren's navy
singlet was a dark shape against the lighter background.

He focused. The split image aligned.

The starting gun fired.

The sound was flat and sharp—a single percussive crack that broke the stadium's
ambient noise like a stone through glass. The eight runners launched—a
synchronized explosion of muscle and stride and the particular, violent beauty
of human bodies accelerating from zero to competitive pace in the space of three
seconds.

Ren was in the middle of the pack off the start. His stride was long,
controlled, the rhythm smooth and unhurried—the stride of a runner who
understood that the 800 was not a sprint but a sustained effort, a two-lap
negotiation between speed and endurance that was decided not by who was fastest
but by who managed their energy most precisely.

_Click._

Caspar photographed the start—the eight bodies in motion, the staggered lanes,
the red track stretching ahead. Then he lowered the camera and watched.

The first lap unfolded with the tactical complexity of a chess match compressed
into sixty seconds. The runners sorted themselves—the front-runners pushing the
pace, the mid-pack holding position, the closers sitting back and waiting. Ren
was fifth. His form was clean—shoulders relaxed, arms pumping in a compact,
efficient arc, his footstrike landing under his center of gravity with the
precise, cushioned contact of new shoes working exactly as designed.

"His stride is different," Isabelle said quietly.

Caspar looked at her. She was watching Ren with the specific, analytical
attention of someone who understood movement—who could see, in the mechanics of
a running stride, the same structural details she saw in choreography: the
alignment of joints, the transfer of force, the relationship between effort and
efficiency.

"The lateral wobble is gone," she said. "Look at his left ankle. It's tracking
straight."

She was right. Caspar couldn't see it with his naked eye—the resolution was too
low at this distance—but he trusted Isabelle's eye the way he trusted his own.
If she said the ankle was tracking straight, the ankle was tracking straight.

The bell rang. Last lap. The stadium's energy shifted—the ambient noise
tightening, the spectators leaning forward, the collective attention narrowing
to the eight bodies on the red track.

Kenji was on his feet. The banner was abandoned—he was standing on the concrete
bench, his hands cupped around his mouth, his voice cutting through the stadium
noise with the penetrating force of a human foghorn.

"REN! GO! _MOVE!_"

The runners entered the back straight. The pace accelerated—the front-runners
kicking, the mid-pack responding, the closers beginning their drive. Ren was
fourth. His stride lengthened. His arms pumped faster. His face—visible now as
they rounded the final curve—was set in the particular expression of controlled
suffering that distance runners wore like a mask: the mouth open, the eyes
narrowed, the entire face organized around the single objective of maintaining
pace while the body screamed for permission to stop.

The final straight. Two hundred meters. The eight runners strung out in a line,
the gaps between them measured in meters and fractions of seconds. Ren was
third. He was gaining on second. His spikes hit the polyurethane with a rapid,
precise cadence—the carbon-fiber plate returning energy with each footstrike,
the biomechanics clean, the kinetic chain from ankle to knee to hip functioning
without the lateral stress that had threatened to compromise everything.

"GO!" Kenji screamed. He was not alone—Sora was screaming too, her voice pitched
high and sharp, the fan-event voice repurposed for athletics. Daiki was
standing, his glasses slightly askew, his phone forgotten in his hand. Yuki had
closed her notebook. Even Hina was leaning forward, her characteristic
detachment suspended, her eyes tracking Ren with an intensity that belied her
calm.

Isabelle gripped Caspar's arm. Her fingers dug into his forearm, the dancer's
grip, strong and specific. She was leaning forward, her body angled toward the
track, her entire frame oriented toward the finish line with the full-body
engagement of someone who understood, intimately, what it meant to push a body
past its limits in front of people who were watching.

Ren crossed the finish line.

Third.

Third out of eight. Third in the prefectural 800 meters. Third, with a personal
best time that the track-side announcer read over the PA system in a flat, nasal
voice—1:58.32—and that landed in the stands like a small explosion.

"ONE FIFTY-EIGHT!" Kenji's voice was cracking. He was jumping—actually jumping,
on the concrete bench, his feet leaving the surface, the structural integrity of
the seating arrangement a secondary concern. "HE BROKE TWO MINUTES! REN! ONE
FIFTY-EIGHT THIRTY-TWO!"

Caspar raised the Leica.

Through the viewfinder, he found Ren. The runner was standing on the track, bent
at the waist, his hands on his knees, his chest heaving. The navy singlet was
dark with sweat. The white shorts were streaked with the red dust of the track
surface. His face was—

Caspar pressed the shutter.

_Click._

Ren's face. Post-race. The suffering mask dissolved, replaced by something rawer
and more essential: relief, pride, exhaustion, and a smile. A real smile. Not
the almost-smile, not the controlled expression, not the baseline Ren face that
gave away nothing. A smile that used his entire face—the mouth open, the eyes
creased, the expression of someone who had pushed his body to its absolute limit
and found, at the other side, that the limit had moved.

Frame 27 of 36.

Caspar wound the film advance. He lowered the camera.

Beside him, Isabelle was crying. Not the silent, controlled tears of the fan
event—these were different, more visible, the tears of someone who was happy and
tired and proud and who had stopped trying to manage her emotional architecture
and was simply letting it respond.

He put his arm around her. She leaned into him—the lean, the familiar lean, her
head against his shoulder, the weight of her resting against the weight of him.
They sat on the concrete bench in the humid June light and watched Ren jog
slowly along the track's infield, recovering, his new spikes carrying him
through the cool-down with the same clean, stable stride that had carried him
through the race.

"He did it," Isabelle said.

"He did it."

"The spikes worked."

"The system worked."

She looked up at him. Her face was tear-streaked and flushed and radiant with
the specific beauty of a person who was experiencing joy without reservation—no
filter, no calibration, no careful management of what she showed and to whom.
Just happiness, uncut, expressed through tears and a smile and the pressure of
her body against his.

"우리가 했어," she said. _We did it._

"우리가 했어." _We did it._

---

The 1500 was at 3:40.

Ren ran it on tired legs—the 800 had taken something, and the gap between races
was not enough for full recovery. He finished fifth. The time was good—not a
personal best, but respectable, the performance of an athlete who had given
everything in the first race and found enough in reserve to compete, if not to
podium, in the second.

He walked off the track after the 1500 with the particular gait of someone whose
legs were operating on credit—each step a negotiation with muscles that had been
overdrawn and were now charging interest. He found his coach. He received a nod.
He received a water bottle.

Then he looked up into the stands.

The banner was there. Kenji was there, still holding one end, his voice reduced
to a hoarse whisper after two hours of continuous projection. Sora was there,
her phone out, having documented every moment for a social media story that she
would later describe as "the most important athletic content I've ever created."
Daiki was there, his phone showing a spreadsheet he had built during the races
that tracked split times, pace zones, and a metric he had invented called
"effort efficiency ratio." Yuki was there, her notebook full, her observations
complete, her understanding of the 800-meter race structure now thorough enough
to discuss at length. Hina was there, her face calm, her eyes steady.

Caspar was there, the Leica around his neck. Isabelle was there, her head on
Caspar's shoulder.

Seven people. In a stadium. For him.

Ren raised his hand—a small wave, the fingers curling once and then relaxing.
The same gesture Hina had used on the first day of school, when Isabelle had
walked arm-in-arm through the gate. The gesture that said: _Good for you._

But directed at them, the meaning shifted: _Good for us._

## III.

After the meet. Komazawa Park.

They sat on the grass near the stadium, in the shade of a row of zelkova trees
whose canopy was dense and green, the late-afternoon light filtering through the
leaves in shifting patterns. The humidity had eased slightly—the air still
thick, but softened by the hour, the shadows lengthening, the first breath of
evening moving through the park.

Isabelle distributed the éclairs. Eight pastries—chocolate fondant gleaming, the
crème pâtissière visible in the cross-section where she had cut one to check the
filling's consistency. She handed them out on squares of parchment paper, the
same way she had served the tarte au citron, the same choreography of care.

Ren ate his in three bites. Then he lay back on the grass and stared at the sky
and said: "One fifty-eight."

"One fifty-eight thirty-two," Kenji corrected, from where he was lying two
meters away, his banner draped over his chest like a blanket.

"Thirty-two," Ren agreed. He was holding his spikes in one hand—he'd changed
into regular sneakers for the walk, but he'd kept the spikes, carrying them by
the heels, unwilling to put them in his bag. They dangled from his fingers now,
the carbon-fiber plates catching the filtered light.

The group was arranged in a loose constellation on the grass—no formation, no
structure, just bodies finding comfortable positions relative to each other the
way they had learned to do over the preceding weeks, the spatial relationships
encoded in muscle memory: Kenji near the center, Sora near Kenji, Daiki slightly
apart, Yuki with her book (closed, for once), Hina at the periphery, Caspar and
Isabelle together, always together, their proximity no longer remarked upon
because it had become as fundamental as the grass or the sky.

"Ji-won," Hina said.

Isabelle looked at her. Hina was sitting cross-legged, her hands in her lap, her
face turned toward the group rather than the sky. This was, Isabelle recognized,
a deliberate orientation—Hina choosing to face inward, to be part of the circle
rather than its observer.

"I want to say something," Hina said. "To the group."

The grass went quiet. Hina speaking to the group—not to an individual, not in an
aside, but to all of them, collectively—was an event without precedent. The
group recognized this instinctively, the way they recognized all shifts in their
social weather: through the quality of the silence that followed.

Hina looked at them. She looked at each of them, one by one, with the steady,
sweeping attention of someone who was seeing them all clearly and who was
choosing, for the first time, to let them see her.

"I've been watching," she said.

No one spoke.

"Since the beginning. Since the first day. I watched Ji-won walk into the
classroom and I saw someone who was hiding. I watched Ryu-kun look at her across
the gym and I saw someone who was hoping. I watched Yuki start a file on both of
them and I watched Sora fall in love with their cooking and I watched Kenji
adopt Ryu-kun through sheer force of personality and I watched Ren let himself
be fed by strangers and I watched Daiki put his phone down for the first time in
his life."

She paused. The park continued its ambient life around them—birdsong, the
distant sound of children in a playground, the hum of a vending machine.

"And I watched myself," she said. "I watched myself decide to stay at the edge.
To observe. To keep the shapes I saw to myself. Because that's what I do. I see
shapes and I keep them. It's—" She looked down at her hands. "—it's safe. Seeing
without being seen. Knowing without being known. I'm good at it. I've been good
at it my whole life."

The silence was absolute. Seven people, on the grass, in the filtered light,
listening.

"But this group—" She stopped. She started again. "This group is the first place
where staying at the edge feels like missing something. Where watching isn't
enough. Where I want to be—" She searched for the word. Her usual precision
wavered for a moment—the analytical framework briefly insufficient for the
emotional content. "—inside."

She looked up. Her dark eyes were bright—not with tears, but with something
else, something that Isabelle recognized because she had seen it in her own
reflection on the first morning in the Minami-Aoyama apartment: the look of
someone who was about to stop hiding and didn't know yet what would be on the
other side.

"I don't have secrets like yours," Hina said, looking at Isabelle and Caspar. "I
don't have a Swiss boarding school or a gap year or a—" The faintest trace of a
smile. "—an expensive shape. I have a mom who works at a convenience store and a
dad who drives a delivery truck and a little brother who's eight and who thinks
I'm boring because I read too much. I live in a 2LDK in Nakano and my school
shoes are from the sale bin at Shimamura."

She said this without self-pity. Without apology. Without any inflection that
invited sympathy. She said it the way Caspar stated exposure settings—as factual
information, neutral in value, relevant to the frame.

"And that's fine," Hina said. "I'm not telling you this because I want anything.
I'm telling you because you told me things. Not with words—with actions. Ji-won,
you brought me madeleines on the first day before I'd said ten words to you. You
brought them to the back row. You didn't wait for me to come to you. You came to
me." She looked at Isabelle. "Nobody does that. People notice the front row. The
back row notices itself."

Isabelle felt something move behind her ribs. A specific thing—not warmth, not
sadness, but the precise, calibrated emotion of someone who was being told that
a small act of kindness had been received by the exact person it was meant for,
and that the receiving had mattered.

"And Ryu-kun," Hina continued. "You nod at me. In the hallway. Every day. A
small nod—most people wouldn't notice it. But you do it consistently, and you do
it because you see me, and being seen by someone who sees the way you do is—"
She paused. "It's a lot."

Caspar's expression didn't change. The stoic architecture held. But his right
hand, resting on the grass beside Isabelle's, moved—a fractional shift, his
little finger extending until it touched hers, the smallest possible contact,
the body's response to an emotion that the face couldn't show.

"Kenji," Hina said. "You are the loudest person I have ever met. And also the
kindest. Those two things don't usually go together. In you, they're the same
thing."

Kenji opened his mouth. For once—for the first time in the group's history—no
sound came out.

"Sora. Your passion is—" Hina searched. "—contagious. In the real sense. Not
superficial. You love things without embarrassment. That's rare and it's brave
and it makes the people around you braver."

Sora's eyes were filling. She didn't wipe them.

"Yuki. You see everything I see, but you organize it. You turn observation into
architecture. You're the reason this group functions—not because you lead it,
but because you hold the structure that lets everyone else be messy."

Yuki's book was in her lap. Her hands were on the book. Her knuckles were white.

"Daiki. You build worlds. In code, in spreadsheets, in the systems you design.
And you let us into them. That's—the dungeon crawler you're building. When you
offered to let people playtest it on the first day. That was you opening a
door."

Daiki adjusted his glasses. He adjusted them again. He removed them, wiped them
on his shirt, replaced them. His eyes, behind the lenses, were suspiciously
bright.

"Ren." Hina looked at the boy on the grass, the spikes in his hand, the navy
singlet still dark with sweat. "You ran 1:58.32 today. In spikes that exist
because these people saw you needed them and built a system to provide them
without making you feel small. And you let them. That's not weakness. That's the
hardest kind of strength."

Ren was still looking at the sky. He didn't move. He didn't speak. But the hand
holding the spikes tightened—the fingers closing around the heels, gripping the
shoes the way a person gripped something they intended to carry for a long time.

"I'm telling you all of this," Hina said, "because I don't want to be at the
edge anymore. I want to be here. In this." She gestured—a small, encompassing
motion, her hand drawing a circle that included the grass and the trees and the
seven people and the éclairs and the banner and the spikes and the humid June
air. "Whatever this is. I want to be in it."

The circle was quiet. The park moved around them—the trees rustling, the vending
machine humming, the distant children laughing. The late-afternoon light shifted
through the canopy, painting the grass in patterns that moved like water.

Kenji sat up. He looked at Hina. His face—the face that was never still, the
face that performed and projected and broadcast on every available
frequency—was, for once, quiet. Not blank. Not suppressed. Just quiet, the way a
room was quiet when something true had been said and the room was deciding how
to hold it.

"Hina," he said.

"Yes."

"You're in."

He said it simply. Two words. No performance, no volume, no embellishment. Just
the fact, stated plainly, the way Ren stated race times or Caspar stated
exposure settings or Isabelle stated ingredient measurements: as information
that was true and that did not require decoration.

"You were always in," he said.

Hina looked at him. The rare smile appeared—slower this time, wider, the smile
that she had given Isabelle at the shoe lockers and Caspar after the exhibition
and the group after the fan event. The smile that was, Isabelle now understood,
not rare because Hina was reluctant to smile but because Hina reserved her
smiles for moments that deserved them, and this moment—this specific,
unrepeatable constellation of people and light and grass and truth—was one.

"Okay," Hina said.

"Okay," Kenji said.

The group breathed. The circle held.

## IV.

They stayed in the park until the light began to fail.

The conversation that followed Hina's speech was not dramatic—it was ordinary,
which was the point. They talked about the meet, about Ren's split times, about
the banner (which Kenji wanted to preserve and Sora wanted to frame and Yuki
wanted to recycle because "it's a bedsheet, Kenji, your mother will want it
back"). They talked about the exhibition's success and the athletics fund and
the possibility of a second exhibition in the fall. They talked about summer
plans—the break was six weeks away, and the approaching end of their first term
hung in the air like the humidity, present and undeniable.

"What are you doing for summer?" Sora asked the group.

The answers came in layers. Kenji was staying in Tokyo—his parents worked, his
summer would be a landscape of convenience stores and basketball courts and the
comedy club's summer workshop. Sora was going to her grandmother's house in
Nagano for two weeks, then back to Tokyo. Daiki was attending a programming camp
in Yokohama—five days, intensive, the application for which he had submitted
seven months ago. Yuki was staying in Tokyo, working on a "personal project"
that she declined to elaborate on, deflecting questions with the serene opacity
of someone whose personal projects were not for public consumption. Hina was
staying home. Ren was training—summer track season, daily practices, a regional
invitational in July.

"Ji-won?" Sora said.

Isabelle looked at Caspar. The look was the look—the daily look, the check-in,
the micro-negotiation of two people who shared information that was too large
for casual conversation and who were constantly calibrating how much of it to
release and when.

"I might go to Seoul for a bit," she said. "To visit family."

"Family" meant VESPER. "A bit" meant two weeks in the practice room, watching
the tour rehearsals, sleeping on the floor of the trainee dorm, eating Yuna's
stolen snacks and Jiwoo's instant ramyeon and the madeleines she would bring,
packed in a suitcase between layers of clothing and Auntie Choi's disapproval.

"Caspar?" Sora said.

"Munich. To see my mother. And maybe—" He paused. He looked at Isabelle. "—maybe
Seoul."

Isabelle's heart did something it wasn't authorized to do.

"Maybe Seoul," she repeated.

"My mother has business there. I could visit."

"You could visit."

"If that's okay."

"If that's okay," she said, and the restraint in her voice—the careful,
deliberate neutrality, the filter operating at full capacity to prevent the
sentence from arriving with the full weight of what it meant, which was _yes,
come to Seoul, come to the practice room, meet my sisters, let Yuna interrogate
you in person, let Jiwoo watch you dance, let Minji assess your posture, let
Haeun feed you, let me show you the world I lived in for a year, the world that
made me who I am, the world where the girl in your photographs learned to
move_—was, to anyone paying attention, not restraint at all.

Hina was paying attention. Hina smiled.

## V.

They left the park at 6:30.

The walk to the station was slow—the group moving at the pace of eight tired
bodies processing sugar and emotion and the specific, pleasant fatigue of a day
spent in the sun watching someone they loved run fast. They walked in a loose
formation—no structure, no hierarchy, just bodies finding their natural
positions, the spatial grammar of the group expressed in the distances between
shoulders and the directions of gazes and the way certain people gravitated
toward certain other people with the unconscious, inevitable drift of celestial
objects finding their orbits.

Kenji and Sora at the front, their conversation continuous, their topics
revolving around VESPER and the banner and Kenji's increasingly transparent
attachment, which Sora navigated with the cheerful obliviousness of someone who
was either genuinely unaware or genuinely skilled at pretending.

Daiki and Yuki behind them, walking in the particular silence of two people who
were comfortable with each other's quietness—Daiki on his phone, Yuki with her
book closed under her arm, neither of them speaking, neither of them needing to.

Hina and Ren in the middle—Hina walking, Ren walking beside her, the spikes
still in his hand, neither of them speaking either, but the quality of their
silence was different from Daiki and Yuki's: warmer, newer, the silence of two
people who had always been at the periphery and who were now, tentatively,
walking closer to the center.

And at the back: Caspar and Isabelle.

They held hands. The gesture was automatic now—their fingers finding each other
without conscious direction, the interlacing of hands as natural as breathing,
as unremarkable as gravity. They walked, and they held hands, and the Tokyo
evening settled around them in gradients of amber and violet, and the city's
noise—trains, traffic, the distant hum of fourteen million lives—was a sound so
constant and so vast that it functioned as silence.

"캐스퍼." _Caspar._

"응." _Yeah._

"오늘 좋았어." _Today was good._

"응." _Yeah._

"다 좋았어. 이 사람들. 이 그룹. 렌. 히나 말한 거. 에클레어. 배너. 다." _All of
it was good. These people. This group. Ren. What Hina said. The éclairs. The
banner. Everything._

"응." _Yeah._

"너 오늘 '응'밖에 안 해." _You've only been saying "yeah" all day._

"다른 말이 필요 없어서." _Because no other words are needed._

She looked at him. The evening light was doing things to his face—catching the
plane of his cheekbone, shadowing the line of his jaw, turning his pale blue
eyes into something that looked like the sky just after sunset, when the blue
deepened and the first stars appeared, the color of a day that was ending and a
night that was beginning.

She stopped walking. He stopped. They were on a quiet side street, a block from
the station, the others ahead of them, their voices fading around a corner.

"캐스퍼." _Caspar._

"응." _Yeah._

"사진 한 장 찍어줘." _Take one photo for me._

He looked at her. She was standing in the evening light—white dress, canvas
sneakers, honey hair, hazel-green eyes, her face open and unfiltered, the filter
permanently down now, at least for him, at least in the moments when they were
alone and the world contracted to a space that held only two.

"뭘?" _Of what?_

"우리." _Us._

He looked at the Leica. He looked at her. The Leica was a rangefinder—a manual
camera, no self-timer, no remote, no mechanism for the photographer to be in the
photograph. To take a picture of _us,_ someone had to hold the camera at arm's
length and compose blind, trusting the framing to instinct, accepting that the
image would be imperfect because the photographer was also the subject and no
one could be both at the same time.

He raised the camera. He held it at arm's length, the lens pointing back at
them. He pulled her close with his free arm—her body against his, her head
against his shoulder, the specific configuration they had developed over weeks
of proximity, the shape of them together that was becoming, through repetition,
a single form.

He couldn't see through the viewfinder. He was composing by feel—the angle of
the camera, the distance of the lens, the position of the sun behind them. He
was shooting blind.

"웃어," Isabelle said. _Smile._

He smiled. The real one. The full one. The smile that Auntie Park had seen after
the first kiss, the smile that Kenji had tracked at three-hundred-percent
increase, the smile that had been, for a year, locked behind the stoic
architecture and that now, in this moment, on this street, with this girl
against his shoulder and the evening light warm on their faces, was the most
natural thing in the world.

Isabelle smiled too. Her smile. The dimple. The whole face.

_Click._

Frame 34 of 36.

He lowered the camera. He didn't check the frame—he couldn't, not on film, not
until the roll was developed and the image emerged from the chemistry. He would
have to trust it. He would have to carry the uncertainty of not knowing what the
photograph looked like until he stood in his darkroom and watched it resolve
from the silver and the light.

But he didn't need to see it. He knew what it looked like. It looked like
them—two tall, complicated, fifteen-year-old people with heavy last names and
hidden lives and a love that had survived a Swiss terrace and a year of silence
and an entire ocean of unsaid things, standing on a Tokyo side street in the
last light of a June evening, smiling, together, whole.

"두 장 남았어," he said. _Two frames left._

"뭐에 쓸 거야?" _What will you use them for?_

He wound the film advance. Frame 35. He looked at her. He looked at the street.
He looked at the evening sky, the first stars, the amber glow of the city
against the deepening blue.

"모르겠어," he said. _I don't know yet._

"좋아." _Good._

"좋아?" _Good?_

"아직 안 끝났으니까." _Because it's not over yet._

She took his hand. They walked toward the station, toward the train, toward the
city, toward the summer that was coming, toward the Seoul visit and the Tokyo
Dome concert and the second exhibition and the morning runs and the evening
baking and the nightly LINE thread that ran beneath everything like an
underground river, carrying the weight of who they were in a language only they
shared.

Two frames left. Thirty-four spent. A whole roll of film—thirty-six exposures,
each one a moment, each one a decision to press the shutter and commit the light
to silver—and two remaining, held in reserve, waiting for whatever came next.

The station swallowed them. The train carried them home. The city turned beneath
them, vast and luminous and indifferent and full of people whose stories were as
complicated and as beautiful as theirs, and who would never know, and who didn't
need to.

The doors closed. The train moved.

Isabelle leaned her head on Caspar's shoulder. Caspar rested his cheek against
her hair.

The Leica hung from his neck, warm from the day, the film inside it carrying
thirty-four images and two empty frames, the silver waiting in the dark for the
light that would come.
