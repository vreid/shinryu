# The Lore Bible — Isabelle & Caspar

## The Families

### van Rijn–Shin Clan

**European root:** van Rijn Private Banking & Global Real Estate — Dutch banking
dynasty, seventeenth-century old money based in Wassenaar. The kind of wealth
that does not appear on lists because the lists are compiled by people who work
for them. The operating structure is layered trusts, nominee holdings, a
family-office apparatus in Zurich, and a philanthropic foundation — Oranje
Meridian Stichting — that launders reputation through cultural patronage.

**Korean root:** Shin Entertainment — Asia's premier K-pop agency, founded in
the late-1990s financial-shock afterglow by a former dancer who understood that
cultural influence appreciates faster than real estate, then scaled through
culture exports as the government began treating popular content as both
perception engine and trade logic. The business model runs on trainees,
catalogs, sub-labels, touring rights, and exportable cultural appetite.

**Father:** Willem van Rijn — CEO of van Rijn Private Banking. Perpetually in
Zurich, London, or Singapore. Communicates through family-office memos, driver
schedules, and foundation minutes. His affection for his daughter is genuine; it
is simply routed through beneficial-owner structures.

**Mother:** Shin Hee-yeon — CEO of Shin Entertainment. Compact, sharp bob,
minimal makeup. Enters rooms like a conductor. Loves her daughter fiercely,
strategically, and from behind a desk via structural decisions — trainee
evaluations, line distribution sheets, release-form approvals.

**Power base:** Invisible custody of capital married to emotional velocity of
culture.

**Marriage logic:** Old European capital wanted narrative heat. Korean cultural
power wanted cold infrastructure. They did not imagine love. They imagined
ballast.

**Strongest vulnerability:** Artist-contract scandal, tax-transparency pressure,
leaked internal trainee ranking systems.

### Waldstein–Ryu Clan

**European root:** Waldstein Botanicals & Aesthetics — ultra-luxury skincare
brand rooted in Austrian/Bohemian minor nobility that pivoted to luxury
branding. The public face is clean science and premium sustainability language.
The operating logic is formulations, clinical positioning, and vertically
integrated manufacturing from raw botanical sourcing to prestige packaging.

**Korean root:** Ryu BioPharma & Chemical — industrial chaebol,
chemicals-to-pharma pipeline. The Bavarian lab presence anchors the European R&D
leg in Munich's biotech cluster.

**Mother:** Helena Waldstein — CEO of Waldstein Botanicals. Silver-blonde hair,
navy blazer, gold chain with the Waldstein crest. She understands extraction:
from soil, from labs, from people.

**Father:** Ryu Ji-seok — Chairman of Ryu BioPharma & Chemical. Communicates in
sentence fragments and strategic silences. His authority runs through HR chains,
board seats, and legal counsel.

**Power base:** Beauty and biotech vertical integration — from volcanic soil to
clinical claims to department-store counters.

**Marriage logic:** European luxury prestige married to Korean industrial
infrastructure.

**Strongest vulnerability:** Labor injury, contamination event, sourcing
exploitation, patent dispute over indigenous formulations.

### The Inter-Family Web

The alliance between the two houses was never a romance project. It was an asset
hedge. The clans orbit each other through corporate boards, charity galas, and a
joint public-facing umbrella — the Meridian-Haneul Arts and Science Trust —
respectable enough to justify any overlap. Close enough to plan a generational
merger-marriage between their children. Distant enough that the children never
grow up as siblings. The corporate architecture and personal architecture
converge — and eventually detonate.

## The Children

### Isabelle van Rijn / Shin Ji-won

**Born:** January 14, 2001

**Home base:** Wassenaar, Netherlands.

**Appearance:** 5'10", statuesque. Heavy wavy caramel-blonde hair. Pale glowing
glass skin. The flawless posture of a trained dancer — not decorative, but
structural.

**Languages:** Dutch, Korean, English, French, German, Japanese. She downplays
her Japanese fluency; it is simply easier to be underestimated.

**Language map:** Dutch and French are school and inheritance residue. Korean is
hierarchy and discipline — the language of trainee years. English is global
transactions. German is private logistics, conflict, the language she argues in
with Caspar about laundry detergent. Japanese is social tact, the daily
operating language in Tokyo.

**Physical training:** Ballet since age three. The grammar underneath the K-pop
choreography she later learns during the corrective secondment.

### Caspar Waldstein / Ryu Tae-sung

**Born:** March 29, 2001

**Home base:** Munich, Germany.

**Appearance:** 6'1", lean but broad-shouldered. Icy pale blue eyes. After the
secondment: shoulders broadened by agricultural labor, hands roughened by basalt
and pruning. A body built by weather and repetition that reads as older than his
classmates.

**Languages:** German, Korean, English, Dutch, Japanese. He also downplays his
Japanese.

**Language map:** German is his private language with Isabelle — logistics,
disagreement, tenderness. Korean is the language of the cooperative elders and
Ryu-side corporate hierarchy. English is neutral ground. Dutch is an intimacy —
learned for her, used rarely, always noticed. Japanese is the public-facing skin
of daily life.

## The Caretakers

### Moon Jungsook

Assigned to Isabelle since birth. Compact, center-parted bob. Korean in her
cooking and standards, European in her pragmatism. Inspects produce with visible
disapproval. On paper: legal guardian and domestic manager for the Tokyo period.
In practice: archivist of everything the families would prefer to forget. Loyal
to Isabelle first, the families second.

### Bae Myunghi

Assigned to Caspar since birth. Tall, broad-shouldered, permed hair. Irons
creases into school trousers because a man without a crease lacks self-respect.
Her cooking is better than Jungsook's — a source of quiet permanent war. On
paper: legal guardian and domestic manager. In practice: the same as Jungsook.
Loyal to Caspar first.

### Their Unified Front

Jungsook and Myunghi know each other and coordinate off the books. They carry a
fierce, defensive edge born of specific memory: they watched the parents tear
two thirteen-year-olds apart over a panicked loss of control. They were removed
during the corrective secondment. When they are returned and reunited in Tokyo,
they draw a line in the sand. They will not allow the parents to break these
children again.

Their household governance is precise:

- Both teenagers have separate rooms on paper — separate emergency-contact
  forms, separate keys, separate address lines for school records. In practice,
  the caretakers do not intrude beyond breakfast and school attendance.
- No strategic conversation while anyone is standing.
- No visible brand display in domestic space.
- Their only non-negotiable rule is the **First Bowl Rule**: both teenagers eat
  breakfast before leaving the apartment.
- They maintain what amounts to a **household correspondence file** — a parallel
  archive of communications, placement records, and family-office memos. Not
  blackmail. Proof that the adults always understood more than they admitted.

## The Breakpoint (Spring 2014)

At age thirteen, Isabelle and Caspar learn that their meeting was a planned
merger-marriage. Every shared moment is reframed as engineered.

Instead of pulling away, they aggressively reclaim their connection. They make a
runaway pact — forged travel documents, a shared room in a hotel outside Rolle
paid for with a card Caspar was never supposed to use, a plan to disappear into
a geography the families did not control. They are found within forty-eight
hours. The discovery is not of a crime. It is of **cohabitation**: two
thirteen-year-olds sharing a room, a suitcase, and a refusal to be separated.
What the parents find is a threshold already crossed — not sexually, but
structurally. The children have already built a household. They have already
chosen.

When the parents find out, they do not just get angry — they panic. They wanted
a pristine, manageable merger at age twenty-five. They got a volatile,
unsupervised declaration of autonomy at thirteen. The parents act out of sheer
terror at their loss of control, dropping the hammer to hit the brakes. This
triggers the 2.5-year corrective secondment.

## The Corrective Secondment (2014–Early 2017)

Officially, the families called it corrective placement. Unofficially, it was
exile. Caretakers removed. Zero contact.

Their privileges were not removed; they were **converted into productivity**.
That was the parents' preferred form of punishment: not deprivation, but
utility. If the children insisted on becoming autonomous, then autonomy would be
made exhausting. Both remained inside elite corporate placements — just punitive
ones.

### Isabelle in Seoul

She was sent into Shin Entertainment's pre-debut trainee system — a world where
every hour could be justified by rehearsal, evaluation, language drills, and
dorm routine. She trained with the four girls who would become VESPER. She
learned isolation choreography, dorm hierarchy, how to survive on four hours of
sleep and evaluation-day adrenaline. She baked madeleines at 2 AM in a
practice-room kitchenette as stress relief — the only act of European
domesticity she could perform without it being noticed.

**The sacrifice:** When pre-debut evaluations compressed toward a five-member
launch, Isabelle **ceded center position**. She walked into the evaluation room,
told the managers she would accept a voluntary release from the debut lineup,
and asked that her training scores be redistributed to protect the ranking of
the four remaining girls. This was not altruism. It was precision: she knew that
if she debuted, the family name would surface within a news cycle, and the other
four would be consumed by the scandal. She buried her own clip reel, signed the
release paperwork, and made the managers promise the girls would never know it
was a concession rather than a cut. The four debuted. Isabelle stayed to protect
them and then walked out of the system with nothing but the choreography in her
body and the baking in her hands.

### Caspar in Jeju

He was sent to a botanical farming cooperative on Jeju Island — the **Basalt
Gate Cooperative** — operating under Waldstein Botanicals' sourcing arm. The
cooperative expanded after the island's 2006 self-governing turn and development
push, making it policy-enabled capital rather than random rural punishment.

The island's geology did the teaching: volcanic formations, lava-tube systems,
and a landscape shaped by wind. He learned the supply chain from soil to
product:

- **Camellia** — for oil extraction, branding, and the beauty side of the
  dynasty
- **Tea** — for extracts, hospitality, ritual, and the quiet rhythm of harvest
  timing
- **Citrus and Hallabong** — for preserves, fragrance compounds, and
  vitamin-marketing language
- **Mugwort and barley** — for kitchen intimacy, bread, and the daily texture of
  feeding people
- **Black pork and seafood** — for celebratory communal meals, the food you make
  when something matters

He learned **batdam** — basalt stone-wall construction. Field walls, house
walls, fishing walls, grave walls, all built to survive typhoon wind through
elasticity rather than rigidity. He learned to read traditional gate-rod signals
— the wooden bars across a threshold that indicate whether someone is home,
briefly out, or gone for the day. He learned wall repair after sea storms,
pruning after salt wind, storage, stock-making, and the ethics of reading a
house by its threshold.

He also accidentally discovered VESPER's pre-debut content on a fan forum during
a late-night break and became fiercely, privately devoted to the fandom. This is
discoverable evidence. Cached clips, saved fan translations, a shared-drive
folder he thinks no one will ever find.

### What the Secondment Produced

The exile was meant to break their bond. Instead, they survived, hardened, and
learned how to build with their own hands. Caspar did not gain generic "farm
toughness." He gained a supply chain, a domestic vocabulary, and the ability to
read a building before entering it. Isabelle did not gain generic "discipline."
She gained choreography, an understanding of contractual power, and the
knowledge of what it costs to protect people inside a system designed to consume
them.

## Tokyo: The Silent Apology (April 2017)

The parents' retreat was not full surrender. It was a controlled concession.
Both teenagers were placed in Setagaya, enrolled in the same metropolitan high
school under delayed returnee documentation — but housed in separate apartments
with their respective caretakers. Two addresses. Two emergency-contact forms.
Two sets of keys. Proximity without cohabitation. The families were willing to
admit they had failed to break the bond. They were not willing to hand the
children a shared home and call it permission.

Neither Isabelle nor Caspar was told the other would be at the same school. The
caretakers suspected — Jungsook and Myunghi had been coordinating off the books
for years — but they did not confirm it. They had already been removed once for
insufficient control. They would not risk it again until they were certain the
families had no second punishment left to deploy.

The entrance ceremony was, therefore, exactly what it looked like: a collision.
Roll call in a gymnasium full of three hundred freshmen in identical uniforms. A
Dutch-Korean name. A German-Korean name. Eye contact across a room that had no
structural provision for what happened next.

Annex House came later. Within two weeks, Caspar stopped returning to his
apartment. Myunghi called Jungsook. Jungsook called Myunghi. They met at a
family restaurant in the ward, split a bill, and made the decision that the
families would not have authorized: consolidate. One apartment. Two teenagers.
Two caretakers. Separate rooms on paper. A single household in practice. The
families were not consulted. The caretakers filed the address change through
school administration and waited for a phone call from the family offices that
never came — because objecting would mean admitting the arrangement existed, and
admitting the arrangement existed would mean admitting the secondment had
failed.

The silence was the second apology. Annex House was not given. It was built,
again, by the people the families had tried to break.

### The Entrance Ceremony Collision

Japanese high school entrance ceremonies are early-April gymnasium events:
rigid, silent, conformist. Formal speeches, homeroom introductions, applause,
commemorative photos. Three hundred freshmen in identical uniforms, seated in
rows, performing the shared fiction of a clean start.

Isabelle and Caspar hear each other's names during roll call. They lock eyes.

There is no slow-burn realization. There is no waiting for an empty hallway.
After 2.5 years of surviving trainee evaluation rooms and typhoon-season wall
repair to get back to each other, they do not hesitate. Caspar stands up, walks
across the gymnasium floor in front of three hundred freezing freshmen, and
Isabelle meets him. They kiss. Desperate, possessive, European, completely
unapologetic.

The gymnasium short-circuits. In the back row, Jungsook and Myunghi adjust their
purses.

## The "Bulletproof" Dynamic

There is no "will they / won't they" in this story. They possess the energy of a
fifty-year-old married couple trapped in adolescent bodies. The initial mystery
is not "do they like each other?" It is **"what kind of war did these two
survive?"**

Their intimacy is not performed. It is structural. They have already built a
household once, at thirteen, in a hotel room in Switzerland, and the families
tore it apart. Now they build it again, inside school rules and apartment walls,
with the patient, unshakeable confidence of people who have already lost
everything and rebuilt.

The secret is discovered layer by layer. The hurt experienced by the friends

ordinary lives while hiding an entire war. The healing happens at the table —
through the Jeju cooking and Seoul baking that Isabelle and Caspar have always
sincerely shared with them.

## Household Customs at Annex House

Breakfast became law. However badly the morning had gone, both of them had to
cross the kitchen before leaving. The table rotated through barley bread, citrus
preserves, rice porridge, sliced fruit, broth saved from bones, and coffee or
tea depending on who had slept less. Coffee on exam mornings. Tea stronger on
bad days. Black-pork leftovers repurposed into fried rice. Madeleines when
Isabelle cannot sleep.

No strategic conversation while anyone was standing. Wet umbrellas stopped at
the hall. Shopping bags without logos were folded and reused. Fruit peels were
saved, bones were boiled, and nothing edible that had crossed an ocean was
wasted.

Off school grounds, Caspar wore plain, weathered island workwear — breathable,
sun-faded, built for movement. Isabelle wore immaculate, minimal clothing with
quietly better construction than anyone could quite explain. Neither wore logos.
Both wore durability.

Their friends slowly understood that this was not eccentricity. It was how
survivors made a house behave like safety.

**Household taboos:**

- Wasting imported food.
- Discussing board politics at the table.
- Arriving at school without having eaten.

## Work — Choosing the Dynasty

Isabelle and Caspar do not join school clubs. Out of their own free will, they
take **school-approved vocational placements** — Isabelle at Shin Entertainment
Japan's Tokyo office, Caspar at Ryu BioPharma Japan's liaison lab. These are
structured as vacation observer programs and supervised company shadowing, filed
through proper homeroom-teacher approval and job-permission paperwork. They
fetch water and scan inventory under bosses who know exactly who they are.

This signals absolute victory on their own terms. The parents tried to force the
merger and failed. The teens choose the companies, and each other, autonomously
— not as heirs, but as applicants. The endgame is staged as a decision made
after graduation or after sanctioned pre-graduation placement, making the
victory read as earned rather than administratively impossible.

## The Stealth Wealth

The wealth never performs. It serves. Name the material, not the brand. Show the
absence of logos. Deploy wealth as quiet problem-solving: a suspiciously smooth
car ride, an impossibly sourced ingredient, a tailored uniform seam, paperwork
that moves faster than it should, an apartment in Setagaya that is slightly too
large and too quiet for two teenagers and two guardians.

The friends notice the gap between what Isabelle and Caspar treat as basic and
what is actually basic. The wealth reveals itself through logistics, tailoring,
ingredients, and institutional access — never through display.

## Plot Engines

These are grounded pressure points — not melodrama, but institutional friction:

- A **leaked trainee-era clip** threatens not the romance, but Isabelle's
  promise to the four VESPER girls. If someone identifies her face, the story of
  her ceding center surfaces, and the girls learn their debut was a concession
  rather than a victory.
- A **cooperative worker files a dispute** over ownership of a camellia-derived
  formulation that the Waldstein brand tried to absorb as "heritage sourcing."
  Caspar is caught between the island community that raised him and the family
  that exiled him there.
- A **homeroom teacher demands proper documentation** for the vocational
  placements, exposing just how arranged the "normal" life really is. The school
  bureaucracy pushes back on the dynasty.
- **Ogawa Shun** recognizes that Caspar reads houses the way other people read
  faces — checking thresholds, noting gate signals, scanning a room before
  sitting — and tracks the habit back to labor years on the island.
- The **caretakers' parallel archive** becomes a weapon: not blackmail, but
  proof that the adults always understood more than they admitted. The household
  correspondence file can surface at the moment of maximum institutional
  pressure.
- **Board politics re-enter through philanthropy first**, not direct coercion: a
  scholarship, a cultural grant, a school donation, a "friendly" invitation to a
  gala. The families do not storm back in. They fund their way back in.

## Tone and Pacing

**Tone:** Cinematic, grounded, punchy. The comedy is purely **specific cultural
friction**: two European trauma-bonded teenagers colliding with concrete school
rules about phone storage, job permits, hair standards, and physical-contact
norms. No cheap drama, no love triangles, no national stereotypes.

**Dialogue:** Identifiable speech patterns. Husbands-and-wives banter. Arguments
in hushed German about laundry detergent, grocery logistics, and whose caretaker
overcooked the rice. They switch languages by jurisdiction and emotional
register, and the friends notice before they understand.

**Food as care:** He cooks (Jeju — barley, citrus, camellia oil, black pork,
seafood, broth from saved bones). She bakes (Seoul — madeleines, pastry, the
muscle memory of 2 AM stress relief in a practice-room kitchenette). It is how
they tell the friends, and each other, that they are safe.
