# Chapter 12: Somebody Knows

## I.

Saturday night. The VESPER group chat.

Isabelle sat on her bedroom floor with her back against the bed, her knees drawn
up, her phone in both hands. The apartment was quiet—Auntie Choi had gone to bed
at 10, her room dark, the only sound the soft mechanical hum of the imported
German refrigerator maintaining its precise four degrees Celsius.

She had been staring at the chat for eleven minutes.

The last message was from Yuna, sent at 8:47 PM—three hours after the showcase,
two hours after the members had been released from the venue, one hour after
Yuna had presumably showered, changed, eaten whatever the agency staff had
prepared for post-event recovery, and sat down with her phone and the specific,
accumulated patience of a woman who had recognized her surrogate little sister
in a fan queue and had been waiting to detonate ever since.

> **유나언니:** 신지원.
>
> _Shin Ji-won._

That was it. Two words. Full name. No emojis. No exclamation marks. No aegyo.
Just the name, deployed like a summons, carrying the weight of someone who knew
exactly what she had seen and was giving Isabelle exactly one chance to explain
before the interrogation began in earnest.

Isabelle typed.

> **지원:** 언니. 오늘 행사에 갔어요.

## II.

Sunday morning. The Hiroo apartment.

Caspar sat at the kitchen table eating the breakfast Auntie Park had made—rice,

The message was in Korean. It read:

> **Unknown:** 캐스퍼-씨. 저 서유나입니다. 지원이가 번호 줬어요. 그냥 한 가지만

He stared at the screen.

He set down his chopsticks.

He looked at the message again.

_This is Seo Yuna._

Seo Yuna. Center-left position. Main vocalist. The voice that sang the bridge.
The lazy-cat smile. The woman whose photo card he had traded for on VESPERspace
six weeks ago, a limited-edition holographic variant from the debut showcase,
which he kept in a protective sleeve inside the filing box in his darkroom
between the Bavarian Alps negatives and the Tokyo day-one negatives, because the
filing box was the safest place he owned and the photo card was the most
valuable thing in it.

Seo Yuna had his phone number. Seo Yuna was texting him. At 7:14 on a Sunday
morning.

He picked up his chopsticks. He set them down again. He picked up the phone.

> **류태성:** 안녕하세요, 유나 씨. 캐스퍼입니다.
>
> _Hello, Yuna-ssi. This is Caspar._

The reply was instantaneous.

> **유나:** 반말해도 돼요? 나 지원이 언니니까.
>
> _Can we speak casually? I'm Ji-won's unnie, after all._

> **류태성:** 네.
>
> _Sure._

> **유나:** 좋아. 그러면 간단하게.
>
> _Good. Then I'll keep it simple._

> **유나:** 지원이는 내 동생이야. 피가 안 섞였어도 동생이야. 걔가 널 좋아한다는
> 건 네가 좋은 사람이라는 뜻이야. 지원이 눈은 틀린 적이 없거든.
>
> _Ji-won is my little sister. Not by blood, but my sister. The fact that she
> likes you means you're a good person. Ji-won's judgment has never been wrong._

> **유나:** 근데.
>
> _But._

> **유나:** 울리면 나 도쿄 간다.
>

Caspar read the message. He read the _but._ He read the threat—delivered with
the casual specificity of someone who meant every syllable and who had the

>
> _I won't make her cry._

>

> **유나:** 그리고 하나 더.
>

> **류태성:** 뭔데?

> **유나:** 지원이가 네가 "Glass Hour" 포인트 안무 출 수 있다고 했어. 진짜야?
>
> _Ji-won said you can do the "Glass Hour" point choreo. Is that true?_

> **류태성:** 네.
>

He stared at the phone. Seo Yuna—his bias, his favorite member of his favorite
group, the voice that made him cry on the inside—was asking him to send a video
of himself performing her group's choreography.

> **류태성:** 지금요?
>
> _Right now?_

> **유나:** 지금 말고 언제? 빨리.
>
> _If not now, when? Hurry._

He set the phone down. He looked at the kitchen. Auntie Park was at the stove,
her back to him, stirring something. The morning light came through the window
at a low angle, painting the oak floor in the familiar parallelograms of gold.

He stood up. He walked to the center of the living room. He positioned the phone
against a stack of photography books on the coffee table, angled up, the camera
framing him from the waist up. He pressed record.

He stood still for two seconds. Then he performed the chorus of "Glass Hour"—the
point choreo, the wrist flick, the body roll, the hip isolation, the final
extended hand—in the middle of his living room, in his pajama pants and an
undershirt, his hair uncombed, his feet bare on the heated oak floor, with
Auntie Park's kimchi-jjigae bubbling on the stove behind him.

He stopped recording. He watched the playback. The execution was clean—not
performance-grade, not stage-ready, but precise in the way that mattered: the
timing was right, the isolations were sharp, the intention was clear. He was not
performing for an audience. He was performing for one person, and the one person
was Seo Yuna, and the performance needed to communicate not skill but
sincerity—the sincerity of a boy who had watched this choreography a hundred
times in a hotel corridor and who had learned it not to show off but to feel
close to something beautiful.

He sent the video.

The "read" indicator appeared. Three seconds passed. Five. Eight.

> **유나:** 민지가 "자세 좋다" 래. 하은이가 웃으면서 울어. 지우가 아무 말 안

> **유나:** 진짜 합격.

Caspar set the phone on the table. He sat back on the sofa. He looked at the

"누구야?" Auntie Park said from the kitchen. _Who was that?_

"아무도 아니에요." _Nobody._

"아무도 아닌 사람한테 문자 받으면서 거실에서 춤추는 거야?" _You dance in the

"..."

"밥 먹어라." _Eat your breakfast._

He looked at the message. A signed photo card. From Yuna. His bias. Sent
directly. To him. Because his girlfriend's idol-group sister had decided he was
approved and was now expressing that approval through the fan economy's most
valuable currency.

He didn't know what to do with his face. The stoic architecture was failing—not
cracking, not collapsing, but simply _insufficient_, the way a container becomes
insufficient when the thing inside it grows larger than the container was
designed to hold.

He was smiling. He was smiling the full smile, the one that used his entire
face, the one Auntie Park had seen for the first time after the first kiss, the
one that made the ceiling look like it was smiling back.

Auntie Park glanced at him from the stove. She saw the smile. She did not
comment. She turned back to the stove and stirred, and the small, private
softening of her shoulders said everything her voice did not.

## III.

Sunday afternoon. Isabelle's apartment.

She called him at 3 PM. Not LINE—a phone call. The actual, old-fashioned act of
dialing a number and hearing a voice in real time, without the mediation of
text, without the safety of editing and deleting and the three-dot typing
indicator that gave you time to reconsider.

He picked up on the second ring.

"여보세요." _Hello._

"유나 언니가 연락했지?" _Yuna contacted you, right?_

A pause. "네 번호를 줬어?" _You gave her my number?_

"어젯밤에. VESPER 단톡에 다 얘기했어. 우리 얘기. 전부 다." _Last night. I told
the VESPER group chat everything. About us. All of it._

"알아. 유나가 오늘 아침에 문자했어." _I know. Yuna texted me this morning._

"뭐라고 했어?" _What did she say?_

"울리면 도쿄 온다고." _That she'll come to Tokyo if I make you cry._

Isabelle laughed. The laugh moved through the phone like warm water—full,
unguarded, the sound of a girl who was lying on her bed in a Sunday afternoon
apartment with the light coming through the windows and the weight of one secret
lighter on her shoulders.

"그리고?" _And?_

"포인트 안무 영상 보내라고 했어." _She asked me to send a video of the point
choreo._

"보냈어?" _Did you send it?_

"보냈어." _I sent it._

"캐스퍼." _Caspar._

"응." _Yeah._

"거실에서 잠옷 입고 춤췄어?" _Did you dance in your living room in your
pajamas?_

A silence. The silence of a person who was deciding how much dignity to concede.

"잠옷 바지랑 속옷이었어." _Pajama pants and an undershirt._

She laughed again. Harder this time, the laugh building on itself, feeding on
the image—Caspar, six-one, icy-eyed, stoic-faced Caspar, performing K-pop
choreography in his living room in pajama pants while his housekeeper made
jjigae in the background.

"유나 언니가 멤버들한테 보여줬어?" _Did Yuna show the members?_

"응. 민지가 '자세 좋다'래. 하은이가 울었다고. 지우가 세 번 돌려봤대." _Yeah.
Minji said 'good form.' Haeun cried. Jiwoo watched it three times._

"지우가 세 번 봤다는 건 인상 깊었다는 뜻이야. 지우는 뭐든 한 번만 봐. 세 번이면
최고의 칭찬이야." _Jiwoo watching three times means she was impressed. Jiwoo
only watches anything once. Three times is the highest compliment._

Caspar was quiet. She could hear him breathing—steady, even, the breathing of
someone who was processing something that exceeded his emotional bandwidth and
who was handling it the way he handled all overwhelming input: by being very
still and letting it settle.

"이자벨." _Isabelle._

"응." _Yeah._

"내 비아스가 나한테 문자했어." _My bias texted me._

The sentence was delivered with a flatness that was, Isabelle understood,
Caspar's version of screaming. The stoic monotone that meant the emotion was so
intense it had overloaded the expressive system and the only option was to state
the fact as plainly as possible and trust the listener to understand the
magnitude of what was being understated.

"알아." _I know._

"사인 포카 보내준대." _She's sending me a signed photo card._

"알아." _I know._

"이자벨." _Isabelle._

"응." _Yeah._

"너 없이는 이 중에 하나도 없었을 거야. 학교도. 친구들도. 사진도. 유나한테 문자
받는 것도. 다 너 때문이야. 전부 다." _None of this would exist without you. Not
the school. Not the friends. Not the photos. Not getting texts from Yuna. It's
all because of you. All of it._

She heard the words. She felt them—not as flattery, not as a compliment, but as
a statement of fact delivered by someone who did not waste words and who was, in
this moment, using them the way he used film: sparingly, precisely, each one
earning its place in the frame.

"아니야," she said. _No._

"아니야?" _No?_

"나 때문이 아니야. 우리 때문이야." _It's not because of me. It's because of us._

A pause. She could hear him breathing. She could hear, distantly, the sound of
Auntie Park in the kitchen—the clink of dishes, the low murmur of the Korean
news broadcast, the domestic percussion of a household in motion.

"우리," he said. _Us._

"우리." _Us._

The word sat between them—carried across the phone connection, across the twelve
kilometers between Hiroo and Minami-Aoyama, across the entire distance they had
traveled since a school gate and a nod and twelve words in Korean. _Us._ The
word that contained the bench and the strawberries and the madeleines and the
darkroom and the photographs and the riverbank and the hand-holding and the
kisses and the fan event and the secrets told and the secrets shared and the
Aunties coordinating and the friends converging and the tarte au citron and the
éclairs and the LINE thread that ran beneath every day like an underground
river, carrying the weight of everything they felt in a language only they
shared.

"잘 자, 남자친구." _Good night, boyfriend._ Even though it was 3 PM. Even though
the sun was streaming through her window. The ritual. The touchstone. The phrase
that had become, through daily repetition, as essential as breathing.

"잘 자, 여자친구." _Good night, girlfriend._

She hung up. She lay on her bed, phone on her chest, the ceiling above her white
and featureless, and she thought about the word _us_ and the way it sounded in
Korean—_uri_, a single syllable, soft and round, a word that meant _we_ and
_our_ and _the thing that belongs to both of us_, a word that contained within
its two letters the entire architecture of what they had built: not a
relationship that had been arranged by their parents or engineered by corporate
strategy, but something that they had chosen, step by step, day by day, secret
by secret, from a school gate to a riverbank to a Shibuya fan event to this
moment, this Sunday, this quiet apartment, this ceiling, this light.

_Uri._

She closed her eyes.

She smiled.

## IV.

Monday morning.

They walked through the gate together. Hands linked. His camera bag over one
shoulder. Her school bag over the other. The June light warm on their faces, the
cherry trees fully green, the courtyard buzzing with the ordinary, extraordinary
energy of four hundred teenagers starting another week.

Kenji was waiting at the shoe lockers. He had a bag of melon pan from the bakery
near the station, and he was distributing them to the group with the ceremonial
gravity of a man performing a sacred duty.

"HCCL plus delegates!" he announced. "Melon pan Monday! New tradition! I'm
funding this one personally!"

"How much did eight melon pan cost?" Yuki asked.

"That's not the point, Takahashi. The point is community."

"The point is 1,280 yen."

"The point is _community._"

Sora arrived, still radiating the post-event glow of someone who had high-fived
Seo Yuna forty-eight hours ago and who was carrying the experience like a torch.
She showed Kenji her phone—a photo of her wristband, still on her wrist, which
she had not removed and which she announced she would "never remove, this is
permanent, this is a tattoo of the soul."

Daiki arrived with a new analysis: the sound design of "Somebody Knows," broken
down into frequency bands, annotated with timestamps, presented in a spreadsheet
he had built on Sunday afternoon.

Ren arrived, fresh from a morning run, his breathing still slightly elevated. He
accepted a melon pan, ate it in four bites, and said, "Good weekend?"

"Good weekend," Caspar said.

Hina arrived last. She slipped into the group the way she always did—appearing
at the periphery, settling into her place, her presence registering not through
announcement but through the specific quality of attention she brought to every
gathering. She accepted a melon pan from Kenji. She ate it slowly.

She looked at Isabelle.

Isabelle looked at Hina.

Their eyes met. The contact lasted three seconds—three seconds of communication
conducted entirely in the register of two people who both understood that seeing
was a gift and that the most important thing about a gift was knowing when to
give it and when to withhold it.

Hina smiled. The rare smile. The real one.

Then she turned to the group and said, in the clearest, steadiest voice Isabelle
had ever heard from her: "This is nice."

Seven people looked at her. Hina did not elaborate. She ate her melon pan and
looked at the morning light coming through the entrance hall windows, and her
expression was the expression of someone who had been watching from the margins
for a long time and who had decided, quietly and definitively, that she was
ready to step inside.

Isabelle felt something settle in her chest. Not a resolution—not an ending, not
a conclusion. A foundation. The feeling of something being built that would hold
weight. The feeling of a structure becoming sound.

She looked at the group—at Kenji distributing bread, at Sora showing phone
photos, at Yuki calculating costs, at Daiki presenting spreadsheets, at Ren
stretching, at Hina smiling, at Caspar standing beside her with his hand in hers
and his camera over his shoulder and his pale blue eyes steady and warm and
exactly where she needed them to be.

She thought: _This is us._

She thought: _Uri._

She squeezed his hand. He squeezed back.

The bell rang. The day began.
