# Chapter 19: Two Weeks in Seoul

---

## I

Seoul in late July was a city that sweated.

Not metaphorically—literally, physically, the concrete and glass and steel
surfaces of Gangnam and Cheongdam and Apgujeong covered in a fine condensation
that caught the light and made the city look like it had been wrapped in
cellophane. The humidity was absolute. The air was a material—thick, warm,
carrying the smell of hot pavement and grilled meat and the jasmine that bloomed
on rooftop gardens and fire escapes and every unclaimed surface where a plant
could survive. Walking outside was an act of submission to climate. The body
conceded. The clothing conceded. The hair conceded. Even the buildings conceded,
their air conditioning units humming in desperate, continuous chorus, the
mechanical protest of a city fighting its own atmosphere.

Isabelle had forgotten this about Seoul. Or rather, she had remembered it
selectively—the way you remembered a difficult year by keeping the beautiful
parts and storing the uncomfortable parts in a lower-priority archive. The gap
year had been a winter arrival—she had landed in October, trained through the
cold months, and experienced the Seoul summer only in its first weeks before her
departure in August. She had forgotten the weight of the air. She had forgotten
the way her hair, which in Tokyo's milder humidity maintained a disciplined
wave, expanded in Seoul's atmosphere into a volume that Yuna described as
"magnificent" and that Isabelle experienced as "structurally unsound."

She was standing outside a convenience store in Cheongdam at 10 AM, holding two
bottles of Pocari Sweat and waiting for Caspar, and her hair was doing the
thing, and the morning was already thirty-one degrees, and she was happier than
she had been in months.

He appeared around the corner. He was wearing a white linen shirt—already
translucent with sweat at the shoulders—and light trousers and sneakers and the
Leica around his neck, the camera a permanent appendage, as much a part of his
body as his hands. His hair, which in Tokyo's drier air sat obediently in its
pushed-back configuration, had responded to the Seoul humidity by developing a
slight wave at the temples—a curl that he hadn't known he possessed and that
Isabelle found unreasonably endearing.

"너 머리 곱슬이야," she said, handing him a Pocari Sweat. _Your hair curls._

He took the bottle. He touched his temple—the reflexive gesture of a person
discovering a new physical fact about themselves. "습기 때문이야." _It's the
humidity._

"귀엽다." _It's cute._

"귀엽지 않아." _It's not cute._

"귀여워." _It is._

He drank the Pocari Sweat. He looked at her—at her hair, which was achieving a
volume that made her head appear approximately thirty percent larger than usual,
and which, in the morning light, with the Seoul haze softening everything, gave
her the appearance of a Renaissance painting of a saint who had been
electrocuted.

"네 머리도 그러는데." _Your hair is doing it too._

"알아. 포기했어." _I know. I gave up._

"포기가 잘 어울려." _Giving up suits you._

She hit his arm. He caught her hand. They held onto each other—hand in hand, on
a Cheongdam sidewalk at 10 AM, surrounded by Seoul's morning traffic and the
smell of the bakery next door and the ambient hum of a neighborhood where the
buildings cost more per square meter than most cities' average homes and where
two teenagers with European passports and Korean names were, for two and a half
weeks, just another couple in the heat.

---

They had developed a routine.

Mornings: they walked. Caspar photographed and Isabelle navigated, guiding him
through the Seoul she knew—not the tourist Seoul of palaces and markets and the
Instagram-curated streets of Bukchon, but the Seoul of back alleys and service
entrances and the specific, unglamorous geography of a city she had experienced
from the inside out. They walked through the streets behind Shin Entertainment,
where the trainees bought convenience store meals at midnight. They walked along
the Cheongcheon Stream, where Isabelle had run in the early mornings before
practice, her breath clouding in the October air, the water running shallow over
its concrete bed. They walked through the Gangnam station underground, the vast
subterranean mall where she had bought her first Korean shoes and where Haeun
had taught her to haggle in the dialect of Daegu, Haeun's hometown, because
"standard Korean haggling is for amateurs."

Caspar photographed everything. He shot three rolls in the first four days—Tri-X
for the back alleys and the service streets, Portra for the mornings when the
light turned the concrete gold, Ilford HP5 for the underground, where the
fluorescent lighting created a flat, shadowless world that felt, he told her,
"like photographing the inside of a thought."

Isabelle watched him work. She had watched him photograph in Tokyo—in
Daikanyama, in Yanaka, at the school, on the riverbank. But Seoul changed him.
The way he moved through these streets was different—more alert, more
responsive, his body reacting to the city's visual density the way a musician's
body reacted to a complex score. Seoul was louder than Tokyo. More layered. More
insistent. The signage competed for attention in three scripts—Korean, English,
Chinese—and the architecture shifted register every fifty meters, from brutalist
office blocks to traditional hanok to glass towers to cramped shopfronts stacked
on top of each other in configurations that defied structural engineering. The
visual noise was immense, and Caspar navigated it by narrowing—by finding, in
the chaos, the single frame that was still, that was true, that said the thing
the noise was drowning out.

"도쿄는 침묵으로 말해," he said one morning, sitting on a bench by the
Cheongcheon Stream, changing a roll of film with the practiced, sightless motion
of someone who could load a camera by touch. _Tokyo speaks through silence._

"그리고 서울은?" _And Seoul?_

"서울은 전부 다 동시에 말해. 내 일은 하나만 듣는 거야." _Seoul says everything
at once. My job is to hear just one thing._

---

Afternoons: the practice room.

Isabelle spent most afternoons at the agency. Not working—she was officially on
leave from her part-time role during the Seoul visit, a concession extracted by
Auntie Choi via a phone call to the Tokyo office that had reportedly involved
the phrase "the child needs rest" delivered with the authority of a woman for
whom "child" was a tactical designation rather than a developmental stage.

But the practice room pulled her. The rubber floor and the mirror wall and the
speakers and the smell—the smell of work, of effort, of the specific chemical
compound produced by the human body when it was pushed past comfort into
competence. She would arrive after lunch, change into practice clothes—black
leggings, a tank top, sneakers she kept in the dorm closet—and stretch in the
empty room, alone, her body relearning the shapes it had held for ten months.

She didn't dance. Not the VESPER choreography, not the routines she had
memorized, not the sequences that lived in her muscles like a second language.
She stretched. She held positions. She ran through barre exercises from her
Amsterdam ballet days—pliés, tendus, relevés—the foundational movements that
were the grammar beneath every other physical language she had learned. The
practice room was quiet during these hours—VESPER rehearsed in the mornings, and
the afternoon was for vocal coaching and media training and the administrative
work of being a group that was selling out arenas—and the quiet suited her. It
was the quiet of a room that was resting between uses, the quiet of potential
energy, the quiet of a stage before the lights came on.

Caspar joined her sometimes. He would sit against the mirror wall with the Leica
in his lap and watch her stretch—not photographing, just watching, the camera
unused, his attention fully on the movement of her body through the positions.
She had told him, once, that his watching felt different from other people's
watching—that when most people watched her move, she could feel their eyes on
the surface of her body, on the shape and the line and the external appearance
of the movement, but when Caspar watched, she could feel him watching _inside_
the movement, watching the mechanics, the intention, the decision-making that
happened between one position and the next.

"그건 사진가의 눈이야," she had said. _That's the photographer's eye._

"아니야," he had said. _No._ "그건 네 눈이야. 너를 보는 법을 너한테서
배웠으니까." _That's your eye. I learned how to look at you from you._

She had not had a response to this. She had continued stretching, and he had
continued watching, and the practice room had held them both in its quiet,
fluorescent light, and neither of them had needed to say anything else.

## II

The second week. The dinner.

Helena Waldstein had been in Seoul for five days—meetings with the Waldstein
Botanicals Seoul R&D team, facility tours, the quarterly review of the Korean
market strategy that accounted for approximately forty percent of the company's
global revenue. She worked the way she always worked: in twelve-hour blocks,
with breaks for meals and calls and the occasional walk through the Cheongdam
streets that she knew intimately from twenty years of business trips and that
she navigated with the proprietary ease of someone whose products were sold in
every department store she passed.

On Thursday evening, she called Caspar.

"Dinner tonight. The Shilla. Eight o'clock. Bring Isabelle." A pause. "And
Shin-ssi will be there."

Shin-ssi. Shin Hee-yeon. Isabelle's mother. The CEO of Shin Entertainment. The
woman who had placed her daughter in a practice room with four trainees and who
had built, from that placement and a hundred other placements and a thousand
other decisions, the entertainment company that was now producing the biggest
girl group in Asia.

Caspar told Isabelle. He told her in person, in the practice room, sitting
against the mirror wall while she cooled down from a stretching session, her
skin flushed, her hair tied back, a towel around her neck.

"오늘 저녁. 신라 호텔. 8시. 우리 엄마들." _Tonight. Shilla Hotel. Eight o'clock.
Our mothers._

Isabelle's stretching towel stopped moving. She looked at him—not with surprise,
not with anxiety, but with the particular focused stillness of someone who had
known this was coming and who was now, in the moment of its arrival, shifting
from anticipation to preparation.

"드디어." _Finally._

"준비됐어?" _Are you ready?_

"이건 준비해서 되는 게 아니야. 이건—" She paused, searching. "—이건 그냥
살아내는 거야." _You can't prepare for this. This is just—you survive it._

"우리 엄마 이미 만났잖아. 공항에서." _You already met my mom. At the airport._

"그건 달라. 그건 도착이었어. 오늘 밤은—" She looked at the practice room. At the
mirrors, the speakers, the rubber floor. "—오늘 밤은 비즈니스야." _That was
different. That was an arrival. Tonight is—tonight is business._

She was right. The airport had been personal—a mother meeting her son's
girlfriend, the chemistry of two individuals. Tonight would be structural. Two
mothers. Two CEOs. The heads of two corporate empires sitting across a table
from each other and conducting, beneath the surface of dinner conversation, the
real negotiation: the assessment of whether the relationship between their
children was an asset or a complication, a convergence or a collision, a thing
to be supported or a thing to be managed.

"이자벨." _Isabelle._

"응." _Yeah._

"오늘 밤에 무슨 일이 있어도, 우리는 우리야. 그건 안 변해." _Whatever happens
tonight, we are us. That doesn't change._

She looked at him. The boy in the practice room, the photographer with the
curling hair, the fan who had danced in his living room in pajama pants, the
porter who had carried luggage and learned to see. The boy who had turned away
from her kiss on a Swiss terrace and who had, four months later, on a Tokyo
street corner with a crow as witness, kissed her with the full, unhurried
intention of someone who had decided that the timing was finally right.

"알아," she said. _I know._

"그럼 됐어." _Then that's enough._

## III

The Shilla Hotel's private dining room was on the twenty-third floor.

The room was designed for exactly this kind of occasion—a space that
communicated, through its materials and proportions and the particular quality
of its silence, that the people inside it operated at a level where privacy was
not a luxury but a structural requirement. The table was set for four: white
linen, crystal glassware, Noritake bone china, silverware laid with the
geometric precision of a technical drawing. The view through the
floor-to-ceiling windows was the Seoul nightscape—the Han River, the towers, the
bridges lit with LED sequences that turned the water into a slow-moving light
show.

Helena arrived first. She was wearing a black silk blouse and gray trousers and
a single piece of jewelry—a thin gold chain at her throat, the Waldstein
Botanicals logo rendered in miniature, the pendant so small and so fine that it
was visible only to someone who knew to look for it. She stood at the window and
looked at the city the way she looked at everything: with the evaluative
attention of someone who was assessing not just the view but the systems that
produced it.

Caspar and Isabelle arrived together. He wore a dark navy suit—no tie, the
collar open, the same configuration Auntie Park had prescribed for Daikanyama
but elevated for the occasion: the fabric finer, the cut sharper, the overall
effect communicating the specific, restrained elegance of old European money
expressing itself through the absence of anything that could be called flashy.
Isabelle wore a black dress—simple, structured, falling to just below the knee,
the kind of garment that in a department store would cost 5,000 yen and that
this particular version, cut from Italian wool crepe by an atelier in Amsterdam
that had been dressing the van Rijn women for two generations, cost
approximately a hundred times that amount. She wore no jewelry except the small
pearl studs that her father had given her for her thirteenth birthday and that
she wore for occasions that required her to look like what she was.

"You both look wonderful," Helena said, in English. The neutral language. The
language of international business and the specific, shared vocabulary of
families whose members held passports from multiple countries.

"Thank you, Mama," Caspar said.

"Thank you, Frau Waldstein," Isabelle said.

"Helena," Helena said. "Please."

Isabelle received this—the first-name invitation, the lowering of the formal
barrier, the signal that Helena was operating tonight not as
CEO-to-CEO's-daughter but as something closer to family. She received it with a
nod and a small, warm expression that communicated her understanding of the
gesture's weight.

"Helena," Isabelle said.

Then the door opened.

Shin Hee-yeon entered the room the way a conductor entered an orchestra pit—with
the specific, calibrated authority of someone who understood that every space
she occupied was, by definition, her stage. She was not tall—five-four, compact,
with a frame that suggested both delicacy and tensile strength, the body of a
woman who had been a dancer herself before becoming the person who managed
dancers. Her hair was black, cut in a sharp bob that framed her face in clean
angles. Her makeup was minimal and precise—the same philosophy as Isabelle's,
the same less-is-more approach, the genetic or cultural or simply aesthetic
conviction that a face should be enhanced, not constructed.

She was wearing a white blazer over a black sheath dress. No jewelry. No
accessories. The outfit communicated what Shin Hee-yeon always communicated:
that she was the most important thing in any room she entered, and that the
room's job was to accommodate this fact.

She looked at the room. She looked at Helena. She looked at Caspar. She looked
at Isabelle.

The look she gave Isabelle lasted two seconds. It was not cold. It was not warm.
It was _specific_—the look of a mother who loved her daughter the way she loved
her artists: fiercely, strategically, and with a professional's awareness that
the line between personal investment and professional management was, in her
life, so fine as to be functionally invisible.

"지원아," Shin Hee-yeon said.

"엄마." _Mom._

The word was quiet. Isabelle said it the way she said everything that
mattered—with composure, with precision, with the careful management of emotion
that her mother had modeled for her entire life and that she had absorbed,
through observation and inheritance, into the architecture of her own
personality.

Shin Hee-yeon crossed the room. She stood in front of her daughter. She raised
one hand and placed it—briefly, precisely—against Isabelle's cheek. The touch
lasted one second. It was the same gesture, Isabelle realized, that Caspar had
made on the street corner before their first kiss: a hand on the face, a touch
that said _I see you_ in the language of skin.

"키 컸네," Shin Hee-yeon said. _You've grown._ She removed her hand. She turned
to Caspar.

"류태성 군." _Ryu Tae-sung._

"신 사장님." _CEO Shin._

"희연이라고 불러. 오늘은 비즈니스 아니니까." _Call me Hee-yeon. Tonight isn't
business._

Caspar looked at her. Shin Hee-yeon's eyes were the same shape as
Isabelle's—large, slightly upturned, the same hazel-green that appeared in
certain lights and that was, Caspar now understood, a genetic inheritance from
this woman, a physical trace of the mother in the daughter, the lineage visible
in the specific color of the iris.

"희연 이모님," Caspar said. _Aunt Hee-yeon._ The Korean honorific for a mother's
female peer—not the formal address of a business meeting but the familial
address of a boy speaking to his girlfriend's mother with the intention of
communicating that he understood the relationship and was taking it seriously.

Shin Hee-yeon's expression moved. Not dramatically—the woman did not do dramatic
expressions, she did calibrations, adjustments, the small, precise movements of
a face that had been photographed and filmed and analyzed for decades and that
had long ago learned to communicate maximum information through minimum
displacement. But the movement was there: a softening at the corners of her
eyes, a fractional lift at the edges of her mouth, the reaction of a woman who
had been addressed correctly and who appreciated the effort.

"앉자," she said. _Let's sit._

---

They sat. The dinner began.

The food was kaiseki-influenced Korean—a progression of small courses that
arrived at intervals determined by a staff whose timing was calibrated to the
rhythm of the conversation, the plates appearing during pauses and receding
during exchanges with the invisible precision of a system designed to serve
without interrupting.

The conversation moved through its own progression. The opening: pleasantries,
travel reports, the surface exchange of information about flights and hotels and
the weather, the social equivalent of a warm-up, the low-intensity activity that
preceded the real effort.

Then Helena, with the directness that was her signature, shifted the register.

"Hee-yeon," she said. "I want to talk about the children."

Shin Hee-yeon set down her chopsticks. She placed her hands in her lap. She
looked at Helena with the expression of someone who had been waiting for this
transition and who was ready for it.

"Of course," she said.

"They're together. We both know this. We've known since before they arrived at
the same school—which neither of us arranged by accident."

The sentence landed on the table between the crystal and the Noritake. The
acknowledgment—open, explicit, undeniable—that the parents had engineered the
proximity. That the school placement was not coincidence. That the corporate
families had made a move, and the move had produced a result, and the result was
the two teenagers sitting across the table who were hearing, for the first time
from their parents' own mouths, the confirmation of what they had always
suspected.

Isabelle looked at Caspar. Caspar looked at Isabelle. The look was brief,
steady, and contained the full weight of the conversation they had had on the
Minami-Aoyama sidewalk: _I know why we're here. I've known since my parents told
me the school._

"The question," Helena continued, "is what we do with this. Do we treat it as a
corporate development? Do we fold it into the Waldstein-Shin partnership
timeline? Do we attach expectations and conditions and the weight of two family
legacies to something that is, at its core, a relationship between two
fifteen-year-olds who found each other before we found a use for them?"

The room was silent. The Seoul skyline glittered through the
windows—indifferent, beautiful, the city that contained both families' ambitions
and that continued its existence regardless of the decisions being made on its
twenty-third floor.

Shin Hee-yeon looked at Helena. "What are you proposing?"

"I'm proposing that we leave them alone."

The sentence was simple. Five words. The most counterintuitive thing Helena
Waldstein could say—a woman whose entire professional identity was built on
control, on design, on the precise engineering of outcomes, proposing that the
most important outcome of the year be left unengineered.

"The partnership between Waldstein Botanicals and Shin Entertainment will
proceed on its own merits," Helena said. "The brand collaboration, the tour
sponsorship, the product lines—all of that continues. It has nothing to do with
them." She looked at Caspar. "My son is not a merger instrument. He's a person.
He fell in love with your daughter not because we placed them in proximity but
because she is—" Helena paused. For the first time in Caspar's memory, his
mother searched for a word. "—she is the person he was always going to find."

Caspar felt something shift behind his ribs. His mother—the woman who
communicated through objects and arrangements, who expressed love through
design, who had sent a toaster across the world because she couldn't send
herself—had just, in a private dining room on the twenty-third floor of the
Shilla Hotel, said the thing she had never said to him directly. _He fell in
love._

Shin Hee-yeon was quiet. She looked at her daughter. Isabelle looked back—the
same hazel-green eyes, the same cheekbone structure, the same composure, the
same fierce, strategic intelligence operating behind a face that gave away only
what it chose to.

"지원아," Shin Hee-yeon said. "행복하니?" _Ji-won. Are you happy?_

The question—the same question Minji had asked in the VESPER group chat, the
same two-word inquiry that cut through every layer of corporate architecture and
family legacy and strategic positioning and arrived at the only thing that
actually mattered.

"네, 엄마. 행복해요." _Yes, Mom. I'm happy._

Shin Hee-yeon looked at her daughter for a long time. Five seconds. Ten. The
gaze of a woman who had built an empire and raised a child and who understood,
with the clarity that came from having done both, that the two achievements were
not comparable—that the empire was a thing she had made and the child was a
person she had loved, and that the person deserved, at minimum, the same care
and attention and strategic genius that the empire received.

"그러면 됐어," Shin Hee-yeon said. _Then that's enough._

The same words Minji had used. The leader's words. The mother's words. The words
of a woman who had learned, or was learning, that the most powerful thing she
could do for her daughter was not to manage or to direct or to engineer but to
_trust_—to trust Isabelle's judgment, to trust the relationship, to trust the
two fifteen-year-olds who had navigated a Swiss terrace and a year of silence
and a Japanese public high school and a VESPER fan event and a pair of running
spikes and a tarte au citron and a darkroom and a riverbank and who had, through
all of it, held onto each other.

"Helena," Shin Hee-yeon said. "I agree. We leave them alone. The partnership is
the partnership. The children are the children. The two things stay separate."

Helena nodded. "Agreed."

The two women looked at each other across the table—two CEOs, two mothers, two
architects of empires who had just agreed to stop designing the one thing that
mattered most. The agreement was not a contract. It was not binding. It was a
statement of intent, made over kaiseki-influenced Korean food on the
twenty-third floor of a Seoul hotel, witnessed by the two people it most
affected and sealed by nothing more than the word of two women who had built
their lives on the premise that their word was sufficient.

The dinner continued. The courses arrived and were consumed. The conversation
lightened—Helena asking about VESPER's tour preparations, Shin Hee-yeon asking
about the Waldstein Botanicals ceramide line, the two women discovering, to
their mutual surprise, that they enjoyed each other's company, that the
intersection of cosmetics and entertainment produced conversations that were
interesting rather than transactional, that the woman across the table was not a
counterpart but a colleague.

At 10:30 PM, the dinner ended. The four of them walked to the lobby. Cars were
called.

In the lobby, while the mothers spoke to the concierge, Isabelle turned to
Caspar.

"우리 엄마가 '그러면 됐어'라고 했어," she said. _My mom said "then that's
enough."_

"알아." _I know._

"그게 큰 거야. 엄마한테. 그건—" She stopped. She was organizing something,
structuring it, building the sentence the way she built a pastry: layer by
layer, each element placed with intention. "—그건 엄마가 나를 내 것으로 인정한
거야. 처음으로. 나를 프로젝트가 아니라, 사람으로." _That's big. For my mom. That
was—that was my mom acknowledging me as my own. For the first time. As a person,
not a project._

Caspar took her hand. In the lobby of the Shilla Hotel, with two of Asia's most
powerful women twenty feet away, with the Seoul night glittering through the
glass, with the weight of everything that had been said and agreed settling into
the structure of their lives.

"넌 항상 네 거였어," he said. _You were always your own._

She looked at him. The boy with the camera and the curling hair and the stoic
face that she had learned to read the way a seismologist read the earth—by the
tremors, by the micro-movements, by the things that were happening underneath.

"알아," she said. _I know._ "근데 가끔 다른 사람이 말해줘야 믿어지는 것들이
있잖아." _But sometimes there are things you need someone else to say before you
can believe them._

He squeezed her hand. She squeezed back.

The cars arrived. Helena's Genesis. Shin Hee-yeon's Hyundai. Two black vehicles,
debadged, idling at the hotel entrance.

"잘 자, 이자벨." _Good night, Isabelle._

"잘 자, 캐스퍼." _Good night, Caspar._

The ritual. The touchstone. The phrase that had been spoken every night since
the second day of school and that had traveled from Tokyo to Munich to Seoul
without missing a single evening, the thread that ran beneath the distance and
the time zones and the corporate dinners, the thread that held.

She got in her mother's car. He got in his mother's car. The two vehicles pulled
away from the curb—in opposite directions, toward different parts of the same
city, carrying the children of two empires who had been told, tonight, that they
were free.

## IV

The next morning. The dorm.

Isabelle woke at 6 AM. The room was warm—the dorm's air conditioning was not as
powerful as the Minami-Aoyama apartment's, and the Seoul heat had infiltrated
overnight, settling into the walls and the bedding with the patient persistence
of a climate that would not be denied.

She lay in bed and looked at the ceiling. The Seoul ceiling. Lower, closer,
textured.

Her phone was on the desk. She reached for it.

LINE. Caspar. Sent at 11:47 PM the previous night—after the dinner, after the
cars, after the drive through the Seoul night.

> **류태성:** 이자벨.
>
> _Isabelle._

> **류태성:** 오늘 밤 우리 엄마가 한 말 중에, 내가 계속 생각하는 게 있어.
>
> _There's something my mom said tonight that I keep thinking about._

> **류태성:** "그가 사랑에 빠진 건 우리가 가까이 배치해서가 아니라, 그녀가
> 어차피 찾을 수밖에 없는 사람이었기 때문이다."
>
> _"He fell in love not because we placed them in proximity, but because she is
> the person he was always going to find."_

> **류태성:** 나 그 말 처음 들었어. 엄마 입에서. "사랑에 빠졌다"는 말. 내
> 얘기로.
>
> _I heard those words for the first time. From my mom. "Fell in love." About
> me._

> **류태성:** 그리고 맞아. 엄마 말이 맞아.
>
> _And she's right. My mom is right._

> **류태성:** 사랑해, 이자벨.
>
> _I love you, Isabelle._

Isabelle read the message. She read it three times. She pressed the phone to her
chest—the same gesture, the same position, the phone against the sternum, the
screen's warmth through her sleep shirt.

_사랑해._

Three syllables in Korean. The word she had been circling since the first
day—since the gate, since the nod, since the shoe locker and the LINE thread and
the strawberries and the madeleines and the bench and the darkroom and the
photographs and the kiss and the riverbank and the fan event and the practice
room and every single moment in between. The word that had been present in every
action and every gesture and every held hand and every good-night and that had
never, until this message, been said.

She typed:

> **신지원:** 나도 사랑해, 캐스퍼.
>
> _I love you too, Caspar._

She pressed send.

She set the phone on the desk. She lay back. She stared at the ceiling.

The ceiling was the same. The room was the same. The city outside the window was
the same. But the girl in the bed was different—the girl in the bed had been
told, by a boy who measured his words the way he measured light, that he loved
her, and she had said it back, and the saying had not broken anything or changed
anything or rearranged the architecture of their lives. It had simply named the
thing that was already there—the way a photograph named the light that was
already falling, the way a recipe named the process that the hands already knew.

_사랑해._

The word sat in the Seoul morning like a stone in still water—present, settled,
sending out ripples that would reach every edge of their lives.

She closed her eyes. She smiled. The dimple appeared.

Through the wall, she could hear Yuna's alarm going off—a K-pop song, played at
maximum volume, the same alarm Yuna had used for the entire gap year and that
functioned less as a wake-up mechanism and more as an act of daily war against
the concept of silence.

The dorm was waking up. The city was waking up. The summer was continuing. Two
weeks remained—two weeks of Seoul, of mornings in the practice room, of
afternoons walking the city, of evenings with the members, of nights with the
LINE thread running beneath everything like an underground river.

And now the river had a name.

_사랑해._

She opened her eyes. She got out of bed. She walked to the window. She looked at
the Seoul skyline in the morning light—the towers, the haze, the jasmine on the
rooftop gardens, the city that had taught her to dance and to endure and to
carry the weight of being looked at.

She thought: _This is the city where I learned to move._

She thought: _And this is the city where he learned to say it._

She thought: _사랑해._

She went to brush her teeth.
