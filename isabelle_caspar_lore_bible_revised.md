# The Lore Bible — Isabelle & Caspar

---

## Core Premise

Two teenagers from intertwined European–Korean dynasties transfer into a
Japanese public high school already functioning as a long-term couple. Three
years earlier, their families attempted to separate them after they prematurely
collapsed a carefully staged merger timeline. The separation failed.

Now reunited in Tokyo, they navigate school life with the quiet certainty of
people who have already chosen each other—while, in the background, their
families shift from control to evaluation.

**Tone:** Comedy-forward cultural collision with sustained, low-visibility
pressure. **Core Question:** _Did they choose each other freely, or were they
shaped into inevitability—and does the distinction still matter?_

---

## The Families (Functional Overview)

Two dynastic systems—finance/real estate and entertainment/pharma—have long
planned a generational merger through their children.

They operate structurally:

- timelines
- optics
- control of outcomes

The children were intended to align gradually, then formally unite in adulthood.

That plan failed.

---

## The Breakpoint (Age 13)

At thirteen, Isabelle and Caspar discover the existence of the merger plan.

Instead of withdrawing, they escalate—deliberately choosing each other outside
the intended timeline.

This creates a structural crisis:

- The relationship becomes real too early
- Emotional and physical commitment removes flexibility
- The families lose control over sequencing and leverage

The issue is not morality. It is loss of control.

The response is immediate and severe: total separation.

---

## The Exile (13–16)

The exile is a forced desynchronization strategy.

**Objective:**

- break emotional continuity
- rebuild independence
- restore controllability

**Result:**

- failure

They maintain secret contact throughout, transforming separation into
reinforcement.

### Lasting Asymmetry

They do not return as mirrors:

- Isabelle becomes controlled, strategic, adaptive
- Caspar becomes direct, instinct-driven, resistant to systems

They are unified—but no longer identical in worldview.

---

## Tokyo: The Evaluation Phase

Tokyo is not reconciliation. It is repositioning.

The families shift from: control → observation enforcement → assessment

This period functions as a soft evaluation before reintegration into the family
system.

Unspoken questions:

- Can they operate within structure without destabilizing it?
- Is their relationship an asset or a liability?
- Can they meet expectations without being forced?

### Visible Signals

- Same school → controlled proximity
- Caretakers returned → normalization
- Internships → monitored exposure
- Academic expectations → baseline competence

They are not told they are being evaluated.

They both understand it anyway.

---

## Academic Context

Academic performance is part of the evaluation.

They must:

- reintegrate into structured education after disruption
- perform at a level consistent with their background
- demonstrate discipline and adaptability

Struggling is acceptable. Failing is not.

---

## The Relationship

They are stable, committed, and already chosen.

There is no uncertainty about whether they are together.

The tension lies in what they will do with that unity.

### Core Ideological Friction

- Isabelle: “We can use the system.”
- Caspar: “The system uses people.”

This difference shapes their decisions and future.

---

## Work

Their internships are voluntary engagement with the system that once tried to
control them.

- Isabelle treats it as leverage and learning
- Caspar treats it with skepticism

Both are being observed. Both know it.

---

## The Friends

The friends act as observers and emotional contrast.

Initial question: “What kind of war did these two survive?”

Conflict comes from asymmetry of trust, not wealth.

---

## The Comedy Engine

### Primary Source

Cultural mismatch between European directness and Japanese social structure.

### Core Joke

They behave like a long-married couple in an environment built for first
relationships.

---

## The Serious Undercurrent

### Internal Tension

“What are we becoming?”

### Fear Layer

- Becoming their parents
- Losing control of their own choices
- Mistaking survival-bonding for free choice

---

## Stakes

By the end of this period, a decision must be made:

- Accept the system and redefine it
- Or reject it entirely

No formal ultimatum is given.

---

## End Direction

They accept the system and redefine it on their own terms.

The difference is ownership of the decision.

---

## Tone & Style Rules

- Comedy: cultural friction only
- Drama: pressure, not misunderstanding
- Dialogue: efficient and distinct
- Intimacy: casual and constant
- Wealth: invisible until needed

---

Two people who already chose each other—now deciding what to do with the world
that chose them first.

## **Character Sheets**

### **Isabelle van Rijn / Shin Ji-won**

#### Isabelle - Surface

- 5'10", poised, dancer’s posture
- Controlled expression; difficult to read
- Perceived as elegant, distant, intimidating

#### Isabelle - Internal

- **Core belief:** Control creates safety
- **Fear:** Losing agency again
- **Blind spot:** Mistakes adaptation for free choice

#### Isabelle - Behavior

- **Under stress:** becomes colder, more precise
- **Relaxed:** quietly affectionate, physically close
- **Angry:** sharp, surgical, rarely raises voice

#### Isabelle - Contradiction

Warm and physically intimate in private, but highly strategic and controlled in
public

---

### **Caspar Waldstein / Ryu Tae-sung**

#### Caspar - Surface

- 6'1", broad-shouldered, physically imposing
- Rough hands, farm-built body
- Perceived as quiet, intense, slightly dangerous

#### Caspar - Internal

- **Core belief:** Systems exploit people
- **Fear:** Being controlled again
- **Blind spot:** Rejects structure even when it could help

#### Caspar - Behavior

- **Under stress:** withdraws or becomes blunt
- **Relaxed:** domestic, attentive, quietly soft
- **Angry:** direct, confrontational, short-spoken

#### Caspar - Contradiction

Looks intimidating but expresses care through simple, consistent actions

## **Behavioral Signatures**

### Isabelle

- Adjusts clothing (collars, sleeves) without asking
- Switches languages mid-sentence for precision
- Observes before speaking
- Maintains physical proximity without hesitation

### Caspar

- Carries bags/items automatically
- Cooks instead of verbalizing care
- Answers questions directly, even when socially inappropriate
- Positions himself slightly between Isabelle and others unconsciously

### As a Pair

- Share food/drinks without thinking
- Sit or stand in contact by default
- Resolve disagreements quickly, no lingering tension
- Speak across each other’s sentences naturally
