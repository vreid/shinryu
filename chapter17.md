# Chapter 17: Homecoming

---

## I.

The Incheon arrivals hall smelled like air conditioning and floor wax and the faint, ubiquitous sweetness of the duty-free perfume corridor that every passenger walked through whether they wanted to or not. Isabelle emerged from customs at 4:47 PM local time, pulling a single suitcase—matte black, hard shell, no brand markings, the kind of luggage that looked anonymous and cost four thousand euros—and carrying a canvas tote bag over one shoulder that contained her phone, her passport, her skincare pouch, and a sealed container of madeleines.

The madeleines were non-negotiable. She had baked them that morning in the Minami-Aoyama kitchen at 4 AM, two hours before Hayashi-san drove her to Haneda. Eighteen shells, packed in three layers of parchment, sealed against the pressurized cabin air that dried out everything, cushioned in the tote bag between a cashmere scarf and a copy of a Dutch novel she was rereading for the fourth time. The scarf was Loro Piana. The novel was Hella Haasse's *Oeroeg.* The madeleines were for Jiwoo, who had requested them and who would receive them and who would, Isabelle knew, eat the first one without comment and then close her eyes and eat the second one with the slow, reverent attention of someone tasting something they had missed.

She walked through the sliding doors into the arrivals lobby.

Seoul hit her.

Not physically—the temperature differential between the air-conditioned terminal and the lobbied waiting area was minimal. But Seoul hit her the way places hit you when you had history with them: in the stomach, in the chest, in the specific neural pathways that stored sense memory and deployed it without permission. The sound of Korean—not the private, encoded Korean she spoke with Caspar, but the ambient, environmental Korean of a country where every conversation, every announcement, every sign and screen and speaker was in a language that had been her operating system for a year. The particular quality of Korean fluorescent lighting, which was subtly different from Japanese fluorescent lighting in ways she could feel but not articulate. The smell—industrial cleaning solution plus the warm, yeasty draft from the bakery concession near Gate 3 plus the specific olfactory signature of Seoul itself, which was denser and sharper than Tokyo's neutral urban scent, a city that wore its flavors closer to the surface.

She had lived here. She had trained here. She had cried in the bathroom of a convenience store three blocks from the agency at 11 PM on a Tuesday because a choreographer had told her that her extensions were "lazy" and the word had, for reasons she couldn't explain, broken something that twelve hours of practice had not. She had sat on the banks of the Han River at 5 AM watching the sun rise over Yeouido and feeling, for the first time in the gap year, something that was not exhaustion or homesickness but a fragile, provisional peace—the peace of a body that had been pushed past its limits and had discovered, on the other side, that the limits were negotiable.

She was back.

"지원아!"

The voice cut through the lobby noise with the surgical precision of someone who had spent three years training their vocal projection in studios designed to amplify sound. Isabelle turned.

Seo Yuna was standing at the barrier.

She was in disguise—or what passed for disguise when you were one of the four most recognizable faces in Korean entertainment. Black cap, oversized sunglasses, a gray hoodie that was approximately three sizes too large and that bore, on closer inspection, the faded logo of a Busan convenience store chain that Yuna had adopted as a personal fashion statement. Her mask was pulled below her chin—technically a violation of the agency's public appearance protocol, but Yuna's relationship with protocol had always been one of creative interpretation.

Beside her, smaller, quieter, standing with the particular stillness of someone who was waiting and who was good at it: Lee Min-ji. The leader. Wearing all black—black jeans, black T-shirt, black sneakers, a black face mask pulled up to the bridge of her nose. Her eyes, visible above the mask, were the eyes that Isabelle had seen evaluate hundreds of performances and dozens of trainees and that now, landing on Isabelle, softened with an expression that was not performance and was not evaluation and was simply, entirely, recognition.

And behind them both, leaning against a concrete pillar with her phone in her hands and her earbuds in and the quiet, contained intensity of someone who was always listening to something: Kang Jiwoo.

Isabelle stopped.

The three of them looked at her. She looked at them. The arrivals lobby continued its churning—travelers, carts, the automated announcements in Korean and English and Chinese cycling through the speakers. None of it registered. The world had narrowed to three women at a barrier and the space between them, which was fifteen meters and shrinking because Isabelle was walking, and then walking faster, and then Yuna was moving too, ducking under the barrier with the casual disregard for infrastructure that characterized everything she did, and then—

Yuna hit her at approximately seven kilometers per hour.

The hug was not gentle. It was full-contact—arms around Isabelle's neck, body pressed against hers, the impact sufficient to make Isabelle stagger back a step before her dancer's balance caught and held. Yuna was smaller than her—five-six to Isabelle's five-ten—and the height difference meant that Yuna's face was pressed against Isabelle's collarbone, her cap knocked askew, her sunglasses pushed up onto her forehead, and she was saying something into Isabelle's shirt that was muffled and broken and that sounded like "보고 싶었어 진짜 너무 보고 싶었어 야 왜 이렇게 커졌어" (*I missed you so much I really missed you so much hey why did you get taller*).

Isabelle's arms came up. They wrapped around Yuna—around the gray hoodie and the small body inside it and the enormous, incandescent personality that had been, for ten months, the loudest and warmest presence in her daily life. She held on. She did not cry—not yet, not in the arrivals lobby, not with the automated announcement reading the next flight arrival in its flat, nasal Korean—but the pressure was there, behind her eyes, in her throat, in the specific physiological response of a body that was receiving something it had been missing and that the receiving was almost too much.

"언니," Isabelle said. Her voice was thick. "숨 좀 쉬게 해줘." *Let me breathe.*

"안 돼. 이건 최소 30초야." *No. This is a minimum 30-second hug.*

"사람들 보고 있어." *People are watching.*

"보면 보는 거지." *If they see, they see.*

Isabelle laughed into Yuna's hair. *보면 보는 거지.* The same phrase—the exact same phrase she and Caspar had used, the same philosophy of visibility, the same refusal to hide the fact of caring. Yuna didn't know about the phrase. She didn't need to. Some truths were universal.

The thirty seconds passed. Yuna released—reluctantly, her arms unpeeling from Isabelle's neck in stages, like a vine being detached from a wall.

Minji was next. The leader's hug was different—brief, firm, the contact of a person who expressed affection through efficiency rather than duration. She held Isabelle for three seconds, her hands flat on Isabelle's shoulder blades, her head turned so her cheek rested against Isabelle's jaw.

"잘 왔어," Minji said. *Welcome back.*

Two words. No elaboration. But the two words carried the weight of a year's absence and a leader's relief and the particular satisfaction of a woman who had watched over four people for three years and who counted the Dutch-Korean girl who had joined them for ten months as a fifth, even now, even after the leaving.

Then Jiwoo.

Jiwoo had removed her earbuds. She was standing where she had been—by the pillar, slightly apart, her phone now in her pocket, her hands at her sides. She was looking at Isabelle with the expression Isabelle had seen in the elevator at Shin Entertainment: the quiet eyes, the calm face, the total attention of someone who communicated primarily through presence.

Isabelle walked to her.

They stood face to face. Jiwoo was shorter—five-four, the smallest member of VESPER, the one whose stage presence expanded to fill arenas because the energy inside her was compressed so tightly that when it was released it occupied more space than her body could account for. In person, off-stage, she was simply small and quiet and very, very still.

"언니," Jiwoo said.

"지우야."

Jiwoo leaned forward. She rested her forehead against Isabelle's collarbone—not her shoulder, not her chest, but the collarbone, the specific point of contact she had established during the Seoul year, the spot where her head fit against Isabelle's frame with the geometric precision of two puzzle pieces interlocking.

Isabelle raised her hand. She placed it on the back of Jiwoo's head—the dark hair, the small skull, the warmth of a person who trusted her enough to close her eyes in a public space.

They stood like that for five seconds. No words. The particular communication of two people who had danced side by side until their bodies shared a language, and whose bodies were now, after four months apart, performing the only greeting that mattered: *I'm here. You're here. The language still works.*

"마들렌 가져왔어요?" Jiwoo asked, her voice muffled against Isabelle's shirt. *Did you bring madeleines?*

"열여덟 개." *Eighteen.*

Jiwoo lifted her head. She smiled—the small, specific Jiwoo smile that began at the corners of her mouth and traveled to her eyes without ever reaching full width, the smile of someone who rationed her smiles because each one cost her something and she wanted them to mean what they meant.

"가자," Jiwoo said. *Let's go.*

## II.

The van was waiting in the underground parking structure—a black Hyundai Staria, the agency's standard transport vehicle, its windows tinted to the legal limit and then, Isabelle suspected, slightly beyond. The driver was a man Isabelle recognized—Park Junho, the same driver who had transported her between the trainee dorm and the agency for ten months, the man who knew her sleep schedule, her convenience-store preferences, and the precise temperature at which she liked the van's air conditioning (twenty-two degrees, not twenty-one, because twenty-one made her shoulders tense and tension migrated to her dance form, a discovery made during month three and never forgotten).

"신 양, 오래간만입니다." *Miss Shin, it's been a while.*

"오래간만이에요, 준호 씨." *It's been a while, Junho-ssi.*

She climbed in. Yuna was already in the back row, her legs stretched across the seats, her cap off, her hair—dark, center-parted, longer than Isabelle remembered—falling around her face as she scrolled her phone with the aggressive multitasking that was her resting state. Minji sat in the middle row, her posture straight, her mask lowered, her face in its natural resting expression: composed, watchful, the face of someone who was always partly managing a situation even when the situation was a van ride.

Jiwoo sat beside Isabelle. She did not lean against her—not in the van, not with the seatbelt on—but she sat close enough that their shoulders touched when the van took corners, the small, repeated contacts a Morse code of proximity.

"어디로?" Junho asked. *Where to?*

"연습실," Minji said. *The practice room.*

Isabelle looked at Minji. "연습실?" *The practice room?*

"하은이가 거기서 기다리고 있어. 저녁까지 연습이거든. 그리고—" Minji paused. The pause was deliberate—Minji's pauses were always deliberate, deployed for emphasis the way other people deployed volume. "—보여주고 싶은 거 있어." *Haeun is waiting there. She has practice until the evening. And—there's something we want to show you.*

"뭔데?" *What?*

"가면 알아." *You'll see when you get there.*

The van moved through Seoul. The city unfolded outside the tinted windows—the broad avenues of Yeongjong, the bridge to the mainland, the highway curving through the southern approach, the skyline assembling itself on the horizon: Lotte Tower, the N Seoul Tower on Namsan, the dense, vertical stacking of a city that had grown upward because it had nowhere else to go.

Isabelle watched. She watched the way Caspar would have watched—with the specific, selective attention of someone who was looking not at what was there but at what had changed. The new construction on the Gangnam side. The repainted facade of the convenience store near the Cheongdam intersection. The billboard on the side of a building that showed—

She leaned forward.

The billboard showed four women. Full-height, fifteen meters tall, their faces lit by integrated LED panels that made the image glow even in the afternoon daylight. They wore the dark-academia concept outfits—blazers, skirts, glasses. The text read: **VESPER — "Somebody Knows" — ASIA TOUR 2026.** Below the text, the tour dates, the venue names, the sponsor logos. And at the bottom right, in a font smaller than the rest but visible from the highway if you knew where to look: **Presented by Shin Entertainment. In partnership with Waldstein Botanicals.**

Yuna followed her gaze. "크지?" *Big, right?*

"크다는 건 부족한 말이야, 언니." *"Big" is an understatement, unnie.*

"빌보드 네 개야. 강남에 두 개, 여의도에 하나, 명동에 하나. 그리고 버스 광고 오십 대. 그리고 지하철 스크린 광고 서울 전역." *Four billboards. Two in Gangnam, one in Yeouido, one in Myeongdong. Plus fifty bus ads. Plus subway screen ads across all of Seoul.*

"그리고 도쿄 돔," Isabelle said.

Yuna looked at her. The lazy-cat smile—the one that Sora had analyzed in approximately two hundred screenshots, the one that managed to be simultaneously inviting and unapproachable—appeared on Yuna's face in its unperformed version: just a girl smiling at her sister.

"도쿄 돔." *Tokyo Dome.* "9월. 5만 5천 석." *September. Fifty-five thousand seats.*

"떨려?" *Are you nervous?*

"아니. 그게 문제야. 안 떨려. 설레. 다른 거잖아. 무서우면 몸이 굳는데, 설레면 몸이 열려. 지금 몸이 열려 있어." *No. That's the problem. I'm not nervous. I'm excited. It's different. When you're scared, your body closes. When you're excited, your body opens. Right now, my body is open.*

Isabelle looked at Yuna—at the woman who would stand on the Tokyo Dome stage in two months and sing to fifty-five thousand people with her body open—and felt the compound emotion that she had come to associate with these four women: pride and tenderness and a fierce, protective awe at the scale of what they were becoming.

"도쿄 돔에서 울 거야, 언니." *You're going to make me cry at Tokyo Dome, unnie.*

"당연하지. 그게 목표거든." *Obviously. That's the goal.*

## III.

The practice room.

Shin Entertainment occupied the sixth through tenth floors of a glass tower in Cheongdam-dong—a building whose lobby alone communicated, through its materials and proportions and the particular quality of its silence, that the organization inside it operated at a scale and a standard that most businesses did not attempt. The lobby was white marble and steel, staffed by a reception team in coordinated black, decorated with a single large-format photograph on the back wall: the four members of VESPER, shot in profile, their faces turned toward a light source outside the frame, the image rendered in high-contrast black and white that made them look like they belonged to a different era, an era of icons and monuments.

Isabelle walked through this lobby the way she walked through the Aoyama streetscape—without assessment, without adjustment, the way a person walked through their own home. She had walked through this lobby a thousand times. She had walked through it at 5 AM for morning practice and at midnight after evening rehearsals and at 3 AM during the stretch of weeks before the debut when the schedule had collapsed into a continuous, undifferentiated block of work that acknowledged neither daylight nor darkness.

The receptionist recognized her. "신 지원 씨, 어서 오세요." *Shin Ji-won-ssi, welcome.*

"감사합니다." *Thank you.*

The elevator. The seventh floor. The hallway—long, fluorescent-lit, the walls lined with framed gold and platinum records and concert photographs and the accumulated evidence of an entertainment company that had been building artists for twenty years. Isabelle walked past them without looking. She knew every frame. She had dusted some of them during her first week, when she was still a trainee-adjacent and her duties had included whatever the junior staff assigned her.

The practice room was at the end of the hallway. Room 7A—the main rehearsal space, the room where VESPER had been built. The door was closed. Through the small window, Isabelle could see fluorescent light and the reflected gleam of the mirror wall.

Minji opened the door.

The room was exactly as Isabelle remembered it. Twenty meters by fifteen. Mirrored on the south wall—floor to ceiling, edge to edge, the glass so clean it looked like a portal into a parallel room where a parallel group of dancers moved in perfect, reversed synchronization. The floor was sprung—rubber-matted over wood, the surface designed to absorb impact and return energy, to protect the knees and ankles and hips of bodies that hit it thousands of times per day. The speakers were mounted in the corners—four black boxes, industrial-grade, capable of producing the kind of bass that you felt in your teeth. The air smelled like rubber and cleaning solution and the faint, persistent sweetness of the floral air freshener that the cleaning staff used every evening.

Haeun was in the room.

She was stretching—on the floor, one leg extended, her torso folded over it, her forehead touching her knee with the casual impossibility of someone whose flexibility was not a skill but a biological condition. She was wearing practice clothes—black leggings, a crop top, her hair in a high ponytail that swung when she moved. She looked up when the door opened.

"지원아!" *Ji-won-ah!*

She was on her feet and across the room in approximately two seconds. The hug was warm—Haeun's hugs were always warm, Haeun's entire existence was warm, she was the member who cried during sad movies and then denied crying and then cried again—and Isabelle received it with the particular gratitude of someone returning to a hug they had missed.

"잘 지냈어?" Haeun asked, pulling back, her hands on Isabelle's shoulders, her eyes scanning Isabelle's face with the attentive concern of the member who had once studied for a nursing assistant certification. *Have you been well?*

"응, 언니. 잘 지냈어." *Yeah, unnie. I've been well.*

"피부 좋아졌다." *Your skin looks better.*

"발트슈타인 제품이요." *Waldstein products.*

"역시." *Of course.*

The five of them stood in the practice room—Yuna, Minji, Haeun, Jiwoo, and Isabelle. The configuration was familiar: Minji slightly apart, overseeing; Yuna in motion, circling; Haeun close, touching; Jiwoo quiet, present. And Isabelle in the center—taller than all of them, her honey hair a bright note in the room's monochrome palette, her posture the dancer's posture that this room had helped refine.

"보여주고 싶은 거 있다고 했잖아," Isabelle said. *You said there was something you wanted to show me.*

Minji nodded. She walked to the speaker system. She connected her phone. She selected a track.

"투어 세트리스트 리허설 중이야," Minji said. *We're rehearsing the tour setlist.* "새로운 안무가 있어. 도쿄 돔 용으로. 아직 발표 안 한 거야." *There's new choreography. For Tokyo Dome. It hasn't been announced yet.*

"신곡이야?" *A new song?*

"신곡은 아니야. 'Glass Hour' 리믹스. 도쿄 돔 스페셜 버전. 안무를 완전히 새로 짰어." *Not a new song. A "Glass Hour" remix. Tokyo Dome special version. Completely new choreography.*

"누가 짰어?" *Who choreographed it?*

"지수 선생님." *Jisoo-seonsaengnim.*

Park Jisoo. The choreographer. The woman who had used Isabelle as a test body. The woman who had built VESPER's physical language from the ground up—every gesture, every transition, every formation change—and who had incorporated, without attribution and without apology, elements of Isabelle's classical training into the group's movement vocabulary.

"보여줘," Isabelle said. *Show me.*

Minji pressed play.

The "Glass Hour" remix began. The original track—the song that had launched VESPER, the song that Caspar had discovered in a hotel corridor, the song whose chorus choreography he could perform from memory—was restructured: the tempo dropped, the production stripped to its bones, the vocals isolated and layered over a sparse, atmospheric beat that was more architecture than song. The effect was dramatic—the familiar melody rendered unfamiliar, the energy shifted from performance to presence, the music becoming the kind of thing that filled a large space not with volume but with weight.

The four members took their positions. Not the original positions—these were new, wider, the formation spread to the dimensions of a stage that was designed for fifty-five thousand people. They stood still for four counts. Then they moved.

The choreography was—

Isabelle sat down on the floor. She sat because her knees had gone soft and because the movement she was watching required her full attention and her full attention was not compatible with the act of standing.

The choreography was different from anything VESPER had done. It was slower, more expansive, the movements occupying more space and more time, each gesture held longer, the transitions flowing rather than snapping. It was choreography designed for a dome—designed for the back row, for the nosebleed seats, for the fan who was two hundred meters from the stage and who needed to see the movement not as detail but as shape, as outline, as the human body expressing something so clearly that it was readable at stadium scale.

And threaded through it—visible to Isabelle the way a watermark was visible in paper when held to the light—was ballet. Not obvious ballet. Not port de bras or arabesques or anything that would be identified by a casual viewer as classical technique. But the foundations: the line, the extension, the way the spine was held, the way the arms moved through space with a continuity that was the opposite of K-pop's staccato snap-and-lock. The movement breathed. It flowed. It looked like—

It looked like Isabelle.

Not her face. Not her body. But her *language*—the physical vocabulary she had brought to the practice room from Amsterdam, from the ballet studio where she had spent five years learning to move with the particular grace that came from treating the body as an instrument of continuous line, and that Park Jisoo had absorbed and translated and woven into the group's DNA so thoroughly that it was now inseparable from who they were.

The sequence lasted three minutes. They ran it once—full out, performance quality, the four bodies moving with the synchronized precision and the individual expression that was VESPER's signature. Yuna's lazy power. Minji's sharp clarity. Haeun's explosive warmth. And Jiwoo—Jiwoo, who moved through the choreography with the focused intensity of a person who was channeling something that went deeper than rehearsal—performing the final sequence with the extension, *the* extension, the reach that Isabelle had taught her and that now, in this new choreography, was not a single gesture but a motif, repeated and developed and varied across the entire piece, the way a composer developed a theme.

They finished. They held the final position—a tableau, four bodies in a formation that was open and grounded and still, the mirror behind them reflecting the image back, doubling it, the room containing eight dancers instead of four.

The music faded. The speakers went quiet. The practice room held its breath.

Isabelle was sitting on the floor. Her hands were in her lap. Her eyes were wet.

"지수 선생님이 뭐라고 했어?" she said. Her voice was steady—the training held, the composure held, even now. *What did Jisoo-seonsaengnim say?*

Minji looked at her. The leader's eyes were dark, direct, carrying the particular intensity of someone who was about to say something that had been held in reserve for exactly this moment.

"지수 선생님이 이 안무의 움직임 언어 원형이 너라고 했어. 'Glass Hour'를 처음 만들 때, 지원이 몸에서 출발한 움직임이 많았다고. 그래서 이번 돔 버전도 같은 뿌리에서 시작했다고." *Jisoo-seonsaengnim said the movement language in this choreography originates from you. When she first created "Glass Hour," many of the movements started in your body. She said this dome version grows from the same root.*

"그리고 크레딧에—" Minji stopped. She looked at Yuna. Yuna nodded. Minji looked back at Isabelle.

"도쿄 돔 프로그램 노트에, 안무 어시스트 크레딧이 들어가. 네 이름으로. '안무 보조: 신지원.' 지수 선생님이 직접 요청했어." *In the Tokyo Dome program notes, there will be a choreography assist credit. Under your name. "Choreography Assistant: Shin Ji-won." Jisoo-seonsaengnim requested it personally.*

The practice room was silent. Four women standing. One woman sitting. The mirrors reflecting all five. The speakers quiet. The rubber floor absorbing the weight of every body in the room.

Isabelle looked at the floor. She looked at her hands. She looked at the reflection in the mirror—five figures, one sitting, four standing, the composition of a moment that would not repeat.

"나 이름이 올라가?" she said. Her voice cracked. The composure, the filter, the architecture—all of it cracked, on the single syllable of *올라가*, the Korean word for "goes up," the word that meant her name would appear in a program that fifty-five thousand people would read.

"네 이름이 올라가," Minji said. *Your name goes up.*

Isabelle pressed her hands to her face. She breathed. In through the nose, out through the mouth—the breath Jisoo had taught her, the breath before the stage, the breath that held everything together when everything wanted to come apart.

Yuna sat down beside her on the floor. She didn't hug her—she just sat, hip to hip, shoulder to shoulder, the contact saying: *I'm here.*

Jiwoo sat on her other side. Forehead to collarbone. The familiar geometry. *I'm here too.*

Haeun knelt in front of her. Hands on Isabelle's knees. The warm eyes, slightly red. *We're all here.*

Minji stood. She stood because someone had to stand—because a leader's job was to hold the structure when the structure was full of emotion, to be the wall that the others leaned against, to watch over the family while the family felt what it felt.

Isabelle lowered her hands. Her face was wet. Her eyes were bright. Her expression was the one from Frame 6—the Daikanyama photograph, the one Caspar had captured on Portra 400, the face of someone who was about to be happy.

"고마워요," she said. To all of them. To the room. To the mirrors and the speakers and the rubber floor that had absorbed the impact of her training and returned to her, now, the evidence that it had mattered. *Thank you.*

"고맙긴," Yuna said, bumping her shoulder. "우리 안무잖아. 네가 만든 거라고." *Don't thank us. It's our choreography. But you made it.*

"같이 만든 거야." *We made it together.*

"같이," Yuna said. *Together.*

The word sat in the practice room—the same room where Isabelle had learned the difference between performing and meaning it, the same floor she had collapsed on at 2 AM, the same mirrors that had shown her every imperfection and every improvement. The word *같이*—together—in the place where together had been the only way to survive.

Isabelle wiped her eyes. She reached into her tote bag. She pulled out the container of madeleines.

"배고프지?" she said. *Aren't you hungry?*

Yuna opened the container before the sentence was finished. The smell of browned butter filled the practice room—warm and complex and entirely incongruous with the rubber and the fluorescent light, the smell of a Parisian patisserie deployed in a Korean rehearsal space by a Dutch-Korean girl who carried the cooking of two continents in her hands.

"야 이거 진짜—" Yuna ate a madeleine. Her eyes closed. "—진짜 이거 때문에 우는 건 좀 그런데, 울 것 같아." *Okay this is really— I know it's weird to cry over a madeleine, but I might cry.*

"울지 마, 언니. 아까 이미 다 울었잖아." *Don't cry, unnie. We already cried enough earlier.*

"울고 싶을 때 우는 거야! 마들렌이 울만큼 맛있으면 마들렌 때문에 우는 거야!" *I cry when I want to cry! If the madeleine is good enough to cry over, I cry over the madeleine!*

Minji sighed. The sigh was the sigh of a leader who had been sighing at Yuna for three years and whose sighs had become, through repetition, a form of affection. She took a madeleine. She ate it. Her expression did not change—Minji's expressions never changed during evaluations—but her hand reached for a second, which was, from Minji, the equivalent of a standing ovation.

Jiwoo ate hers slowly. Eyes closed. The second bite held for five seconds. When she opened her eyes, she looked at Isabelle and said:

"이 맛이에요." *This is the taste.*

"무슨 맛?" *What taste?*

"집 맛이요." *The taste of home.*

Isabelle looked at Jiwoo. The youngest, the quietest, the one who distilled entire emotional landscapes into single sentences. The taste of home. Not a place, not a building, not a country. A madeleine. A flavor. The taste of browned butter and vanilla and the hands that made them.

Isabelle pulled Jiwoo close. One arm around her shoulders. Jiwoo leaned in—the lean, always the lean—and they sat on the practice room floor eating madeleines, four women and one girl, five bodies in a room that smelled like rubber and butter, the mirrors reflecting all of them, the city humming outside, the tour approaching, the Tokyo Dome waiting, and the name *Shin Ji-won* waiting to be printed in a program that fifty-five thousand people would hold.

## IV.

That night. The trainee dorm.

The dorm was a four-bedroom apartment on the fourteenth floor of a residential building in Gangnam—modest by the standards of the agency's revenue, luxurious by the standards of the trainees who had lived there before VESPER's debut elevated the members to individual housing. The individual housing had been arranged months ago—each member now had her own studio apartment within a few blocks of the agency—but the dorm remained, maintained by Shin Entertainment as a communal space, a home base, the place the members returned to when they needed to be together instead of alone.

Isabelle was given the guest room—the small room at the end of the hallway that had been hers during the gap year, furnished with a single bed, a desk, a closet, and a window that looked out onto the Gangnam skyline. The room was clean—someone had changed the sheets and placed a towel on the bed and put a small vase of flowers on the desk, white chrysanthemums, the kind of gesture that suggested Minji's hand.

She sat on the bed. She unpacked—the Loro Piana scarf, the Dutch novel, the skincare pouch, the empty madeleine container. She placed her phone on the desk.

She opened LINE.

> **신지원:** 서울이야.
>
> *I'm in Seoul.*

Munich was seven hours behind. It was 10 PM in Seoul. It was 3 PM in Munich. Caspar would be awake—probably at the house in Bogenhausen, probably with his mother, probably in the study where Helena kept her collection of photography books, the room where Caspar had first seen a Leica and understood what a camera could be.

The reply came in forty seconds.

> **류태성:** 어때?
>

She looked at the message. Two words. The question that could be answered in a hundred ways—how is Seoul, how is the dorm, how is the weather, how is the food, how are the members, how is the practice room, how is the rubber floor and the mirror wall and the girl sitting on a bed in a room she used to sleep in.

She typed:

> **신지원:** 연습실에 갔어. 멤버들이랑.
>
> *I went to the practice room. With the members.*

> **류태성:** 어땠어?
>

> **신지원:** 도쿄 돔 안무를 보여줬어. "Glass Hour" 리믹스. 새로 짠 거.

> **류태성:** 어땠어?

> *How was it?*

She stared at the screen. She thought about the choreography—the slow, expansive movement, the ballet foundations, the extension woven through the piece like a thread, the way the four bodies moved through space with a grace that was not K-pop's snapping precision but something more fluid, more continuous, something that looked like—

> **신지원:** 내가 보였어.

>

> **신지원:** 응.
>

Twenty seconds. Then a message that was longer than any Caspar had ever sent—longer than the terrace confession, longer than the darkroom revelation, longer than the *"I would have found you anyway"* that had rearranged everything:

> **류태성:** 이자벨. 나는 사진을 찍어. 빛을 보고, 프레임을 잡고, 셔터를 누르고, 암실에서 인화해. 그게 내가 세상에 남기는 거야. 프레임. 순간. 근데 너는—너는 움직임을 남겨. 네 몸이 만든 언어가 다른 사람 몸에서 살아. 네가 가르친 팔의 연장이 지우의 춤에 있어. 네가 보여준 전환이 민지의 안무에 있어. 네가 없어도 네가 있어. 그게—그건 사진보다 큰 거야. 그건 영원한 거야. 그리고 5만 5천 명이 도쿄 돔에서 그걸 볼 거야. 네 이름이 프로그램에 올라가 있는 채로.

The language your body made lives in other people's bodies. The arm extension you taught is in Jiwoo's dance. The transition you showed is in Minji's choreography. Even when you're not there, you're there. That's—that's bigger than photography. That's permanent. And fifty-five thousand people will see it at Tokyo Dome. With your name in the program.*

Isabelle read the message. She read it twice. She read it a third time, slowly, each word landing individually, the way the words landed in his voice—unhurried, precise, each syllable placed like a stone in a wall he was building.

She pressed the phone to her chest. She closed her eyes. She breathed—the Jisoo breath, the stage breath, the breath that held everything together.

When she opened her eyes, she typed:

> **신지원:** 캐스퍼.

> **류태성:** 잘 자이자 좋은 아침, 여자친구.

> **신지원:** 잘 자이자 좋은 아침, 남자친구.
>
> *Good night and good morning, boyfriend.*

She locked the phone. She set it on the desk beside the white chrysanthemums. She turned off the light. She lay on the single bed in the room at the end of the hallway in a dorm in Gangnam and she stared at the ceiling—a different ceiling, a Seoul ceiling, lower and closer than the Tokyo ceiling, textured with the small imperfections of an older building that had housed dozens of trainees over the years, each one leaving invisible marks of their passing.

Through the wall, she could hear Yuna's voice—muffled, laughing, probably on the phone with someone, probably being told to go to sleep by Minji, probably ignoring the instruction the way she ignored all instructions that conflicted with her preference for conversation over rest.

Through the window, she could see the Gangnam skyline—the towers lit against the dark sky, the neon signs, the moving lights of traffic on the Teheran-ro. Seoul at night, electric and alive, the city that had taught her to dance and to endure and to carry the weight of being looked at by people who expected perfection.

Five days.

Five days until Caspar arrived. Five days until she could take him to this building, through this lobby, down this hallway, into this room—this practice room with its rubber floor and its mirror wall—and show him the place where the language began. Where her body had spoken and Jisoo had listened and the listening had become movement and the movement had become a group and the group had become VESPER and VESPER had become the thing that saved him in a hotel corridor in Bavaria while she was learning to be saved by the same four women two thousand kilometers to the east.

Five days.

She could handle five days.

She closed her eyes. She slept. And for the first time in four months, the ceiling above her was a Seoul ceiling, and the sounds around her were Seoul sounds, and the girl in the bed was the girl who had left and who had come back and who was, finally, exactly where all the pieces of herself converged.
