# Chapter 4: The Weight of Small Things

---

## I.

Wednesday.

The madeleines arrived at lunch in a small container lined with parchment paper,
sealed with a rubber band that Isabelle had taken from Auntie Choi's kitchen
drawer—the same kind of rubber band Auntie Choi used to triple-secure sauce
containers, because Auntie Choi did not trust lids and apparently neither did
Isabelle.

Caspar opened the container on the bench behind the gymnasium. The four
madeleines sat in a neat row, golden and shell-shaped, each one bearing the
distinct hump on its back that Isabelle had once explained to him, in a Swiss
dormitory at midnight, was called the _bosse_ and was "the difference between a
madeleine and a cake that's lying about its identity."

He picked one up. It was heavier than it looked—dense in the way that good
pastry was dense, the weight of real butter and properly developed batter
compressed into a small, precise form. The surface was matte gold, lightly
caramelized, with a faint sheen where the butter had risen to the crust during
baking.

He bit into it.

The shell cracked gently at the edge, giving way to an interior that was soft,
yielding, almost custard-like in its moisture. The flavor arrived in layers:
first the browned butter, deep and nutty, then the vanilla, then a delicate
sweetness that sat at the back of the palate without cloying. The texture
dissolved—not crumbled, _dissolved_—against his tongue, leaving behind the warm,
roasted fragrance of beurre noisette and the faint mineral trace of good French
flour.

He stopped chewing.

He looked at the madeleine. He looked at Isabelle, who was sitting forty
centimeters to his left, eating her own bento with studied casualness, not
watching him, very obviously not watching him, with the disciplined
non-attention of someone who was tracking his every micro-expression through
peripheral vision.

"This is—" He paused. He did not typically struggle with vocabulary. He spoke
four languages with functional fluency and a fifth with academic competence. But
the madeleine had done something to his capacity for adjectives. "—very good."

"I know," Isabelle said.

"No, I mean—" He looked at the madeleine again. The cross-section where he'd
bitten was a gradient of gold to pale yellow, the crumb tight and even, no air
pockets, no dry spots. This was not amateur baking. This was not hobby baking.
This was the output of someone who had been trained by professionals and had
continued to practice with the obsessive precision of an athlete maintaining
form. "This is _very_ good."

"I know," she said again, and this time there was something in her voice—a
warmth, a quiet pride, the satisfaction of someone whose work had been seen by
the one person whose seeing mattered.

He ate the second madeleine in three bites. He did not speak while eating it.
There was nothing to say. The pastry said everything.

Isabelle ate one of her own. They sat in silence—the good silence, the silence
that had been building over three days of lunches, the silence that no longer
needed to be filled because it was already full.

"I want to bring some for the others," she said, after a while. "For Yuki and
Sora. Maybe Hina."

"You should."

"They'll ask where I learned."

"What will you tell them?"

She considered this. A cherry blossom petal detached from a branch somewhere
above them and landed on the lid of Caspar's bento. He didn't brush it away.

"That I took a course in France. Which is true."

"And if they ask which course?"

"I'll be vague. I'm good at vague."

"You're excellent at vague."

She looked at him. The corner of her mouth lifted—not quite a smile, but the
closest neighbor to one, the expression that preceded an Isabelle smile the way
a tremor preceded an earthquake. "Was that a compliment or an accusation?"

"Both."

The lift became a smile. Small, controlled, lasting approximately two seconds
before she returned her attention to her bento. But Caspar saw it—the full
duration, the exact shape of it, the way it changed the geometry of her face,
lifting her cheekbones and narrowing her eyes and creating a single, shallow
dimple on her left side that he had not seen in over a year and that hit him
with the force of a physical event.

He looked away. He looked at the gymnasium wall. He studied a crack in the
concrete with the intensity of a geologist examining a fault line.

"Caspar."

"Mm."

"You're staring at a wall."

"It's an interesting wall."

"It's concrete."

"Concrete can be interesting."

"당황한 거야?" _Are you flustered?_

He turned back to her. Her hazel-green eyes were steady, amused, and
devastatingly direct. She knew exactly what she'd done. She knew exactly what
that smile had cost him. She was sitting there, in her school uniform, on a
bench behind a gymnasium, asking him in Korean if he was flustered, and the
answer was _yes, catastrophically, in a way that involves my entire circulatory
system,_ and they both knew it, and she was enjoying it.

"아니," he said. _No._

"거짓말쟁이." _Liar._

"너도." _You too._

They looked at each other. The air between them was doing something—thickening,
charging, developing the particular atmospheric density that preceded either a
thunderstorm or a conversation that would change everything. Caspar could feel
it against his skin, in his chest, in the slight elevation of his pulse that he
clocked and cataloged and filed under _things I will deal with later,
potentially never._

The bell rang.

The atmospheric density broke. They packed their bentos. They stood. They walked
back toward the building together—side by side, close enough that their arms
might have touched if either of them had been slightly less disciplined about
spatial boundaries.

At the door, they separated. Caspar went left, toward the 1-2 stairwell.
Isabelle went right, toward 1-3.

Neither of them looked back.

Both of them wanted to.

## II.

Thursday. Kenji got his lunch.

"HCCL! Hallway Cleaning Crew Lunch! First official meeting!" Kenji stood at the
center of a desk cluster he had assembled with the logistical determination of a
field commander establishing a forward operating base. Four desks had been
pushed together in the center of the 1-2 classroom, creating a square island
around which Kenji, Ren, Daiki, and Caspar sat with their bentos.

"We need to establish traditions," Kenji said, unwrapping his lunch—a large,
slightly battered bento box that bore the signs of having been packed by someone
who loved him but had limited time. Rice with a single umeboshi in the center.
Fried chicken. Tamagoyaki. Broccoli, seemingly added as an afterthought,
occupying a small corner with the defeated posture of a vegetable that knew it
would not be eaten. "Every great group needs traditions. The baseball team at my
middle school had a thing where they all clapped three times before a game. We
need something like that."

"We could eat in silence," Ren said.

"That's the opposite of a tradition. That's the absence of tradition. That's a
tradition vacuum."

"I'd vote for the tradition vacuum," Daiki said, not looking up from his phone,
where he appeared to be reviewing lines of code between bites of rice.

"You don't get a vote. You're not paying attention." Kenji turned to Caspar.
"Ryu. You're the tie-breaker. Do we need traditions?"

Caspar considered this with the seriousness it warranted, which was minimal, but
he gave it the full weight of his attention anyway because he had learned, over
the course of four days, that Kenji operated on a fuel of attention and
acknowledgment, and starving him of it only made him louder.

"We could share food," Caspar said.

The table went quiet.

Kenji looked at Caspar's bento—today's offering from Auntie Park: rice,
galbi-jjim with tender braised short ribs, three kinds of banchan, and a small
container of fruit that included two more of the Tochigi Skyberries. Then he
looked at his own bento, with its lonely umeboshi and its abandoned broccoli.

"Share food," Kenji repeated. "Like, we each put something in the middle?"

"If you want."

Kenji picked up a piece of his fried chicken with his chopsticks, held it out
over the center of the desk formation, and dropped it onto Caspar's bento lid
with the ceremony of a knight laying a sword on an altar.

"I offer this chicken in the name of HCCL."

Caspar, without changing expression, placed a piece of galbi-jjim on Kenji's
bento lid.

Kenji stared at the braised short rib. It was dark with soy and sesame, the meat
falling apart at the edges, glistening with the gelatinous sheen of a braise
that had been going for at least three hours. He picked it up. He put it in his
mouth. He chewed.

His eyes closed.

"Ryu."

"Yes."

"What _is_ this."

"Galbi-jjim. Braised short ribs."

"I know what galbi-jjim is. I've had galbi-jjim. This is not galbi-jjim. This
is—" Kenji opened his eyes. They were slightly wider than usual. "This is a
religious experience. Who made this? Was it your mom?"

"My housekeeper."

"Your—" Kenji processed the word. Unlike Yuki, who had processed it with a
single fractional eyebrow raise and filed it silently, Kenji processed it out
loud, his face cycling through surprise, curiosity, recalibration, and finally a
kind of cheerful acceptance. "You have a housekeeper. Okay. Cool. Is she
single?"

"She's fifty-eight."

"Age is just a number when galbi-jjim is on the line."

"She would destroy you," Caspar said.

"I believe you." Kenji took another piece. "Ren. Ren, you have to try this. Get
in here."

Ren, who had been eating his own lunch—a clean, organized bento of rice, grilled
salmon, and blanched vegetables assembled with the precision of someone who
viewed food as fuel—reached over without comment, took a piece of galbi-jjim,
and ate it.

He chewed. He swallowed. He looked at Caspar.

"That's excellent," he said. Two words. Maximum Ren.

"Daiki." Kenji pushed the remaining piece toward the fourth member of the group.
"Put the phone down and eat this. This is more important than your dungeon
crawler."

Daiki set his phone down with the reluctance of a parent setting down a newborn.
He ate the galbi-jjim. He adjusted his glasses. He looked at Caspar with an
expression of mild scientific interest.

"The collagen breakdown on that braise is significant. The gelatin content of
the sauce suggests a cooking time of at least three hours at low simmer. The
soy-to-sugar ratio is—"

"Daiki, you're not _analyzing_ the food, you're _eating_ it—"

"I can do both."

"You literally cannot—"

"The Maillard reaction on the seared surface indicates an initial high-heat step
before the braise," Daiki continued, unperturbed. "Probably a cast-iron pan.
Heavy-bottomed."

Caspar looked at Daiki. Something clicked—a recognition, a kinship. Daiki's mind
worked the way a camera worked: observing, capturing, breaking down the
composition of a thing into its component elements and understanding how they
fit together. It was the same instinct that made Caspar frame a shot, the same
analytical attention applied to a different medium.

"You're right," Caspar said. "She uses a cast-iron pot. Korean-made."

Daiki nodded, satisfied. He returned to his phone. Kenji stared at both of them
with the bewildered admiration of someone watching a conversation in a language
he didn't speak but could tell was important.

"I don't understand either of you," Kenji said. "But I love this group."

---

The food-sharing tradition stuck.

By the end of Thursday's lunch, each member of HCCL had contributed something to
the center: Kenji's fried chicken, Caspar's galbi-jjim, Ren's grilled salmon
(donated without comment, received with gratitude), and a single onigiri from
Daiki that he had purchased from the school store and offered with the mumbled
caveat that "it's just tuna mayo, don't get excited."

The exchange was small. The portions were small. The gesture was not.

Caspar understood this because the hotel had taught him the weight of small
things. At the Alpenhof, the difference between a good stay and a great one was
never the suite or the view or the thread count. It was the glass of water
brought without being asked. The extra pillow placed on the bed because the
concierge had noticed the guest adjusting the existing ones. The handwritten
note from the general manager on a birthday, the name spelled correctly, the
message specific enough to prove it wasn't a template.

Small things. Precise things. Things that said _I see you, I noticed, you
matter._

A piece of galbi-jjim on a plastic bento lid in a public school classroom was
exactly that kind of thing.

## III.

Friday. The first week was ending.

Isabelle brought madeleines for the girls.

She had baked a fresh batch Thursday night—twelve shells, each one inspected and
graded on a personal rubric that accounted for bosse height, color uniformity,
and structural integrity. She packed six in a container: two for Yuki, two for
Sora, two for Hina. She left the container unsealed so the madeleines could
breathe. She wrapped it in a cloth. She placed it in her bag beside her bento
and her skincare pouch and the small notebook where she wrote things in Dutch
that she didn't want anyone to read.

At morning break, she set the container on her desk and removed the cloth.

The smell hit the room immediately—browned butter and vanilla, warm and complex,
cutting through the ambient classroom scent of chalk and floor polish with the
quiet authority of something that was unambiguously, professionally excellent.

Yuki looked up from her book. Her nostrils flared—a single, involuntary
expansion, barely visible, the kind of reflexive response that happened before
conscious thought could intervene.

"What is that," she said.

"Madeleines. I made them last night."

"You made—" Yuki set the book down. This was significant. Isabelle had never
seen Yuki voluntarily abandon a book mid-paragraph. "You made these."

"Yes."

"In your apartment."

"In my kitchen."

Yuki reached for one. She held it up, examining it the way she examined
everything—with a systematic thoroughness that bordered on forensic. She turned
it over, inspecting the bosse, the scalloped edges, the color gradient from
golden brown at the crust to pale gold at the center. She brought it to her nose
and inhaled.

Then she bit into it.

The classroom continued around them. Students talked, laughed, checked their
phones. A boy near the front was trying to balance a ruler on his finger. Two
girls by the window were comparing sticker collections. The normal, noisy
machinery of a Japanese high school homeroom during break.

Yuki chewed. She swallowed. She looked at Isabelle.

"This is not a hobby," she said.

The statement landed with the precise, devastating accuracy of a well-placed
arrow. Not a question. Not a compliment. A diagnosis. Yuki had tasted the
madeleine and derived from its flavor, its texture, and its technical execution
a conclusion about the person who had made it: that this level of skill was not
acquired casually, was not maintained recreationally, and was not the output of
someone who "liked baking" the way people liked baking. This was the output of
someone who had been _trained._

"I took a course," Isabelle said. "In France. A few years ago."

"What kind of course?"

"A patisserie intensive. Two weeks."

"Two weeks produced this?"

"I've practiced a lot since then."

Yuki took another bite. She chewed slowly, her eyes slightly narrowed, the file
being updated in real time with data points that Isabelle could almost see being
cross-referenced: _housekeeper, skincare routine, ballet training, French pastry
course, apartment in walking distance of Aoyama._ Each item alone was
explicable. Together, they formed a constellation that pointed toward a
conclusion Yuki was not yet ready to articulate but was clearly, methodically,
approaching.

"These are extraordinary," Yuki said. "Thank you."

"You're welcome."

Sora arrived. She had been in the hallway, performing what she called her
"morning social round" (visiting friends in other classes, exchanging gossip,
checking hairstyles), and she returned to find Yuki eating a madeleine with an
expression of concentrated reverence.

"What. What is that. Why does it smell like Paris in here? Ji-won, _did you
bake?_"

"I brought some madeleines."

Sora took one. She did not examine it with forensic precision. She bit into it
immediately, aggressively, with the wholehearted commitment of someone who
approached food the way she approached VESPER fandom: with zero hesitation and
maximum emotional investment.

"Oh my _God._"

"Do you like it?"

"Ji-won. Ji-won, listen to me." Sora held the remaining half of the madeleine at
eye level, regarding it with the intensity of someone who had just discovered a
new dimension of reality. "This is the best thing I have ever eaten. Including
the tonkatsu at Maisen. Including my grandmother's chahan. Including the crêpe I
had in Harajuku that cost 800 yen and changed my understanding of what food
could be."

"It's just a madeleine."

"It is not _just_ a madeleine. It's a _life event._" Sora turned to the room at
large. "Everyone! Ji-won made madeleines! They're incredible! Form a line!"

"Please don't—"

"MADELEINES! FREE MADELEINES!"

Isabelle closed the container. "There are only six. They're spoken for."

"Spoken for? By whom?"

"You, Yuki, and Hina."

From two rows back, Hina's voice: "Me?"

Isabelle turned. Hina was at her desk, chin in hand, watching the commotion with
her characteristic expression of amused detachment. But something had shifted in
her eyes at the sound of her name—a small flicker of surprise, quickly
controlled, the look of someone who was used to observing from the periphery and
was not accustomed to being seen.

"I brought two for you," Isabelle said. She picked up the container, walked to
Hina's desk, and held it out.

Hina looked at the container. She looked at Isabelle. The eye contact lasted
three seconds—long enough for something to pass between them, something unspoken
and precise, a communication conducted entirely in the register of two people
who were both very good at watching and were, for the first time, being watched
by each other.

Hina took a madeleine.

She ate it slowly. She didn't comment on the flavor, the texture, or the
technique. She ate it, set the shell-shaped impression of her first bite down on
a tissue, and said:

"You didn't learn this in two weeks."

Isabelle said nothing.

"But you don't want to talk about it," Hina continued. "That's fine." She picked
the madeleine back up. "Thank you, Shin-san."

"Ji-won."

A pause. The smallest recalibration—a word being weighed, a distance being
measured, a decision being made about how close to stand.

"Ji-won," Hina said. "Thank you."

She returned to her madeleine and her window and her watching.

## IV.

Friday afternoon. Club recruitment.

The gymnasium was transformed—tables arranged in long rows down both sides of
the hall, each one staffed by upperclassmen with signs and banners and varying
degrees of enthusiasm. The air hummed with the competitive energy of forty-odd
clubs competing for the attention of two hundred first-years who were streaming
through the doors with the wide-eyed, slightly overwhelmed expressions of
consumers at a market they hadn't prepared for.

Isabelle walked through the gauntlet with Yuki on one side and Sora on the
other. The three of them had fallen into a configuration that felt, after only
five days, surprisingly natural: Yuki on the left, organized and navigational,
guiding them through the space with the efficiency of a GPS; Sora on the right,
reactive and enthusiastic, pulled toward every table with bright colors or loud
music; and Isabelle in the center, tall and visible, a landmark the other two
orbited.

"Dance club," Sora said, pointing at a table where a group of second-year girls
were performing a choreographed routine to a song playing from a portable
speaker. The choreography was basic—a TikTok-style arrangement of simple
isolations and arm movements, performed with energy but without the precision
that would have passed muster in a professional setting.

Isabelle watched. Her body did a thing it always did when she watched dance—a
subtle internal engagement, a mirror response, her muscles firing in sympathetic
micro-contractions as they tracked the movement. She could feel the choreography
in her bones, could feel her body wanting to correct, to refine, to extend the
movements past their amateur endpoints into the clean, full-body articulation
that Park Jisoo had spent ten months drilling into her.

She didn't move. She watched the way a retired surgeon watches a medical
drama—with professional interest and a practiced poker face.

"Are you interested?" Yuki asked.

"I'll think about it."

"Your posture changed while you were watching them."

Isabelle looked at Yuki. Yuki looked back with her characteristic expression of
neutral, forensic attention.

"What do you mean?" Isabelle said.

"Your spine straightened. Your shoulders dropped back and down. Your weight
shifted to the balls of your feet. It happened unconsciously. You did the same
thing yesterday when Sora showed you the VESPER dance practice video."

Isabelle processed this. Yuki's powers of observation were, she was realizing,
not a personality quirk but a genuine perceptual capability—an ability to
register and catalog physical details that most people's brains filtered out as
noise. It was, in its own way, the same skill that Caspar used when he framed a
photograph: seeing what was actually there instead of what you expected to see.

"I used to dance," Isabelle said. "Ballet. It's probably muscle memory."

"Muscle memory from ballet makes you react to K-pop choreography?"

"Dance is dance."

Yuki accepted this with a small nod that communicated, clearly, _I am accepting
this for now and reserving the right to revisit it later._ They moved on.

The photography club's table was modest—a folding table with a printed sign, a
few framed photos on small easels, and a second-year boy with an undercut and a
DSLR around his neck who looked like he hadn't slept in several days.

"We meet Tuesdays and Thursdays in the art room," the boy said, with the
slightly desperate energy of a club that was always three members away from
being dissolved. "We do digital and film. We have access to a darkroom—well,
it's more of a converted closet in the basement, but it works. We do exhibitions
twice a year."

Isabelle studied the framed photos. They were competent—a sunset over the school
courtyard, a close-up of a cicada on a tree trunk, a blurred long-exposure of
traffic at night. The technical skill was there but the _seeing_ wasn't—the
images showed what was in front of the camera without revealing anything about
the person behind it.

Caspar's photos were different. Caspar's photos were always different. Even his
casual shots—the ones he took on walks, through car windows, in the margins of
ordinary days—had a quality that she could only describe as _attention._ Not
just looking, but attending: a deliberate, almost reverent engagement with the
subject that made even mundane scenes feel like they mattered. His photos didn't
show you what things looked like. They showed you what things _meant._

She realized she was standing at the photography club table thinking about
Caspar and needed to stop.

"Thank you," she said to the undercut boy, and moved on.

---

Across the gymnasium, Caspar was having a different experience.

He had entered the club recruitment fair with Kenji at his side—a proximity that
was becoming, he was beginning to understand, a permanent condition. Kenji had
attached himself to Caspar with the cheerful, irrevocable tenacity of a
barnacle, interpreting Caspar's stoic silence not as a deterrent but as a
challenge, a surface to be adhered to through sheer force of personality.

"Track club," Kenji said, pointing. "Ren's already over there. Look at him,
signing up like it's a military enlistment."

Ren was at the track club table, speaking with the club captain—a third-year
with the lean, wiry build and the sun-darkened complexion of someone who spent
every afternoon running in circles. Their conversation was brief, efficient, and
appeared to involve no words beyond the strictly necessary.

"Computer club," Kenji continued, pointing at another table. "Daiki's
definitely—yep, there he is. Look at him, pretending to be casual about it. He's
been waiting for this all week."

Daiki was at the computer club table, examining a laptop display with the
restrained excitement of an archaeologist who had just unearthed something
significant.

"What about you?" Caspar asked.

"Comedy club." Kenji said this without hesitation, with the confidence of
someone who had known his path since birth. "They have a comedy club. I checked
the list twice. They perform at the culture festival. It's my destiny."

"And me?"

"You? Photography, obviously. It's over there." Kenji pointed toward the far
wall, where the undercut boy was now talking to a first-year girl. "But I gotta
warn you, the club's kind of small. Like, five members. They might be
desperately excited to see you."

"I'll manage."

"You'll manage." Kenji shook his head. "You're the most managing person I've
ever met. You manage the way other people breathe. It's incredible."

They split up. Kenji headed for the comedy club table with the momentum of a man
answering a calling. Caspar walked toward the photography club.

He had almost reached it when he saw her.

Isabelle was standing at a table thirty feet away—the cooking club, it looked
like, staffed by a cheerful second-year girl in an apron who was offering
samples of what appeared to be tamagoyaki. Yuki was beside her, reading the
club's information pamphlet with the thoroughness of someone reviewing a legal
contract. Sora was elsewhere, probably at whatever table was playing K-pop.

Isabelle was listening to the cooking club girl explain the club's
activities—monthly cooking sessions, a booth at the culture festival, occasional
field trips to local restaurants. She was nodding politely, her expression open
and interested, her body language conveying the perfect simulation of a normal
student exploring her options.

But Caspar saw through it. He saw the way her eyes moved past the cooking club
girl to scan the gymnasium—a quick, practiced sweep, the kind of environmental
check that dancers and performers learned to do instinctively, mapping the space
and the people in it without appearing to look. He saw the sweep land on him.

She found him.

Their eyes met across the gymnasium, over the heads of a hundred milling
students, through the noise and the banners and the portable speakers. The
contact lasted one second. Maybe two. Long enough for a complete exchange: _I
see you. I know you see me. We are in the same room pretending to be separate
people and we are both aware of the performance._

She looked away first. Back to the cooking club girl. Back to the simulation.

Caspar walked to the photography club table.

"Hi," said the undercut boy. "Are you interested in photography?"

"Yes."

"Digital or film?"

"Film. 35mm."

The undercut boy's eyes widened. He leaned forward with the sudden, intense
engagement of someone who had just met a fellow traveler in a country where his
language was rarely spoken. "Film? Seriously? What do you shoot? What camera?"

"Leica M6. Mostly Tri-X 400. Some Portra for color."

"A _Leica_—" The boy's voice cracked. He recovered. "Sorry. We— I'm Fujita, by
the way. Fujita Kōhei. Third-year. I'm the club president. We have a darkroom.
It's small. But it has an enlarger and chemical trays and—you develop your own
prints?"

"I have a darkroom at home."

Fujita looked like he was about to cry. "Please join our club. I'm not above
begging. We have five members and two of them only joined because they thought
it would be an easy credit. You're the first person who's mentioned actual film
in the three years I've been running this club."

"I'll join," Caspar said.

"Really? You're not— that's not— really?"

"Really."

Fujita reached across the table and shook Caspar's hand with both of his, his
grip communicating a gratitude that bordered on spiritual.

## V.

Friday evening. The first week was over.

Isabelle sat on the floor of her living room with her legs stretched in front of
her, her back against the sofa, her phone in her hands. The apartment was
quiet—Auntie Choi had gone to the Korean grocery in Shin-Ōkubo for the Saturday
shopping, a weekly pilgrimage that involved three trains and a list organized by
aisle number. The light through the windows was the deep amber of early Tokyo
evening, the sun sitting low over the western skyline, painting the room in
tones of honey and shadow.

Her phone buzzed. A group chat. Not the one with Sora and Yuki—a different one,
older, the one that made her chest tight every time a notification appeared.

**VESPER 🖤** (5 members)

> **유나언니:** 지원아~~ 첫 주 어땠어?? 학교 재밌어?? 남자애들 잘생겼어?? 보고
> 싶어 ㅠㅠ
>
> _Ji-won-ah~~ How was your first week?? Is school fun?? Are the boys cute?? I
> miss you ㅠㅠ_

> **민지언니:** 유나야 좀 진정해. 지원아 잘 지내고 있지?
>
> _Yuna, calm down. Ji-won, you're doing well?_

> **하은언니:** 나도 궁금!! 도쿄 날씨 어때? 벚꽃 폈어?
>
> _I'm curious too!! How's the weather in Tokyo? Are the cherry blossoms out?_

> **지우:** 언니 보고 싶어요. 학교에서 춤 추고 있어요?
>
> _I miss you, unnie. Are you dancing at school?_

Isabelle stared at the screen. Four messages from four women who were, at this
moment, somewhere in Seoul—probably at the agency, probably in rehearsal,
probably being filmed for a behind-the-scenes vlog that would be released in
three weeks and watched by eleven million people. Four women who called her
_Ji-won-ah_ and _unnie_ and who texted her the way sisters texted: casually,
constantly, without preamble.

She typed:
