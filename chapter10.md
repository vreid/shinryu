# Chapter 10: Glass Hour

---

## I.

Being Caspar's girlfriend changed nothing and everything.

Nothing, because the fundamental architecture of their days was the same:
separate classrooms, shared lunches, the LINE thread that ran like an
underground river beneath the surface of the school day, carrying messages in
Korean that ranged from the mundane (_Morimoto-sensei's pen-clicking is worse
today_) to the intimate (_I keep thinking about your hands_). The school
operated on its rhythms—bells, lessons, cleaning duty, clubs—and they moved
through those rhythms the way they had since the first day, navigating the
institutional choreography of a Japanese public high school with the quiet
competence of two people who had been trained, in different ways, to perform.

Everything, because the performance had changed. The filter was still there—the
calibrated Japanese, the studied ordinariness, the careful management of what
they revealed and to whom—but between them, in the private space they had carved
out of the public world, the filter was off. The distance was gone. The
forty-centimeter gap was a memory.

Thursday morning. Caspar waited at the gate again. This was now precedent—two
consecutive days established a pattern, and Caspar understood patterns the way
his mother understood brand architecture: as structures that communicated
without speaking.

Isabelle arrived at 8:11. She was carrying two bags today—her school bag and a
second, smaller bag that she held by the handle, away from her body, with the
delicate caution of someone transporting something fragile.

"What's that?" he said.

"Tarte au citron."

"You made a tarte au citron."

"Last night. For the combined lunch."

He looked at the bag. He looked at her. "How many people?"

"Eight. I cut it into ten slices so there are extras."

"You baked a tarte au citron for eight people on a Wednesday night."

"It's not difficult. The crust is pâte sucrée—I can do it in my sleep. The curd
is just lemon, sugar, eggs, and butter. The meringue takes ten minutes." She
paused. "I torched the meringue with Auntie Choi's kitchen torch. She
supervised. She had opinions."

"What kind of opinions?"

"She said the browning was uneven on the left side. She's right, but I'm
choosing to view it as rustic."

He took the bag from her. She let him—transferring the handle with the precise
handoff of a relay runner passing a baton—and he carried it in his left hand,
his right hand finding hers, their fingers interlacing with the automatic ease
of a gesture that was, after four days, already muscle memory.

They walked through the gate. Hands together. The tarte au citron between them
and the school day ahead.

---

The combined lunch had become, by the end of week three, an institution.

The desk formation in Class 1-2 was no longer assembled by Kenji alone—Ren now
helped, moving the furniture with the efficient, wordless cooperation of someone
who had accepted that this was happening and had decided to optimize it. The
configuration had expanded to accommodate eight: Caspar, Isabelle, Kenji, Ren,
Daiki, Yuki, Sora, and Hina. Sora had begun referring to the group as "the
table," which had the accidental effect of making them sound like a medieval
council, which Kenji found delightful and immediately leaned into.

"The table is convened," Kenji announced, as the last desk clicked into place.
"I call this session to order. First agenda item: Shin-san has brought—" He
looked at the bag Caspar was setting on the desk. "—something that smells
insane."

Isabelle opened the bag. She lifted out the tarte.

It was presented on a round of parchment paper, the edges trimmed clean, the
tart itself a perfect circle of golden pâte sucrée filled with pale yellow lemon
curd and topped with a crown of Italian meringue, torched to a gradient of ivory
and caramel and deep amber. The surface of the meringue was a landscape of peaks
and valleys, each one tipped with a shade of brown that moved from pale gold at
the base to nearly black at the crest, the browning uneven on the left side in a
way that looked, despite Auntie Choi's critique, deliberately artisanal.

The table went quiet.

Kenji was the first to speak. "Shin-san. What is this."

"Tarte au citron."

"I don't know what those words mean, but I know that I would fight someone for
it."

"It's a French lemon tart. With meringue."

"French. Lemon. Tart. With. Meringue." Kenji repeated each word as a separate
sentence, giving each one the weight it deserved. "Shin-san, do you understand
that there are professionals—people who went to school for this, people who do
this for a living—who cannot make something that looks like that?"

"It's just a tart."

"It's not 'just a tart.' Daiki, tell her it's not just a tart."

Daiki was looking at the meringue with the focused attention he normally
reserved for code architecture. "The caramelization pattern on the meringue
suggests a butane torch at approximately 1,200 degrees, held at varying
distances to create the gradient. The crust edge is uniform to within two
millimeters, which indicates either a professional tart ring or exceptional
hand-shaping skill." He adjusted his glasses. "It's not just a tart."

"See?" Kenji said. "Science agrees."

Isabelle cut the tart. Ten slices, as promised—she had brought a small knife
from home, wrapped in a cloth, the blade thin and sharp, the kind of knife that
existed in home kitchens only if someone in that kitchen cooked seriously. She
served the slices on squares of parchment paper, distributing them around the
table with the practiced efficiency of someone who had done this before, in a
different context, for different people.

She had done this before. In the Shin Entertainment practice room. At 11 PM.
After a twelve-hour rehearsal, when four exhausted trainees were lying on the
floor in their practice clothes and Isabelle had walked in from the small staff
kitchen carrying a tray of tarte au citron slices that she'd made during a
break, and Yuna had sat up and said, _"Shin Ji-won, if you ever leave us, I will
have you arrested,"_ and Minji had eaten her slice in silence and then hugged
Isabelle without warning, and Haeun had cried because she was tired and the tart
was good and sometimes those two things combined into tears, and Jiwoo had eaten
hers slowly, methodically, and then said, _"Teach me how to make this,"_ and
Isabelle had said, _"The crust takes three years to learn,"_ and Jiwoo had said,
_"I have time."_

The table ate.

The reactions were various and universal. Kenji closed his eyes and made a sound
that was somewhere between reverence and distress. Ren ate his slice in four
bites and then looked at the remainder of the tart with the controlled longing
of someone who wanted more but would not ask. Daiki ate his while taking notes
on his phone, documenting the flavor profile with the thoroughness of a food
critic. Sora ate hers and immediately began photographing the remaining slices
from multiple angles, muttering about "food content" and "aesthetic."

Yuki ate hers slowly. One bite at a time. Her eyes moved from the tart to
Isabelle's face to the knife Isabelle had brought to the way Isabelle's hands
had moved while cutting—the particular economy of motion, the confidence, the
absence of hesitation.

"Ji-won," Yuki said.

"Mm."

"The two-week course in France."

"Yes."

"What was it called?"

The table continued eating around them. Kenji was asking Caspar if Auntie Park
could make a Korean dessert for next week's lunch. Sora was showing Hina a photo
of the tart she'd just taken. Ren was stretching his calves under the desk.

"It was a private course," Isabelle said. "At a small school near the Bastille."

"And the instructor?"

"A pastry chef. Very experienced."

"A Meilleur Ouvrier de France, by any chance?"

Isabelle looked at Yuki. The precision of the question—the specific, technical
title, the French terminology deployed without hesitation—told her that Yuki had
been researching. Yuki had gone home, probably that first week, and looked up
French patisserie training programs, and had identified the tier of instruction
that could produce the level of skill Isabelle was demonstrating, and had
arrived at the conclusion that the "two-week course" was not at a community
cooking school but at an institution where the instructors held the highest
culinary distinction in France.

"Something like that," Isabelle said.

Yuki nodded. She ate another bite of tart. She did not press further. The file
was updated. The picture was becoming clearer—not complete, not yet, but
clearer, the outline sharpening, the shape of what Isabelle was hiding resolving
from a blur into a form that Yuki could almost name.

---

## II.

Friday afternoon. The first real test.

It came from outside the school—from the world that existed beyond the gates of
Sakuragaoka, the world of train stations and commercial districts and the
specific, accelerating energy of a Tokyo Friday afternoon when the week ended
and the city exhaled.

The group—all eight of them, the full table—had decided to go to a café after
school. This was Kenji's initiative, naturally—he had identified a place in
Shibuya, a chain café near the station, the kind of place where high school
students went because the drinks were cheap and the seating was abundant and no
one cared if you stayed for two hours.

They walked from school to the station in a loose cluster—Kenji and Sora at the
front, their VESPER conversation now a permanent background broadcast that the
others had learned to tune in and out of like a radio; Ren and Daiki in the
middle, Ren walking at a pace that required Daiki to lengthen his stride; Yuki
and Hina behind them, Yuki reading as she walked (a skill she had developed,
Isabelle suspected, as a form of social self-defense); and Caspar and Isabelle
at the back, their hands linked, their pace matched, the quiet couple anchoring
the group's rear guard.

They reached Shibuya. The station disgorged them into the scramble crossing's
chaos—the famous intersection where a thousand people crossed simultaneously
from all directions, the pedestrian signal creating a brief window of organized
anarchy that was, Isabelle thought, the purest expression of Tokyo's
relationship with collective movement: total freedom within total structure.

They crossed. They found the café. They went inside.

The café was standard—blonde wood, pendant lights, a menu of flavored lattes and
cream sodas displayed on backlit panels above the counter. The music was J-pop
at a volume that existed precisely at the boundary of audible and ignorable. The
seating was a mix of booths and tables, most of them occupied by clusters of
students from various schools, identifiable by their uniforms.

Kenji secured a booth. The eight of them compressed into a space designed for
six, the resulting proximity creating a physical closeness that forced the group
into an intimacy that the school's desk formation only approximated. Isabelle
was pressed between Caspar and Yuki, her thigh against Caspar's thigh, her
shoulder against Yuki's shoulder, the warmth of eight bodies in a vinyl booth
generating a specific, adolescent heat.

Orders were placed. Kenji got a caramel latte. Sora got a strawberry cream soda.
Ren got water. Daiki got a black coffee, which provoked Kenji into a
three-minute lecture about how black coffee at age fifteen was "a personality
statement, not a beverage choice." Yuki got green tea. Hina got an iced cocoa.
Isabelle got an oat milk latte. Caspar got a pour-over, which was not on the
menu, which he discovered after ordering, which resulted in a brief, awkward
exchange with the counter staff that ended with him ordering a regular drip
coffee and sitting down with an expression that suggested the chain café had
personally offended him.

"Ryu-kun," Sora said, leaning across the table. "You ordered a pour-over. At a
chain café."

"It was a reflex."

"A _reflex._ Who has pour-over reflexes? That's like—that's like asking for a
wine list at McDonald's."

"He had a barista at the hotel," Isabelle said, without thinking.

The table went quiet.

The word _hotel_ landed in the booth with the specific, resonant weight of a
bell being struck. It was the first time the hotel had been mentioned in front
of anyone other than Isabelle. It was a detail from Caspar's hidden life—the gap
year, the porter year, the year of invisible labor—and it had slipped through
the filter because Isabelle was comfortable, because the booth was warm, because
Caspar's thigh was against hers, and because the architecture of secrecy relaxed
in the presence of people you trusted, which was both its greatest feature and
its most dangerous flaw.

Caspar's expression didn't change. The stoic architecture held. But Isabelle
felt the shift—a micro-tension in his thigh against hers, a stiffening of his
posture, the physical evidence of a filter being hastily reconstructed.

"Hotel?" Kenji said.

"Ji-won means a hotel café," Caspar said. Smoothly. Without hesitation. "There's
a hotel near my old apartment in Germany. The café had good pour-over."

"A hotel café in Germany. With pour-over. That's—okay, yeah, that tracks.
Germans take coffee seriously."

The moment passed. The conversation moved on—Kenji pivoted to a story about a
coffee vending machine at his uncle's office that dispensed a liquid he
described as "technically coffee in the same way that technically a bicycle is a
vehicle"—and the table laughed and the booth relaxed and the filter held.

Under the table, Caspar's hand found Isabelle's. He squeezed once. Brief. The
squeeze said: _I know. It's okay. We'll be more careful._

She squeezed back. _I know. I'm sorry._

---

They stayed at the café for an hour and a half. The conversation moved through
the topics that eight fifteen-year-olds generated when compressed into a booth
with caffeine: school gossip, teacher impressions, club activities, weekend
plans. Sora launched a VESPER discussion that pulled Kenji in like a tractor
beam and left the others orbiting at various distances of engagement.

"The second single drops next Friday," Sora said, her phone out, displaying a
countdown timer she had set on her home screen. "Six days, fourteen hours,
twenty-two minutes. The music video teaser is already out. Have you seen it?
Have you _seen_ it?"

She played the teaser. Thirty seconds of footage—the library concept from the
photos, but in motion now, the four members of VESPER moving through a dark
wood-paneled space, their choreography visible in fragments between cuts. The
editing was fast—two-second clips, jump cuts, the kind of rapid-fire visual
storytelling that demanded multiple viewings to fully parse.

Isabelle watched the teaser on Sora's phone, which was being held at an angle
that allowed the entire table to see.

She recognized the choreography. Not from the teaser—from the source. She had
been in the room when the choreographer, Park Jisoo, had first demonstrated the
movement vocabulary for the second single. She had watched Jisoo build the
choreography in real time—block by block, eight-count by eight-count, the
movements evolving from rough sketches into polished sequences over the course
of three days. She had been asked to demonstrate a transition—a turn sequence
that Jisoo wanted to test before teaching it to the group—and she had performed
it, and Jisoo had watched, and Jisoo had said, _"That. Exactly that. Don't
change anything."_

The transition was in the teaser. Frame 22 through 25—Minji performing the turn,
the exact turn, the turn that had originated in Isabelle's body and migrated
through Jisoo's choreographic notation into the final product that was now being
watched by millions.

Isabelle's face did not change. Her breathing did not change. She watched the
teaser with the same expression of mild, friendly interest she wore for all
VESPER-related content—the filter working overtime, the composure tested to its
limit, the distance between her public self and her private history stretching
like a wire under tension.

Beside her, Caspar was also watching.

She could feel his attention—not on the teaser but on her. He was watching her
watch it. The photographer's eye, tracking her micro-expressions, reading the
tension she was hiding, seeing the things she didn't know she was showing.

He saw something. She felt him see it—a stiffening beside her, a change in the
quality of his attention, a shift from passive observation to active analysis.
He had seen something in her reaction to the teaser that had connected to
something else—to the Jiwoo extension, to the ballet posture, to the gap year in
Seoul, to the accumulating evidence of a secret that was larger and more
specific than mere wealth.

The teaser ended. Sora replayed it. The table discussed it—Kenji asked about the
library concept ("Is that a real library? Can we go there?"), Daiki analyzed the
editing pace ("2.3-second average shot length, which is consistent with
contemporary K-pop visual language"), Ren said nothing. Yuki observed. Hina
observed.

Caspar said: "The choreography is different from 'Glass Hour.'"

The table paused. Sora looked at him.

"You noticed the choreography?" Sora said.

"The first single was more synchronized—four members moving in unison, tight
formations. This looks more individual. The members have separate movement
phrases."

Sora stared at him. The stare was the stare of a person who had just heard a
casual acquaintance deploy the phrase "separate movement phrases" in reference
to K-pop choreography and who was reassessing every assumption she'd made about
that person's familiarity with the genre.

"Ryu-kun," Sora said. "How much VESPER have you watched?"

"Some."

"'Some' doesn't produce the phrase 'separate movement phrases.' 'Some' produces
'they dance differently.' What you just said was _analysis._"

Caspar looked at his coffee. The drip coffee that was not a pour-over. The
coffee that represented, in its mediocrity, the gap between what he wanted and
what he was willing to reveal.

"I've watched more than some," he said.

Sora's eyes widened. She leaned across the table. "How much more?"

"Sora," Isabelle said. Gently. The intervention was subtle—a single word,
deployed at the precise moment when the conversation was about to tip from
curiosity into interrogation. It was the same technique she'd used in the
practice room when Yuna pushed a joke too far or when Haeun's honesty crossed
into bluntness: the quiet redirect, the social cushion, the careful application
of interpersonal pressure that changed the trajectory without anyone feeling
pushed.

Sora heard it. She leaned back. The interrogation defused.

"Well," Sora said, after a beat. "When you're ready to admit you're a fan, I'll
be here. With open arms. And a lightstick."

Caspar looked at Isabelle. The look lasted one second—a private exchange,
conducted in the space between the public conversation, encrypted in the
language of shared knowledge. _She almost got me. You saved me. Thank you._

Isabelle looked back. _I know. Be more careful._

Under the table, their hands were still linked. The squeeze came again—not a
warning this time, but a reassurance. _We're okay. We're together. The secrets
are heavy, but we're carrying them together, and together they're lighter._

## III.

Saturday. The third weekend.

They didn't go to a neighborhood this time. They went to the river.

The Tama River ran along the western edge of Tokyo, wide and slow, its banks a
mix of concrete flood walls and grassy embankments where the city thinned and
the sky opened up. Caspar had found the spot on a map—a section of the riverbank
near Futako-Tamagawa where the embankment was wide and grassy and the view was
long, the river stretching west toward mountains that were visible on clear days
as a blue-gray smudge at the horizon's edge.

They sat on the grass. The afternoon was warm—genuinely warm, the first day that
felt like the edge of summer rather than the tail of spring. The sky was a deep,
washed blue. The river moved past in no hurry, its surface catching the sunlight
in shifting patterns that made the water look like it was made of something
thinner and more precious than water.

Caspar had the Leica. He photographed the river, the grass, the distant
mountains, a dog running on the opposite bank, a child's lost sandal sitting on
the concrete wall like a small monument to summer.

Isabelle lay on the grass beside him. She was wearing a white linen
shirt—untucked, sleeves rolled—and high-waisted shorts and sneakers, and she was
lying on her back with her arms behind her head and her eyes closed and her hair
spread across the grass in a fan of honey-colored waves that caught the light
and held it.

She was not sleeping. She was listening—to the river, to the cicadas that were
beginning to tune up in the trees along the bank, to the mechanical whisper of
Caspar's shutter as he photographed, to the sound of his breathing beside her,
steady and close.

"Caspar."

"Mm."

"I need to tell you something."

He lowered the camera. He looked at her. She was still on her back, eyes still
closed, her face turned slightly toward the sun, the light painting her features
in warm tones that made her look like a Renaissance portrait of someone who was
about to deliver difficult news.

"Okay," he said.

"About Seoul."

He set the camera down on the grass beside him. He gave her his full
attention—not the diffused attention of someone listening while doing something
else, but the complete, directed focus of someone who understood that this was
the moment, the disclosure he'd been waiting for, the door she was finally
choosing to open.

Isabelle opened her eyes. She looked at the sky—the deep blue, the cirrus clouds
stretched thin and high, the contrails of distant aircraft drawn in white lines
across the dome.

"During the gap year," she said, "I didn't just study Korean and take etiquette
lessons. My mother arranged something else."

"Your mother."

"She placed me at her agency. Shin Entertainment. As a—" She paused. The word
was precise and she needed to get it right because getting it right mattered,
because the difference between what she had been and what VESPER had become was
the difference between a footnote and a headline. "—as a training participant.
Not a trainee. I was never going to debut. But I trained with the group. With
VESPER. For ten months."

The river moved. The cicadas sang. A distant train crossed a bridge, its wheels
making a rhythmic clatter that carried across the water.

Caspar was quiet. She could feel his silence—not the stunned silence of someone
processing a shock, but the deep, still silence of someone who was receiving a
piece of information that confirmed something they had already begun to
understand. The Jiwoo extension. The ballet posture. The choreography
recognition. The gap year in Seoul at her mother's entertainment agency. The
pieces had been assembling themselves for weeks, and now the final piece had
arrived, and the picture was complete.

"You trained with VESPER," he said.

"I lived with them. In the trainee dorm. For ten months. I learned their
choreography. I learned stage presence, vocal technique, media training. I went
through the same daily schedule they went through—dance practice at 6 AM, vocal
lessons, performance evaluations, physical conditioning. Twelve-hour days.
Sometimes fourteen."

"And nobody knows."

"Nobody at school knows. My parents know. Auntie Choi knows. The members
know—they're my—" She stopped. She searched for the word that was accurate to
the relationship. Not friends. Not colleagues. Not mentors. "—they're my family.
My older sisters. They text me every day. They send me memes. Yuna steals my
snacks."

"Yuna."

"Seo Yuna. The one Sora is obsessed with. She's—" Isabelle smiled, her eyes
still on the sky, the smile specific and warm and private. "—she's ridiculous.
She's the funniest person I've ever met. She once ate an entire bag of my
imported Dutch cookies and left an IOU note that said 'will repay in love and
loyalty' with a drawing of a cat."

Caspar was looking at her. She could feel the quality of his gaze—not the
photographer's look, not the analytical assessment, but something simpler and
more overwhelming: the look of someone who was seeing the full person, the
complete architecture, all the rooms and corridors and hidden spaces that made
up Isabelle van Rijn, and who was not afraid of what he found.

"That's why you know the choreography," he said.

"I don't just know it. I can perform it. All of it. 'Glass Hour,' the second
single, the pre-debut material. I learned it alongside them. Park Jisoo—the
choreographer—she used me as a test body. She would build a sequence, have me
perform it first, then teach it to the group."

"The extension."

"What?"

"Jiwoo's extension. The hand thing—the reach at the end of the dance break.
That's ballet. I saw it in a photo and I recognized it."

Isabelle turned her head on the grass. She looked at him. He was sitting beside
her, the Leica on the ground, his pale blue eyes steady, his expression open in
a way that she had only seen in the darkroom and on the Daikanyama sidewalk—the
expression of someone who had lowered every defense and was trusting her with
the full, unprotected surface of his attention.

"You saw that," she said.

"I'm a photographer. I see provenance."

"Provenance."

"Where a gesture comes from. The origin point of a movement. Jiwoo's extension
didn't come from K-pop. It came from classical dance. It came from—" He looked
at her. "—from you."

She stared at him. The accuracy of it—the fact that he had traced a single
gesture, a single extension of a hand, backward through the choreographic chain
from a fancam on his phone to a rehearsal room in Gangnam to a Dutch-Korean girl
with five years of Amsterdam ballet—was so precise and so specific that it felt
like being photographed. Like being seen at a resolution she hadn't known
existed.

"Yes," she said. "From me."

They were quiet for a while. The river ran. The cicadas built their wall of
sound. The light shifted—the afternoon deepening, the shadows on the grass
lengthening, the warmth settling into the kind of golden stillness that happened
in the hour before the sun began to descend.

"Thank you," Caspar said.

"For telling you?"

"For trusting me."

She reached over and took his hand. Their fingers interlaced on the grass
between them—her hand warm from the sun, his hand cooler, the photographer's
hand that had been holding metal and glass.

"Your turn," she said.

"My turn?"

"I showed you mine. Show me yours."

He looked at her. She looked at him. The joke was deliberate—light, playful, the
kind of teasing that Isabelle deployed when a conversation had become so heavy
that it needed to be lifted. But underneath the joke was a genuine request: _I
told you my secret. Now tell me yours._

He knew which secret she meant. She had told him about VESPER. Now she wanted
him to tell her about VESPER—not the secret of the idol connection, but the
other secret, the smaller and in some ways more vulnerable one. The secret of
the fan.

He looked at the river. He looked at the sky. He looked at his camera, sitting
in the grass, the lens cap on, the body warm from the sun.

"I found them during the hotel year," he said. "November. It was—I was having a
bad week. A guest had complained about me. The head concierge gave me a formal
warning. I was standing in the staff corridor at midnight, and I had my phone,
and I was scrolling, and the algorithm served me a clip. A dance practice video.
Four girls in a rehearsal room."

"Which video?"

"The pre-debut one. The one that leaked before the official channel posted it.
Eight minutes, no cuts, no editing. Just—four people in a room, dancing. And the
quality of the movement—the precision, the synchronization, the way they used
the space—" He stopped. He picked up the camera. He turned it in his hands—the
unconscious rotation, the self-soothing gesture. "I watched it four times. Then
I watched everything else. Every clip, every fancam, every interview, every
behind-the-scenes reel. In two weeks, I had seen everything that existed. I
learned the fan chants. I learned the point choreography. I can—" He paused. A
breath. "I can perform the chorus of 'Glass Hour' from memory."

"Perform it."

"What?"

"Perform it. Right now."

He looked at her. She was sitting up now, cross-legged on the grass, her hair
falling around her shoulders, her eyes bright with something that was equal
parts amusement and affection and a third thing that he couldn't name but that
made his chest tight.

"We're on a riverbank."

"We're alone on a riverbank. No one can see us."

"I'm not—"

"Caspar." She leaned forward. Her face was very close. Her eyes held his. "I
trained with VESPER for ten months. I can perform their entire choreography with
my eyes closed. If you think I'm going to judge you for knowing the point
choreo, you have fundamentally misunderstood who I am."

He looked at her. He looked at the river. He looked at the empty embankment—no
one in sight, just grass and water and sky and the girl who had lived with the
group he quietly worshipped and who was asking him, with genuine warmth and zero
judgment, to show her the thing he had kept hidden since November.

He stood up.

He brushed the grass off his jeans. He squared his shoulders. He pushed his hair
back.

And then, on the grassy bank of the Tama River, on a Saturday afternoon in late
April, Caspar Waldstein—six-foot-one, icy-eyed, stoic-faced, the boy whose
resting expression could frost a windowpane—performed the chorus choreography of
VESPER's "Glass Hour."

He performed it precisely. Not approximately, not roughly, not with the
enthusiastic seventy-percent accuracy that Sora had achieved in Class 1-3.
Precisely. Every beat. Every isolation. Every formation position (adapted for a
solo performer, the spatial relationships maintained through muscle memory and
an understanding of the choreography's architecture that went beyond imitation
into comprehension). The body roll on the pre-chorus. The wrist flick on the
second beat of the bridge. The hip isolation that Sora had tried to teach Kenji.
The point move at the end of the chorus—left hand extended, fingers spread, the
iconic gesture that appeared on merchandise and in fan art and that Caspar
executed with the clean, sharp authority of someone who had practiced it alone
in his room at the Alpenhof after midnight, in his porter's uniform, with the
door closed and the lights dim and nobody watching.

He performed it for approximately forty-five seconds—the duration of one chorus
plus the post-chorus transition. Then he stopped. He stood on the riverbank,
breathing slightly harder than normal, his hair disheveled, his face flushed.

He looked at Isabelle.

Isabelle was looking at him with an expression he had never seen on her face
before.

Her mouth was open. Not dramatically—a small parting of the lips, the
physiological response to genuine surprise, the face of someone who had expected
competence and received mastery. Her eyes were wide. The hazel-green was dark,
dilated, the way they got when something reached her emotionally before her
filters could intervene.

"캐스퍼," she said.

"응."

"너 미쳤어." _You're insane._

"그렇게 나빴어?" _Was it that bad?_

"나빴냐고?" _Bad?_ She stood up. She walked to where he was standing. She
stopped in front of him—twelve inches away, the distance they no longer bothered
to maintain—and looked up into his face with an expression that had transitioned
from surprise to something warmer and more dangerous: admiration.

"That was better than most of the fans I've seen online," she said. "The timing
was clean. The isolations were sharp. Your wrist flick on the bridge is actually
more precise than—" She stopped herself. She had been about to say _more precise
than Haeun's original_, which was true but which would have been a comparison
between a secret fan-boy and a professional idol that would have crossed a line
she wasn't ready to acknowledge.

"More precise than what?" he said.

"Than I expected."

She was smiling. The full smile. The dimple. The smile that used her entire face
and that he had cataloged and chased and waited for since he was eleven years
old.

"Teach me something," he said.

"What?"

"The second single. The choreography. You know it. Teach me."

She looked at him. The request was specific and earnest and completely
genuine—the request of a fan asking the person who learned the choreography
alongside the artists to teach him the movements, which was absurd and wonderful
and exactly the kind of thing that could only happen between two people whose
secret lives had created a Venn diagram with an overlap zone the size of a
continent.

"Here?" she said.

"Here."

"On a riverbank."

"On a riverbank."

She laughed. The laugh was loud—louder than the cafeteria, louder than the
stairwell, the fullest and most uncontrolled sound he had heard from her. It
rang across the Tama River and bounced off the concrete wall on the far side and
came back to them softened and warm, and Caspar thought: _This. This is the
sound. This is the frame. This is the image I will never be able to capture
because it exists only in real time, only in this moment, only on this riverbank
with this girl who is laughing because I just performed K-pop choreography for
her and she thinks I'm insane and she is right and I don't care._

"Okay," Isabelle said. She pulled her hair back and tied it with the elastic she
wore on her wrist. She kicked off her sneakers. She stepped onto the grass in
her bare feet.

"Face me," she said. "Mirror me. The second single starts with a four-count
prep—"

She demonstrated. The prep: weight shift right, arms neutral, chin slightly
down. Then the opening sequence—a rolling wave through the torso, initiated from
the chest, flowing down through the hips and into the knees, the body moving
like water finding its level.

Caspar mirrored her. His body was different from hers—taller, broader, the
muscle built through labor rather than dance—but the movement was there. The
coordination was there. The ability to see a gesture and reproduce it, to map
someone else's movement onto his own frame, was the same ability that made him a
good photographer: the capacity to observe precisely and to respond to what he
observed with his whole attention.

"Good," she said. "Now the arms—"

She taught him. On the riverbank, barefoot, the afternoon light golden around
them, the river running past, she taught him the opening sequence of VESPER's
second single—the choreography that Park Jisoo had built in a practice room in
Gangnam, that four women were rehearsing at this moment in Seoul, that the world
would see for the first time in six days, and that was now being learned by a
fifteen-year-old boy on a grassy embankment in western Tokyo while his
girlfriend counted the beats and corrected his arm angles and laughed every time
he got the hip isolation wrong.

They practiced for twenty minutes. The choreography was harder than "Glass
Hour"—more individual, as Caspar had noted at the café, the movement vocabulary
more complex, the rhythms more syncopated. But he was quick. He was
_good_—better than she'd expected, his body intuitive, his timing clean, the
porter's physicality translating surprisingly well into dance: the core
strength, the controlled movement, the ability to use his body as an instrument
of precision.

By the end of the twenty minutes, he had the opening sequence and the first
chorus. Not performance-ready—not the polished, stage-quality execution that
VESPER would deliver—but structurally sound. The movements were in the right
places. The timing was correct. The intention was there.

Isabelle stood in front of him, breathing hard, her feet bare on the grass, her
hair escaping its elastic, her face flushed with exertion and sunlight and
something else—something that looked like happiness, uncomplicated and
unfiltered, the kind of happiness that existed on the other side of every secret
she had been carrying.

"캐스퍼," she said.

"응."

"솔직히 말해도 돼?" _Can I be honest?_

"항상." _Always._

"너한테 이거 가르치는 거, 서울에서 이후로 제일 행복한 시간이야." _Teaching you
this is the happiest I've been since Seoul._

He looked at her. The sun was behind her. The light was in her hair. The river
was behind him. The world was wide and warm and exactly the right temperature.

He stepped forward. He put his hands on her waist. He kissed her—not the gentle,
careful first kiss of Wednesday, but a kiss that tasted like grass and sunlight
and twenty minutes of K-pop choreography and the particular, specific joy of two
people who had shown each other their strangest, most private selves and found
that the strangest, most private parts fit together like pieces of a puzzle they
hadn't known they were solving.

She kissed him back. Her hands on his shoulders, his hands on her waist, the
Tama River running past them, the cicadas singing, the sun beginning its descent
toward the mountains that were, on this clear day, visible at the edge of the
world—a blue-gray line on the horizon, steady and permanent, the kind of thing
you oriented yourself by.

When they pulled apart, she was smiling. He was smiling. Two smiles,
uncontrolled, unfiltered, full.

"잘 자, 남자친구," she said.

"해 아직 안 졌는데." _The sun hasn't set yet._

"상관없어." _I don't care._

"잘 자, 여자친구."

They gathered their things. They walked to the station. They rode the train back
into the city—sitting side by side, her head on his shoulder, his arm around her
waist, two teenagers in a train car full of Saturday commuters, unremarkable,
invisible, ordinary.

Which was, of course, exactly the point.
